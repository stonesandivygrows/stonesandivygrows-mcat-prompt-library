## Passage-Level Diagnosis

### ★ Main Claim

Patient education should not remain a one-way transfer of generalized lifestyle advice. Patients learn and recover more effectively when they participate actively and take some authority in understanding their condition.

### ⚠️ Central Contrast

General practitioners are expected to educate patients, but their advice can remain abstract, prevention-focused, and difficult to apply. The passage contrasts passive receipt of expert advice with active patient participation.

### → Cause/Effect Chain

Doctor authority and generalized advice
→ patient passivity or perceived blame
→ difficulty applying the advice
→ weaker educational value
→ increased patient participation and authority
→ better understanding and recovery

### EX Core Case

Lower back pain serves as the practical case because it is common and creates repeated opportunities for patient education.

## 3-Layer Passage Map

### Paragraph 1 — Non-Formal Education

★ Education can occur outside classrooms through professional services.

Professionals may teach while helping with health, legal, or financial concerns `even though education is not their primary occupation`.

**Function:** Establishes the broader category that makes doctors relevant as educators.

### Paragraph 2 — General Practitioners as Educators

★ General practitioners are a major example of non-formal educators.

Patients should receive:

- an explanation of the health problem
- information about likely prognosis
- advice about preventing recurrence

**Function:** Defines the ideal or official standard for patient education.

### Paragraph 3 — Gap Between Ideal and Practice

⚠️ It is unclear how consistently the ideal is achieved.

Patients may leave consultations without enough useful advice.

★ The stronger solution is not merely more advice; patients should engage in their own education.

**Function:** Introduces the author’s concern and pivots toward participation.

### Paragraph 4 — Lower Back Pain Study

EX Lower back pain provides a case study because it affects many people and creates repeated educational opportunities.

The study occurred `during a multi-year period in the United Kingdom`.

**Function:** Introduces the evidence base and practical setting.

### Paragraph 5 — Doctors’ Working Definition of Education

★ Many practitioners treated patient education as prevention-focused lifestyle guidance.

EX Advice included weight management, posture, working practices, and lifting technique.

→ Doctor logic: changing behavior should reduce recurrence.

**Function:** Shows what the dominant doctor-centered model looked like in practice.

### Paragraph 6 — Critique of the Doctor-Centered Model

⚠️ Generalized lifestyle advice can become conservative, uncritical, and implicitly blaming.

→ Perceived blame can make patients resistant.

→ Abstract, decontextualized advice can be hard to use.

★ Participation `such as reading relevant material or asking questions` produced better outcomes.

**Function:** Explains why one-way instruction fails and why participation matters.

### Paragraph 7 — Authority and Passivity

★ Patients often view doctors as experts with both knowledge and authority.

→ Strong authority can push patients into a passive role.

Patients may struggle to ask questions, challenge information, or redirect the consultation.

⚠️ When doctors help patients take some authority, patients can learn about their condition more effectively and improve recovery.

**Function:** Identifies the underlying mechanism: authority imbalance produces passivity.

## Trap Forecast

### Trap 1 — More Advice vs Better Education

The passage does not merely argue that doctors should talk more. It argues that education improves when patients participate.

### Trap 2 — Lifestyle Guidance

Lifestyle guidance refers to concrete preventive behaviors. The criticism is not that such advice is always false; it is that generalized advice can be abstract, blaming, and difficult to apply.

### Trap 3 — Anti-Doctor Interpretation

The passage does not reject medical expertise. It criticizes an authority imbalance that leaves patients passive.

### Trap 4 — Authority

Doctor authority is not inherently harmful. The problem is exclusive authority that prevents patients from asking questions, challenging information, or participating.

### Trap 5 — Main Idea Scope

Do not reduce the passage to lower back pain. Lower back pain is the case used to support a broader claim about patient education.

## Transferable Rules

- When a passage contrasts expert instruction with participation, track who holds authority and who acts.
- Distinguish **more information** from **better learning**.
- Examples define the case; the conclusion usually sits one level above the example.
- When advice is criticized as abstract or decontextualized, look for an answer involving application, participation, or contextual use.
- For author-purpose questions, ask whether the passage is describing a professional role or criticizing how that role is performed.

Passage 1 (Questions 1-5)
  Their similarity and differences from us allowed animals to provoke some of the first questions and offer answers. The first subject matter for painting was animal. Probably the first paint was animal blood.
Prior to that, it is not unreasonable to suppose that the first metaphor was animal: something like “that man is a bear.”
Jean-Jacques Rousseau, in his Essay on the Origins of Languages, maintained that language itself began with metaphor: “As emotions were the first motives which induced man to speak, his first utterances were tropes (metaphors). Figurative language was the first to be born, proper meanings were the last to be found.” If the first metaphor was animal, it was men who understood animals metaphorically. Within that relation what the two terms — man and animal — shared in common revealed what differentiated them. And vice versa.
In his book on totemism, Claude Lévi-Strauss comments on Rousseau’s reasoning: “It is because man originally felt himself identical to all those like him [among which, as Rousseau explicitly says, we ‘must include animals’] that he came to acquire the capacity to distinguish himself as he distinguishes them — i.e., to use the diversity of species for conceptual support for social differentiations.” In other words, the “with” in Rousseau’s statement “must include animals” is a clue to the context. It is animals as a “with” that we use to contextualize ourselves, and the way we contextualize ourselves is also a way of forming society.

John Berger, “Why Look at Animals?” (1977) [578 words]

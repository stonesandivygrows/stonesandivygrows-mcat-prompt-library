# Post-Cold War Warfare (Michael S. Drake, 2007)
# Passage Name: Post-Cold War Warfare

## Passage:
- **Passage Details:**
- **Passage Content Category:** Political Science
- **Passage Main Idea:**

**Passage 1 (Questions 1 - 7):**
The end of the Cold War in 1989 produced hopes for a world in which democracy would become a global norm and war would become obsolete. The impasse of the Cold War nuclear stalemate had already led many to conclude that war was no longer a real option. Since many of the actors involved in subnuclear conflicts around the world were ideologically and politically aligned and dependent on external support from one of the superpowers, it was assumed that many of these conflicts would also come to an end.

The end of the Cold War did mark the cessation of many existing long-term wars, and seems to have created grounds to end some otherwise unrelated conflicts, such as the bombings that made up the Troubles of Northern Ireland. Retrospectively, however, rather than marking the advent of an era of universal peace, 1989 seems to mark a transition from one typical form of conflict to another. On a global scale, we can chart a profusion of new conflicts from 1990 onwards. The bitter Balkan Wars of 1991–1995 which ended in the traumatic break-up of Yugoslavia, and the sudden bloody Rwandan genocide perpetrated by Hutus against Tutsis and political opponents in 1994 are examples of the new kind of conflict. As these examples show, these new conflicts involved combatants of different levels of power.

Old struggles were also restructured into new forms. In Afghanistan, Soviet withdrawal did not lead to the emergence of a stable coalition formed from the mujahedin resistance, but left a power vacuum filled by tribal warlordism. This was followed by the rule of the fundamentalist Taliban before history repeated itself with the US bombings and the installation of central power in Kabul dependent on North Atlantic Treaty Organisation (NATO) support, which seemed to incite rather than quell resistance.

The three examples of the Balkans, Rwanda and Afghanistan illustrate how the new armed conflicts did not consist of war between states in the form conventionally analyzed by older thinkers. The typical form of modern war had been analyzed by Carl von Clausewitz in the early nineteenth century, using an analogy with the duel to grasp the new form of warfare that swept Europe with forces of Napoleon Bonaparte after the French Revolution. Clausewitz argued that this was evidence for a number of consequences flowing from the modern institution of the nation-state that replaced aristocratic regimes in Europe at this time. First, mass national conscript armies meant that warfare would tend to be decided by battle. Second, warfare became subject to a “trinity” consisting of relations between government, the national people and the military command, so that war became “politics by other means.” Third, this new political constellation was militarily superior to the pre-existing ancien régime, thus introducing disequilibrium into the old strategic balance of powers and producing a militarily competitive environment in which all states had to adopt similar forms or perish. Thus, even at the end of the ancien régime, Clausewitz’s model of war retained the idea of a symmetrical encounter between two equivalent entities.

With few exceptions, outbreaks of violence since 1989 have not taken the form of such symmetrical encounters, but instead by differently powered combatants. Even in the wars in the Balkans, the binary order of the duel was replaced by three and sometimes more parties. When the new forces confront outside intervention from “global” actors like NATO, the US, or the UN, these conflicts can be seen as an asymmetrical encounter between two entirely different practices of warfare, two different systems of violence.

Michael S. Drake, “Sociology and New Wars in the Age of Globalization” (2007) [588 words]

# Vegetarianism (Peter Singer, 1995)
# Passage Name: Vegetarianism

## Passage:
- **Passage Details:**
- **Passage Content Category:**
- **Passage Main Idea:**

**Passage 1 (Questions 1 - 6):**
The view that we should avoid eating meat or fish has ancient philosophical roots. In the Hindu Upanishads (about 1000 BC) the doctrine of reincarnation leads to opposition to eating meat. Buddha taught compassion for all sentient creatures. Buddhist monks were not to kill animals, nor to eat meat, unless they knew that the animal had not been killed for their sake. Jains hold to ahimsa, or non-violence toward any living creature, and accordingly do not eat meat.

In the Western tradition, Genesis suggests that the first diet of human beings was vegetarian, and permission to eat meat was given only after the Flood. After that, vegetarianism gains little support from either the Jewish or Christian scriptures, or from Islam. Philosophical vegetarianism was stronger in ancient Greece and Rome. Pythagoreans abstained from eating animals partly because of their belief that humans and animals share a common soul, and partly because they appear to have considered the diet a healthier one. Plato shared both these views to some extent. Plutarch’s essay “On Eating Flesh,” written in the late first or early second century of the Christian era, is a detailed argument for “vegetarianism on grounds of justice and humane treatment of animals.”

Interest in vegetarianism revived in the nineteenth century, on grounds of health and humanity towards animals. Notable vegetarian thinkers included the poet Percy Bysshe Shelley, Henry Salt (who wrote a pioneering volume entitled Animals’ Rights), and George Bernard Shaw, who said that he put into his plays the ideas that he learned from Salt. In Germany, Arthur Schopenhauer urged that ethically we should become vegetarian, “were it not for the fact that the human race cannot exist without animal food in the north!”

Since the 1970s vegetarianism has gained strength from three major lines of argument: health, ecology, and concern for animals. The first of these grounds rests on a scientific, rather than philosophical, claim and will not be discussed further here. The greatest philosophical arguments for vegetarianism, then, argue for the greatest good for the greatest number. In ecological terms this prohibition on eating meat arises from the well-documented inefficiency of much animal-raising. This applies especially to intensive farming, in which grain is grown on good agricultural land and fed to animals confined indoors, or in the case of cattle, in crowded feed-lots. Much of the nutritional value of the grain is lost in the process, and this form of animal production is also energy-intensive. Hence concern for world hunger, for the land, and for energy conservation provide an ethical basis for a vegetarian diet. The philosophical argument for vegetarianism in ecological terms is that it allows the fewest resources (i.e. food) to be distributed to the greatest number.

Arguments for a reassessment of the moral status of animals have also given support to vegetarianism. If animals have rights, or are entitled to have their interests given equal consideration with the similar interests of human beings, it is easy to see that there are difficulties in claiming that we are entitled to eat non-human animals (but not, presumably, human beings, even if through some accident they are at a similar mental level to the animals we do eat). These ethical arguments for vegetarianism may be based on the view that we violate the rights of animals when we kill them for our food, or on the more utilitarian grounds that, in raising them for our food, we cause them more suffering than we gain by eating their flesh. If the good of animals is allowed to factor into the greatest good, then we have to avoid eating them.

Peter Singer “Vegetarianism” (1995) [599 words]

Education sometimes occurs in “non-formal” educational settings where people seek help from occupational groups such as doctors, lawyers, accountants, and bank managers. Although these professions are not primarily concerned with education, they do make an educational contribution as a part of the broader services they provide, relating to health, legal or financial concerns. This educational contribution may involve giving people information they require and providing opportunities for them to ask questions and develop an understanding.

In the medical profession, one particular group that does a great deal of education is “general practitioners.” The educational processes which occur when doctors and patients meet in the consultation room is part of the broader remit of “maintaining health” or “managing disease.” Professional guidance for general practitioners concerning patient education states that all patients should be entitled to an explanation of their health problem, information on its likely prognosis and advice on how to prevent the problem reoccurring in the future.

The extent to which this guidance is adhered to in practice, however, is unclear, although it has been suggested that patients often leave the consultation room short of the advice they require. More attention should be paid, then, to how doctors educate their patients. Essentially, patients should be encouraged to engage in their own education in order to benefit from it.

A recent study focused on low back pain (LBP), a condition which is commonly presented in general practice, offers many possibilities for patient education, and which almost everyone suffers from at some point in their lives. The study was carried out between [year omitted] and 1995 and involved 12 general practices in Nottinghamshire, UK.

For the majority of general practitioners in the study, patient education for lower back pain—like most things they treat—meant giving advice on how to prevent the problem from reoccurring. When asked about patient education, most referred to the individual “lifestyle” guidance they gave patients. One of the participating general practitioners, a Dr. Thomas, stated, “The most important thing of all I like to get over to people is...the preventative side of it, and, so they've got the pain and the problem at that time, I want to get over to them that really...the most important thing is for them to look after it and maybe lose weight and adopt a better posture, better working practices in the way they lift.” In offering an individualistic lifestyle approach to prevention, general practitioners were implicitly adopting a conservative, uncritical form of patient education for lower back pain. Viewing prevention in this way meant that the patient was implicitly blamed for lower back pain, making them hostile to the advice. Many patients, additionally, found it difficult to act on the prevention advice given to them by their doctors. This advice was found to be abstract and so that patients were unable to apply it to their own real-life situations. Having patients participate in their education—having them, for instance, ask questions—would produce better outcomes and therefore recover from their lower back pain.

Most patients viewed general practitioners as experts who possessed both the specialist knowledge and authority to tell them about their lower back pain and how to manage it. This meant that for virtually all patients, the words of general practitioners carried great power and significance. Within the consultation, most patients played a passive role: it was not common to ask questions or challenge received information, or alter the flow of events. Yet, the passage suggests that if the doctors could get patients to participate in their own education, they could recover from their lower back pain.

Alan Skelton, “The Hidden Curriculum of Patient Education For Lower Back Pain in General Practice” (1998) [605 words]

**Passage 1 (Questions 1 - 7):**
The view that we should avoid eating meat or fish has ancient philosophical roots. In the Hindu Upanishads (about 1000 BC)
the doctrine of reincarnation leads to opposition to eating meat. Buddha taught compassion for all sentient creatures.
Buddhist monks were not to kill animals, nor to eat meat, unless they knew that the animal had not been killed for their sake.
Jains hold to ahimsa, or non-violence toward any living creature, and accordingly do not eat meat.

In the Western tradition, Genesis suggests that the first diet of human beings was vegetarian, and permission to eat meat was given
only after the Flood. After that, vegetarianism gains little support from either the Jewish or Christian scriptures, or from Islam.
Philosophical vegetarianism was stronger in ancient Greece and Rome. Pythagoreans abstained from eating animals partly because of
their belief that humans and animals share a common soul, and partly because they appear to have considered the diet a healthier one.
Plato shared both these views to some extent. Plutarch’s essay “On Eating Flesh,” written in the late first or early second century of
the Christian era, is a detailed argument for “vegetarianism on grounds of justice and humane treatment of animals.”

Interest in vegetarianism revived in the nineteenth century, on grounds of health and humanity towards animals. Notable vegetarian
thinkers included the poet Percy Bysshe Shelley, Henry Salt (who wrote a pioneering volume entitled Animals’ Rights), and George Bernard
Shaw, who said that he put into his plays the ideas that he learned from Salt. In Germany, Arthur Schopenhauer urged that ethically we
should become vegetarian, “were it not for the fact that the human race cannot exist without animal food in the north!”

Since the 1970s vegetarianism has gained strength from three major lines of argument: health, ecology, and concern for animals. The
first of these grounds rests on a scientific, rather than philosophical, claim and will not be discussed further here. The greatest
philosophical arguments for vegetarianism, then, argue for the greatest good for the greatest number. In ecological terms this prohibition
on eating meat arises from the well-documented inefficiency of much animal-raising. This applies especially to intensive farming, in which
grain is grown on good agricultural land and fed to animals confined indoors, or in the case of cattle, in crowded feed-lots. Much of the
nutritional value of the grain is lost in the process, and this form of animal production is also energy-intensive. Hence concern for world
hunger, for the land, and for energy conservation provide an ethical basis for a vegetarian diet. The philosophical argument for vegetarianism
in ecological terms is that it allows the fewest resources (i.e. food) to be distributed to the greatest number.

Arguments for a reassessment of the moral status of animals have also given support to vegetarianism. If animals have rights, or are entitled
to have their interests given equal consideration with the similar interests of human beings, it is easy to see that there are difficulties in
claiming that we are entitled to eat non-human animals (but not, presumably, human beings, even if through some accident they are at a similar
mental level to the animals we do eat). These ethical arguments for vegetarianism may be based on the view that we violate the rights of animals
when we kill them for our food, or on the more utilitarian grounds that, in raising them for our food, we cause them more suffering than we gain
by eating their flesh. If the good of animals is allowed to factor into the greatest good, then we have to avoid eating them.

Peter Singer “Vegetarianism” (1995) [599 words]

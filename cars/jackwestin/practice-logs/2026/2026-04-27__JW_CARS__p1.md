Passage 1 (Question 1-6)
  Immortalized in Bartlett’s book of quotations for his inscrutable declamation, “I think therefore I am,” René
Descartes was history’s most persuasive partisan of the mind-body split, a bedrock notion of modern science.
Mental events, the savant declared, occur in a separate domain from those of the flesh. Consciousness has no
effect on the body. As a result, medical science came to be dominated by a materialism so iron-clad that one
19th century theorist felt emboldened to quip that the mind’s influence upon the mechanism of the body was like
“the steam-whistle which accompanies the work of a locomotive engine but cannot influence its machinery.”
The problem with this is obvious to anyone who ever had an unseemly thought about their junior-high English
teacher and then blushed: “The soul’s passions,” said Aristotle, who had the mind right all along, “seem to be
not bodies but involve bodies.”

Candace Pert’s work in neuropharmacology in the 1970s offered one of the first scientific proofs that Descartes’s
mind-body split theory was incorrect.

Pert discovered that the receptors for neuropeptides (chemical messengers in the body) exist not only in the brain
but throughout the body.

The discovery suggested that emotions and mental states can influence physical states.

This research helped overturn the view that consciousness has no effect on the body.

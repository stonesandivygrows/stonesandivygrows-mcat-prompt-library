# UWorld CARS Strategy Update — 2026-06-05

Source session: `UWorld_CARs_Test 31_06052026_IDNoID_17Q.pdf`

## Executive decision

Today's strategy is not a brand-new system. It is an evolution of the existing CARS plan:

- Keep the interruption-skipping strategy.
- Keep the author / passage / paragraph distinction in question stems.
- Keep answer-choice triangulation for main idea orientation.
- Add a controlled first-paragraph / last-paragraph emphasis strategy.
- Add a limited location-map skim for body paragraphs.
- Do not let the skim become passive scanning or total non-reading.

The strategy is valid as a training experiment, but it needs guardrails. The danger is not skimming itself. The danger is skimming so aggressively that the passage becomes a list of locations without an argument.

## Current reading algorithm

### Step 1 — Before reading

1. Read the title.
2. Make a very light prediction only if it is obvious.
3. Do not preview every question.
4. If the first question is clearly main-idea / author-purpose style, use answer choices only to triangulate likely passage concerns. Do not fully read all stems.

### Step 2 — First paragraph: detailed read

Read paragraph 1 more carefully than the body paragraphs.

Goal:

- Identify the subject.
- Identify the tension.
- Identify the author's framing.
- Decide whether paragraph 1 is thesis, setup, historical background, or hook.

Do not assume paragraph 1 always contains the thesis. Sometimes it only creates the problem that the final paragraph resolves.

### Step 3 — Body paragraphs: location-map read, not deep read

For each body paragraph, the job is to produce a label, not a full explanation.

Minimum required body-paragraph intake:

- First sentence or opening clause.
- Any contrast marker: however, nevertheless, but, yet, rather, although.
- Any named person / quoted phrase / italicized term / unusual vocabulary.
- Final sentence or final argumentative turn.

Optional only if needed:

- One middle sentence if the paragraph is evidence-heavy or has a sharp transition.

Do not read "barely anything" as the default. That is too extreme. The safer rule is:

> Read enough to know what job the paragraph is doing.

If the paragraph label is blank or vague, the skim failed.

Good labels:

- origin story
- example of popularity
- author objection
- theft evidence
- contrast with prior view
- definition of term
- evidence for author's conclusion

Bad labels:

- stuff about art
- details
- names
- confusing paragraph

### Step 4 — Last paragraph: detailed read

Read the last paragraph carefully because it often performs one of these jobs:

- resolves the central tension
- restates the thesis
- adds irony
- shifts from evidence to judgment
- clarifies why the passage exists

If the passage felt confusing, the last paragraph gets priority over rereading the middle.

### Step 5 — After reading: 5-second passage map

Mentally compress the passage into this format:

> Topic → tension → author angle → why it matters

Example structure:

> Work / person / idea → not what people assume → evidence across paragraphs → final author judgment

No written map unless the passage is especially dense and the map can be made in 5–10 seconds.

## Highlighting rules

Highlighting is allowed, but only as a visual anchor system.

Highlight:

- Names.
- Quoted phrases.
- Italicized terms.
- Strange vocabulary that may become a meaning-in-context question.
- Major contrast or thesis sentence.
- One short anchor per paragraph if needed.

Do not highlight full evidence chains. Do not highlight because a sentence feels important but you cannot explain why.

For vocabulary / quote questions:

- If the word is italicized, quoted, or unusual during passage reading, highlight it.
- Do not expect yourself to memorize where it is.
- If asked about the word later, use the highlight or paragraph label to relocate it.
- If you cannot find it quickly, use answer choices to infer which paragraph context would require that meaning.

## Question-phase rule

Before answering a question, identify the task:

1. Retrieval / paragraph-cited / word-in-context
2. Function
3. Main idea / primary purpose
4. Inference / implication
5. Author attitude
6. Application / new scenario
7. Strengthen / weaken / assumption

### Retrieval / word-in-context

Go back to the passage.

Use a narrow search:

- word itself
- name nearby
- quote nearby
- paragraph label
- local sentence before and after

### Function questions

Do not ask only, "What does this sentence say?"
Ask:

> Why did the author put this here?

Most common answers:

- introduce tension
- provide example
- support prior claim
- contrast a view
- clarify origin
- illustrate consequence

### Main idea / primary purpose

Do not overuse details.
Use:

- first paragraph framing
- last paragraph judgment
- repeated nouns / verbs in answer choices
- author concern, not topic alone

### Inference / implication

Stay one step away from the text.
If the answer needs two steps, outside knowledge, or a dramatic generalization, it is probably wrong.

### Application / new scenario

Match the mechanism, not the surface topic.
Ask:

> What relationship from the passage is being tested?

## What today's practice suggests

### Working

- Untimed first use of the strategy was appropriate because the goal was strategy acquisition, not speed validation.
- First and last paragraph emphasis helped reduce the need to perfectly understand every body paragraph.
- Highlighting names / quotes / unusual vocabulary is useful because CARS often asks targeted location questions.
- Paragraph association highlighting can help prevent paragraphs from blending together, especially with ADHD fatigue.
- UWorld being harder can be useful for overpreparation, but only if review stays strategy-focused rather than confidence-focused.

### Needs modification

- The body-paragraph skim cannot become "read almost nothing." It must still capture paragraph function.
- Heavy highlighting can become a time sink. Limit to anchors, not comprehension decoration.
- Retrieval / definition questions should be recognized during the question phase, not through a full pre-scan of every question.
- Do not convert every word-in-context question into a long search mission. If the word is not found quickly, infer from local context and answer choices.

### Stop doing

- Do not time a brand-new strategy and then judge it as a failure.
- Do not treat long first-passage timing as proof that the strategy is bad.
- Do not read so lightly that the author disappears.
- Do not highlight every interesting sentence.
- Do not force yourself to remember where every word appeared.

## Final working strategy

Use this version next:

1. Title.
2. First paragraph detailed read.
3. Body paragraphs: first sentence + contrast words + names/quotes/terms + final sentence.
4. Last paragraph detailed read.
5. 5-second map: topic → tension → author angle → why it matters.
6. Questions: identify question type first.
7. Retrieval/word questions: go back narrowly.
8. Inference/application questions: answer from the passage map and mechanism.
9. If stuck after two reads of the question, mark and move.

## Non-negotiable correction

The goal is not to skim because skimming is faster.

The goal is to read with different levels of intensity:

- high intensity for framing and conclusion
- medium intensity for argumentative turns
- low intensity for examples and descriptive filler

That is the actual strategy.

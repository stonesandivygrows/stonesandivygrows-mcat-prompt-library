# 2026-05-16 Jack Westin CARS Autopsy — 12 Questions

## Purpose

This review captures durable reasoning patterns from the May 16 Jack Westin CARS set. It records the transferable causes of missed questions and answer changes rather than reproducing the full source passages.

## Passage 1 — Moral restraint and uncertainty

### Core argument

Because humans cannot reliably predict the future, they should practice moral restraint and prioritize avoiding harm.

### What worked

- The central theme of restraint was identified.
- The weakening question about the Hippocratic Oath was solved by finding a scenario in which minimizing harm could itself create harm.
- The Geneva Protocol example was correctly understood as support for precaution under uncertainty.
- The scientists' opposing viewpoint was correctly identified as confidence in their ability to predict future technological effects.

### Missed pattern 1: softening a strong claim

The author says reliable prediction is blocked not merely by poor skill but by genuine complexity, indeterminism, ignorance, and hubris.

The incorrect interpretation was:

> Humans are capable of prediction but unskilled.

The passage's safer summary was:

> Humans are generally wrong when they act as though uncertain forecasts are dependable.

### Durable rule

When an author uses language such as "in principle," "cannot," "genuine complexity," or "indeterminism," do not soften the claim into a temporary skill deficit.

### Missed pattern 2: overgeneralizing the criticism

The passage criticizes human forecasting, not human intelligence as a whole.

The answer "human intelligence is not trustworthy" expanded a narrow criticism into a global statement.

### Durable rule

Preserve the object of criticism. A weakness in one cognitive task does not establish a weakness in all human thought.

### Theme precision

The passage does not say all change is bad. It says projects should be tempered because unanticipated consequences can cause harm.

Therefore:

- restraint is not total passivity;
- "do no harm" is not equivalent to "never act";
- caution is preferred when action risks irreversible harm.

## Passage 2 — Orientalism, study, and aggression

### Core argument

Western powers used both intellectual study and direct violence as connected forms of aggression against Islamic societies. Empires were prepared and administered through study rather than improvisation alone.

### What worked

- Rational analysis and violence were correctly recognized as complementary rather than contradictory within the author's account.
- The Napoleon example was correctly interpreted as depending on the unusual relevance of scientists to a military expedition.
- Context was used correctly to infer that "antiquarian" contrasted with a more modern, technical, and rational method.
- The Western self-image was correctly distinguished from the author's external criticism.

### Missed pattern: selecting a pleasant philosophy instead of the actual mechanism

The incorrect philosophy was:

> What you understand will be unable to hurt you.

The passage's mechanism was:

> Fear the Orient → study and define it → use that knowledge to support aggression and control.

The better answer therefore reproduced both stages:

> Learn about what you fear, and then attack it.

### Durable rule

For philosophy, theme, and analogy questions, do not choose the option that merely sounds wise or coherent. Choose the option that reproduces the author's causal sequence.

## Answer-change pattern

Several answers were changed during the set. Some changes corrected an initial response; others replaced a correct answer with an incorrect one.

### Answer-change checkpoint

Before changing, name the exact reason:

- I missed a negation or contrast.
- I misread who held the viewpoint.
- My answer is broader than the passage.
- The option changes a possibility into a certainty.
- Another option matches the mechanism more directly.

Do not change because the selected answer feels too easy, too obvious, or emotionally uncomfortable.

## Repeated scope problems

### Capability versus reliability

"Humans cannot reliably forecast complex futures" is not the same as "humans have no intelligence" or "humans can predict but need more practice."

### Some versus all

Evidence that mental treatment affects some diseases does not require that it affect every disease.

### Effect versus intention

A passage may show that a system causes distortion without proving that every participant intentionally designed that distortion.

### Viewpoint tracking

Keep separate:

- what the author believes;
- what scientists or historical actors believe;
- what those actors believe about themselves;
- what the author believes their conduct actually accomplishes.

## Reading-process findings

The syntax-skipping method remained useful, but paragraph-purpose tracking still mattered. A usable map for the first passage was:

- P1: humans forecast poorly;
- P2: complexity and hubris explain why;
- P3: restraint is the antidote;
- P4: scientists resist restraint;
- P5: ambition must be tempered;
- P6: do no harm.

A usable map for the second passage was:

- P1: Orientalist division and fear;
- P2: study and violence work together;
- P3–4: France and Napoleon as examples;
- P5: study enables empire, without claiming all scholarship directly causes violence.

## Main diagnosis

The misses did not indicate a broad comprehension failure. They arose from:

- softening claims that the author stated strongly;
- broadening narrow criticism into global judgment;
- choosing a morally appealing interpretation over the author's actual mechanism;
- changing answers without stronger textual evidence.

## Next-practice focus

1. Circle or mentally mark strength words: can, cannot, generally, always, some, only, in principle.
2. Restate the author's mechanism in an arrow chain before philosophy or analogy questions.
3. Track whose viewpoint each answer describes.
4. Use the answer-change checkpoint before replacing an initial choice.

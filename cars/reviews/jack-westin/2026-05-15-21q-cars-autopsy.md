# 2026-05-15 Jack Westin CARS Autopsy — 21 Questions

## Purpose

This review captures durable reasoning patterns from the May 15 Jack Westin CARS set. It is not a replacement for the original passage export. It records what repeatedly helped, what caused errors, and what should transfer to later practice.

## Reading strategy tested

The main experiment was a core-first reading method:

- Read the title and opening material normally.
- Preserve transition and contrast words.
- Temporarily pass over parentheses, em-dash insertions, long name lists, quotations, and descriptive digressions when they interrupt the sentence skeleton.
- Return to skipped material only when it changes the claim or a question requires it.
- Track each paragraph's purpose rather than every detail.

This method improved speed and reduced the recurring "What did I just read?" loop. The main limitation was deciding which details were safely deferrable and which altered the author's logic.

## Passage 1 — Modernist painting

### Core argument

Modernist painting became flatter and less sculptural in order to distinguish painting from sculpture.

### What worked

- The passage's main idea was identified correctly.
- Technique was distinguished from subject matter.
- Color was correctly interpreted as producing a flattening effect rather than as something absent from sculpture.
- The analogy question was solved by matching the mechanism: a recognizable object placed in recognizable three-dimensional space conflicts with the modernist goal.

### Error pattern

A function question about Manet and the Impressionists was answered as though it asked why painting changed over time. The better question was:

> What larger claim does this example support?

The example established a continuing anti-sculptural trend. It did not prove that painting had to change.

### Durable rule

For "the author mentions X in order to" questions, identify the nearby claim that X supports. Do not merely summarize X.

## Passage 2 — William Blake

### Core argument

Blake was not an untaught or abnormal wild genius. He was an honest, technically skilled poet whose development and composition were more traditional and deliberate than commonly assumed.

### What worked

- Long lists of writers and names were safely passed over on the first read.
- The direct relationship between eighteenth-century poetry and prose was recognized.
- Paragraph-level inference questions were answered by using the paragraph's actual language rather than importing unrelated concepts.
- "Obfuscating reality" was correctly treated as the opposite of the honesty the author admired.

### Precision correction

Do not reduce the author's claim to "Blake was a simple writer." The passage's point was that Blake's greatness came from honesty, technical accomplishment, focus, and normal poetic labor rather than naïveté or spontaneous inspiration.

### Durable rule

When the passage rejects labels such as wild, naïve, abnormal, or untaught, avoid answer choices that merely restate those rejected labels in softer form.

## Passage 3 — Postmodernism and architecture

### Core problem

The broad setup about postmodernism and capitalism was mistaken for the passage's main payoff about postmodern architecture and popular culture.

### Error patterns

- Setup was chosen over thesis.
- Postmodernism as a broad category was treated as interchangeable with postmodern architecture.
- Popular culture was confused with local culture or local context.
- A weakening question was misread because the proposed evidence was interpreted through a different claim than the author's actual claim.
- "Based mainly on" was interpreted as asking where the claim originated rather than what evidence supported it.

### Durable rules

- Prefer the passage's final argumentative destination over its broad opening context.
- Distinguish related categories explicitly when the author narrows the topic.
- Translate "based mainly on" as "What evidence is doing the most work?"
- In weakening questions, state the exact claim first, then ask which option makes that claim less likely.

## Passage 4 — Human and animal language

### Core argument

Animal communication should not be treated as a smaller or inferior version of human language. The author presents the two as fundamentally different systems.

### Error pattern

"Different" was upgraded to "more complex." That added a ranking the author did not necessarily make.

### Durable rule

When the author says two things are different in kind, do not convert the relationship into a degree comparison such as better, worse, simpler, or more complex unless the passage explicitly ranks them.

## Repeated question-type lessons

### Function

Ask:

> Why did the author include this?

> What larger claim does it support?

### Analogy

Reduce the passage relationship to a mechanism. Match the structure, not the topic.

### Inference

Take one supported step. Avoid broadening the answer beyond the paragraph or claim cited in the stem.

### Main idea

Separate setup, examples, and payoff. The topic of the first paragraph is not automatically the thesis.

### Evidence questions

"Based on" asks for support, not origin.

## Reading refinements from this set

- Preserve words such as however, but, rather, not, only, and although.
- Defer names, dates, and examples unless they establish a contrast or sequence required by the argument.
- After each paragraph, retain a one-phrase role label.
- When rereading, use the smallest sufficient window: target sentence, one before, one after, then paragraph claim.
- Do not reopen a full chunk without knowing what information is being sought.

## Main diagnosis

Comprehension was usually adequate. Most preventable errors came from:

- confusing content with function;
- selecting setup instead of thesis;
- adding unsupported rankings or intentions;
- broadening a narrow claim;
- rereading without a target.

## Next-practice focus

1. Label each paragraph by function, not detail.
2. Preserve logical hinges while passing over removable syntax.
3. Before choosing an answer, compare its scope with the author's exact scope.
4. Change an answer only for a specific textual or logical reason.

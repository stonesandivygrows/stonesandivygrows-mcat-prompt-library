## Strategy used

1. Zoom out enough to reduce visual overwhelm while keeping the text readable.
2. Read the title first.
3. Briefly scan question stems only for retrieval landmarks such as names, quotations, or italicized terms.
4. Note the number of questions without solving or reading answer choices in advance.
5. Read the passage fully rather than skim.
6. Read judgmentally, like an editor asking the author to justify each move.
7. Use exaggerated reactions or facial expressions to maintain active engagement.
8. When necessary, read the question stem together with an answer choice aloud so the full claim can be evaluated grammatically.
9. Use paragraph check-ins sparingly; do not let them replace completing the passage.

## Question review

### Question 1 — Roman numerals / author perspective

**What happened:** The choice was narrowed to A versus C. Statement I was secure. Statement III was inferred from the author's rebuttal of the claim that Sun Bin lacked philosophical depth.

**Why the answer worked:** The author explicitly defended Sun Bin against the criticism that his thinking lacked depth and later described the true depth, value, broad scope, and application of his ideas.

**Fix rule:** When an author rejects a criticism, test whether the author is endorsing the meaningful opposite of that criticism.

**Timing note:** Once I and III were supported, remaining on the question for 2:39 was unnecessary.

### Question 2 — Analogy / mechanism transfer

**What happened:** The final comparison was between the struggling employee and the student who spent all her money moving.

**Why D was better:** Playing both general and soldier requires the same person to create the inescapable situation and then confront it. The employee did not create the quota or threat of dismissal. The student deliberately created her own point of no return.

**Fix rule:** For analogy questions, transfer the complete mechanism, not merely one shared feature.

### Question 3 — Extent of evidence

**What happened:** The horse-race claim was selected because the passage gave both the example and the explanation linking it to military strategy.

**Why the answer worked:** Other choices were stated or suggested, but not supported with comparable evidence.

**Fix rule:** “Most supported by evidence” asks which claim has proof and explanation, not merely which statement appears in the passage.

### Question 4 — EXCEPT / retrieval

**What happened:** “Acquiring wealth” initially looked unsupported, but the EXCEPT direction was checked and the relevant discussion of commerce, politics, and how to live was retrieved.

**Why B was better:** The passage supports life guidance, commerce/wealth, and philosophical exploration, but not gaining scholarly recognition.

**Fix rule:** For EXCEPT questions, reverse the task first, then retrieve support for each surviving option.

### Question 5 — Strengthen / defended claim

**What happened:** The relevant claim was identified as the author's defense of Sun Bin's intellectual similarity and comparable value to Sun Tzu.

**Why the answer worked:** Evidence that Sun Bin wrote material attributed to Sun Tzu would directly strengthen the claim that their ideas are highly similar.

**Fix rule:** Before evaluating new evidence, name the exact claim the author is defending.

### Question 6 — Translation example

**What happened:** Meaning was distinguished from aesthetic presentation and character.

**Why the answer worked:** The translation could preserve meaning while losing the acrostic structure and literary effect.

**Fix rule:** Separate informational content from literary form when the passage treats them as different dimensions.

### Question 7 — Function of example

**What happened:** The purpose was identified using the explicit signal “thus,” which connected the example to the perennial difficulty of translation.

**Fix rule:** For function questions, look for the author's own conclusion immediately before or after the example.

### Question 9 — Tone / word meaning

**What happened:** Reading the relevant sentence aloud kept the tone fresh and prevented overinterpretation.

**Fix rule:** For tone words, choose the closest passage-supported emotional meaning, not a stronger evaluative judgment.

### Question 10 — Author agreement

**What happened:** The final choice was between C and D. C matched a local discussion of lost style, but it overstated that local point into the broad claim that style matters more than information.

**Why D was better:** The passage's general claim was that literal meaning and literary character can conflict in translation. The author regretted the loss of the acrostic but did not argue that style always outranks meaning.

## New CARS rule — Author-agreement hierarchy

For author-agreement questions, do not validate an answer using only one paragraph or quotation.

Check the answer in this order:

1. **Main idea or thesis**
2. **Major supporting arguments**
3. **Individual paragraph**
4. **Individual quotation or example**

Before selecting an answer, ask:

- Is it consistent with the entire passage?
- Does it overstate a local claim?
- Does it narrow the author’s position too much?
- Does it mistake an example for the author’s general position?

**Compressed rule:** A local quote may support an answer, but the main idea must authorize it.

## Overall conclusion

The strategy produced perfect accuracy on this set. The main repair is pacing: preserve the active editor-style reading, but leave questions sooner once the best-supported answer has been identified. Continue shifting from quote matching to argument matching.

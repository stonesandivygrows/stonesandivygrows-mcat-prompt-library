## Purpose

This file preserves observations and hypotheses from the first deliberate trial of the revised CARS reading strategy. It is separate from the canonical strategy because a single untimed session cannot establish that every element improves performance.

## Strategy hypothesis

Reading intensity should be distributed unevenly:

- detailed reading for the first paragraph
- controlled location-mapping for body paragraphs
- detailed reading for the final paragraph
- targeted highlighting for names, quotations, italicized terms, and unusual vocabulary

The proposed benefit is reduced cognitive overload without losing the passage's argumentative structure.

## What was actually tested

- The strategy was initially attempted while timing, which made the unfamiliar process harder to execute.
- The later practice was intentionally untimed so the reading procedure could be learned before evaluating speed.
- Reported passage completion times were approximate:
  - about 15 minutes for the first passage
  - about 10–12 minutes for a later passage
  - about 12–15 minutes for another passage
- Body paragraphs were approached as location maps rather than material requiring full mastery on the first read.
- Highlighting was used to associate each paragraph with an argument, example, name, quotation, or retrievable term.

## Claims that remain hypotheses

### UWorld difficulty

The belief that UWorld CARS is harder than AAMC CARS is a training assumption, not a conclusion established by this session. Difficulty may differ by passage style, question construction, scoring expectations, and individual strengths.

Operational use:

- UWorld may be used as harder-feeling practice.
- AAMC performance remains the better standard for test-readiness.
- A strategy should not be retained merely because it works on UWorld; it must transfer to AAMC material.

### First-and-last paragraph emphasis

Possible benefit:

- stronger framing and conclusion recognition
- less overinvestment in examples
- easier main-idea formation

Possible failure mode:

- the central thesis or author reversal may occur in a middle paragraph
- body paragraphs may contain distinctions required for inference and application questions

Therefore, the rule is emphasis, not exclusive comprehension.

### Aggressive body-paragraph skimming

The original impulse to read only an opening fragment, a random middle sentence, and the end is too unstable to become the default.

Reason:

- a random middle sentence may not represent the paragraph's function
- the key contrast may occur elsewhere
- location memory without argumentative meaning may not support inference questions

Revised testable version:

- opening claim
- contrast markers
- named people / quotations / defined terms
- final argumentative turn
- one additional sentence only when the paragraph's role remains unclear

### Heavy highlighting

Possible benefit:

- prevents names, quotations, and unusual terms from becoming expensive searches later
- separates paragraphs visually
- supports retrieval under attention fatigue

Possible failure mode:

- highlighting becomes a substitute for deciding paragraph function
- too many marks destroy visual hierarchy
- highlighting time cancels the speed gained by reduced reading intensity

The useful unit is one anchor, not one fully highlighted paragraph.

### Question preview and answer-choice triangulation

Answer choices can help identify repeated subjects, relationships, or likely thesis language. However, this should not expand into reading every question before the passage.

Current boundary:

- do not preview the full question set
- use answer choices for main-idea orientation only when this does not scatter attention
- identify explicit paragraph, quotation, italicized-word, or definition cues when answering the question
- use answer choices as a secondary location or meaning cue, not as a replacement for passage evidence

## Retrieval and vocabulary protocol

When a question identifies a paragraph, quotation, italicized term, or specific word:

1. Treat it as a narrow retrieval task.
2. Search the relevant paragraph or visual highlight.
3. Read the target sentence plus one sentence before and after when needed.
4. Predict the meaning or function before comparing choices.
5. Use answer choices to resolve ambiguity, not to invent context.

If the term cannot be found quickly:

- use paragraph labels and nearby names as search anchors
- infer what type of context would support each answer
- avoid repeatedly scanning the entire passage

## Evidence needed before promotion to permanent strategy

Track across multiple UWorld and AAMC passage sets:

- accuracy by question type
- total passage time
- reading time versus question time
- number of passage returns
- number of questions missed because a body-paragraph distinction was skimmed
- number of retrieval questions sped up by highlighting
- number of highlights per passage
- whether the main idea can be stated before beginning the questions

## Decision rules

Keep the strategy element when:

- accuracy is maintained or improves
- passage time decreases without increasing rereads
- main-idea and inference performance remain stable
- highlighting reliably shortens targeted retrieval

Modify the strategy element when:

- paragraph labels are vague
- middle-paragraph reversals are repeatedly missed
- answer choices are used to reconstruct a passage that was not understood
- highlighting exceeds a small number of anchors per paragraph

Reject the strategy element when:

- it improves speed but consistently lowers accuracy
- it works on UWorld but fails on AAMC material
- it increases uncertainty and rereading
- it causes the author’s position to disappear behind paragraph locations

## Next controlled trial

For the next passage set:

1. Use the revised first/body/last reading intensity.
2. Limit highlighting to names, quoted or italicized terms, major contrasts, and one paragraph anchor when necessary.
3. Record approximate reading-end time.
4. Mark whether each miss came from:
   - missing passage structure
   - missing a body-paragraph detail
   - misreading the question type
   - answer-choice logic
   - slow retrieval
5. Compare the result with at least one AAMC passage set before making the strategy permanent.

## Current conclusion

The session supports continued testing, not full confirmation.

The strongest part of the strategy is selective reading intensity. The weakest and most dangerous version is near-total non-reading of body paragraphs. The strategy should therefore remain:

> detailed framing, functional body mapping, detailed conclusion, and narrow retrieval.

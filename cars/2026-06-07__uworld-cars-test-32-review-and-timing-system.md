## Executive update

This review does not replace the June 5 strategy. It sharpens it.

The central finding is:

> The main difficulty is not understanding CARS topics. It is preserving sentence structure, question scope, and answer-choice relationships under time pressure.

The strongest current methods are:

- paragraph-function mapping
- selective interruption skipping
- mechanism matching for application questions
- targeted verbalization of logical hinges
- one structured reset when a passage model fails
- a timer-digit question map that minimizes whiteboard use

The major risks are:

- skimming past actor-action-target relationships
- rejecting valid paraphrases because they do not repeat the passage wording
- treating "I do not remember seeing it" as "the passage did not say it"
- using the main idea when the question asks for one local feature
- rescuing an unsupported answer after losing the decisive proof sentence

## Core diagnostic

### What is not the main problem

- inability to understand humanities passages
- lack of content knowledge about film, archaeology, religion, or art
- inability to identify broad passage topics

### What is the main problem

The passage meaning is often available, but precision is lost during translation between:

1. the passage wording
2. the question task
3. the answer-choice paraphrase

This creates errors such as:

- `dialogue` not being recognized as `conversation between characters`
- `glamorous` not being recognized as `attractive or alluring`
- `fate is inexorable` not being recognized as `unchanging or inescapable destiny`
- a pronoun being assigned to the nearest noun instead of the continuing topic
- a modifier being attached to the wrong category or subject

## Reading algorithm: updated version

### 1. First paragraph

Read in detail enough to identify:

- subject
- tension
- author framing
- whether the paragraph is thesis, setup, background, or hook

### 2. Body paragraphs

Use a location-map read rather than either deep reading or empty scanning.

Minimum intake:

- opening claim
- contrast words
- actor-action-target relationships
- named people, quoted terms, or unusual vocabulary
- final argumentative turn

After each paragraph, assign a short functional label.

Examples:

- origin of style
- social ritual evidence
- scroll contents
- language paragraph
- author objection
- final significance

A good label should tell where to return later.

### 3. Last paragraph

Read in detail. It often:

- resolves the tension
- supplies the author judgment
- states why the passage matters
- clarifies the true main idea

### 4. Five-second passage map

Mentally state:

> Topic → tension → author angle → why it matters

Do not write unless the passage is unusually dense and the note can be made in under 10 seconds.

## Selective interruption-skipping rule

The purpose of skipping interruptions is to recover the sentence spine, not to delete all material between commas.

### Temporarily skip

- appositives
- titles
- examples
- parenthetical definitions already understood
- decorative descriptions

### Do not skip

- contrast
- causation
- restrictions
- scope words
- clauses identifying who did what to whom
- phrases that identify the noun a pronoun or modifier refers to

### Sentence-spine rescue

When grammar becomes confusing:

1. Find the subject.
2. Find the main verb.
3. Remove interrupting descriptions temporarily.
4. Replace pronouns with explicit nouns.
5. Restore only the information needed to preserve logic.

Example:

> Citizen Kane, a quasi-documentary drama concerning a newspaper magnate, was influential.

Spine:

> Citizen Kane was influential.

## Question-phase rescue system

Use:

> STEM → PROOF → REPLACE → TEST

### STEM

Identify the exact requested operation:

- retrieval
- inference
- function
- main idea
- application
- strengthen or weaken
- contradiction
- analogy

### PROOF

Locate the one or two sentences that control the answer.

Do not reread the entire passage unless the confusion is global.

### REPLACE

Substitute:

- explicit nouns for pronouns
- plain-language synonyms for unfamiliar wording
- the exact actor, action, and target

### TEST

Ask whether the answer:

- matches
- reverses
- overstates
- narrows
- broadens
- introduces outside knowledge
- is merely possible but unsupported

## Contingency plan: passage is not sticking

### Trigger

Use a reset when several of the following occur:

- repeated full-passage searching
- inability to predict what correct answers should generally sound like
- repeated feeling that the passage has no coherent point
- recognition that the original main idea may be wrong
- six or more minutes into the question phase with continued global confusion

### Diagnose

- Local confusion: reread the relevant sentence or paragraph only.
- Global confusion: perform one strategic passage reset.

### Strategic passage reset: 45–75 seconds

Reread:

1. first paragraph
2. major transition sentences
3. first and last sentence of body paragraphs
4. final paragraph
5. sentences repeatedly referenced by questions

Then state:

> The author is mainly saying ___, and the passage develops that by ___.

Use one reset only. If the passage still does not click, choose the most passage-supported answer and move.

## Delayed-epiphany rule

A new understanding of the passage does not mean every previous question must be redone.

Revisit only answers that depended on the old model:

- main idea
- primary purpose
- author attitude
- questions where the original interpretation was explicitly used
- flagged items that felt incompatible with the passage map

Do not redo direct retrieval questions that remain valid.

> New passage epiphany ≠ redo everything.

## Main-idea panic contingency

If a main-idea question is not immediately visible and panic starts:

- stop hunting for the perfect orientation question after about 10 seconds
- read one complete question and its answer choices to identify the topic
- use the choices only for orientation, not to construct the author’s thesis
- return to first-paragraph framing and final-paragraph judgment for the actual main idea

## Reading aloud and subvocalization

### During review

Use full verbal reasoning:

> A says ___. The passage says ___. This matches or fails because ___.

### During timed work

Do not narrate every word. Verbalize only logical hinges:

- question stem
- decisive proof sentence
- final two-answer comparison

Useful compressed phrases:

- true but irrelevant
- possible, not supported
- same idea, different wording
- wrong actor
- wrong direction
- scope mismatch
- opposite of the passage

A faint subvocal rhythm may be tested as an attention anchor, but it should not force word-by-word reading of low-value details.

## Answer-choice taxonomy

Every review should record both the question type and the wrong-answer type.

### Question types

- retrieval / direct claim
- inference / implication
- function
- main idea / primary purpose
- author perspective
- application / new scenario
- analogy
- strengthen / weaken / assumption

### Wrong-answer types

- passage wording, wrong relationship
- true but irrelevant
- possible but unsupported
- reversal / opposite
- too broad
- too narrow
- right concept, wrong actor
- right concept, wrong direction
- outside knowledge
- paraphrase not recognized
- partial truth plus unsupported addition
- scope mismatch: paragraph versus passage
- full-definition requirement when one feature was enough
- extreme wording
- latest-thought selection

## Specific reasoning rules extracted from Test 32

### Direct claim through paraphrase

A retrieval question may be phrased as an inference question when it requires a synonym substitution.

Example:

- dialogue → conversation between characters
- novels and pulp magazines → novels and magazines

### Source-feature pairing

When a paragraph lists several influences, preserve the correct pair:

> Which source supplied which feature?

Do not select a source merely because its vocabulary appears nearby.

### Example does not equal full definition

A scenario can represent one component of a convention without reproducing the entire archetype.

### Local feature versus whole-passage summary

If the stem asks for a feature of many examples, the correct answer may come from one paragraph. Do not reject it because it does not summarize the whole passage.

### Contradiction test

Ask:

> Can both claims be true at the same time?

If yes, the answer does not directly contradict the passage.

### Analogy test

Ask:

> Which exact characteristics belong to both sides of the analogy?

Reject answers that add a trait that applies to only one side.

### Purpose versus effectiveness

An intended outcome does not prove a successful result.

Trigger words requiring evidence of outcome:

- effective
- successful
- caused
- ensured
- proved
- resulted in

### Changed-variable method

For new-information questions:

1. identify what changed
2. identify what stayed the same
3. determine which original claim is weakened or strengthened

### Missing-item rule

A missing item from a fragmented collection does not prove intentional exclusion.

Prefer the explanation supported by the condition of the evidence before inventing motive.

## Timing system: minimal-whiteboard version

### Core principle

Each passage is treated as a 10-minute visual block.

A typical target is:

- about 3 minutes to read
- the remaining time for questions

The timer map is a pacing signal, not a demand that every question take exactly one minute.

### Seven-question passage

If a passage block begins at `1:30`, begin questions by about `1:27`.

| Timer | Target |
|---|---|
| 1:27 | Q1 |
| 1:26 | Q2 |
| 1:25 | Q3 |
| 1:24 | Q4 |
| 1:23 | Q5 |
| 1:22 | Q6 |
| 1:21 | Q7 |
| 1:20 | Next passage |

Mental map:

> 7 → Q1  
> 6 → Q2  
> 5 → Q3  
> 4 → Q4  
> 3 → Q5  
> 2 → Q6  
> 1 → Q7  
> 0 → leave

### Six-question passage

Use the same map, with the final minute as buffer:

| Timer | Target |
|---|---|
| 1:27 | Q1 |
| 1:26 | Q2 |
| 1:25 | Q3 |
| 1:24 | Q4 |
| 1:23 | Q5 |
| 1:22 | Q6 |
| 1:21 | Buffer / flagged item |
| 1:20 | Next passage |

### Five-question passage

Use the same map, with two buffer minutes:

| Timer | Target |
|---|---|
| 1:27 | Q1 |
| 1:26 | Q2 |
| 1:25 | Q3 |
| 1:24 | Q4 |
| 1:23 | Q5 |
| 1:22–1:21 | Buffer / flagged items |
| 1:20 | Next passage |

### Mental cue

> Read until the 7. Then let the timer digit approximate the question position.

This avoids question-minute subtraction and keeps attention on the computer rather than the whiteboard.

### Important limitation

The map is approximate.

- Easy questions may take 20–40 seconds.
- Hard questions may take 60–90 seconds.
- If behind the map, stop perfecting and move.
- If ahead, preserve the buffer rather than spending it automatically.

## What to keep

- first- and last-paragraph emphasis
- paragraph-function labels
- selective interruption skipping
- mechanism matching
- narrow retrieval searches
- explicit recognition of author / passage / paragraph wording
- targeted verbalization when stuck
- answer-choice taxonomy during review

## What to stop

- passive scanning that loses the author’s argument
- repeatedly rereading the whole passage without diagnosis
- treating lack of memory as proof of absence
- requiring exact passage wording
- using outside knowledge to rescue an answer
- continuing after one answer has direct textual support and the alternative directly contradicts the passage
- using the whiteboard for calculations that the timer itself can supply

## Next-practice protocol

1. Use the paragraph-function map.
2. Protect actor-action-target relationships.
3. Begin questions by the `7` in a 10-minute block when the passage has seven questions.
4. Use `STEM → PROOF → REPLACE → TEST` on difficult questions.
5. Use one reset only if global comprehension fails.
6. Mark the wrong-answer type during review.
7. Record whether each miss came from:
   - passage comprehension
   - grammar / syntax
   - paraphrase recognition
   - scope
   - answer-choice logic
   - timing / panic
8. Do not add another strategy unless the current system fails in a repeatable way.

## Canonical compact version

> Map the paragraph. Protect the relationship. Locate the proof. Replace the pronoun. Test the choice. Use one reset. Let the timer digit pace the questions.

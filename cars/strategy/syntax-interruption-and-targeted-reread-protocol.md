# Syntax Interruption and Targeted Reread Protocol

## Purpose

Use this protocol for dense CARS prose containing parentheses, em dashes, appositives, quotations, name lists, historical details, and other syntax interruptions that obscure the sentence's core claim.

The goal is not to ignore details permanently. The goal is to secure the sentence skeleton first, then reintegrate only the material that changes meaning.

## First-pass reading sequence

1. Identify the subject, main verb, and core claim.
2. Temporarily pass over interrupting material when the sentence remains grammatically complete without it.
3. Preserve all logic-changing language.
4. Ask what role the sentence plays in its paragraph.
5. Return to skipped material only when a question or comprehension gap requires it.

## Usually safe to defer briefly

- Parenthetical examples
- Long lists of names, places, dates, or works
- Appositives identifying a person or object
- Decorative comparisons and metaphors
- Quotations whose role is already clear
- Repetitive elaboration
- Historical detail that supplies evidence but does not alter the claim

## Do not skip the logical hinge

Always notice words that change direction, scope, certainty, or comparison:

- but
- however
- although
- yet
- rather
- not
- only
- except
- despite
- therefore
- because
- instead
- even if
- primarily
- merely

A removable interruption may be deferred. A logical hinge cannot.

## Core-first example

Original structure:

> It was in the name of the purely and literally optical, not in the name of color, that the Impressionists undermined shading and modeling.

Core claim:

> The Impressionists undermined shading and modeling.

Reintegrated qualification:

> They did so for purely optical experience, not because of color.

Read the claim first. Then restore the contrast because the phrase beginning with "not" changes why the action occurred.

## Targeted rereading ladder

Do not reopen an entire passage without a specific target.

1. Read the target sentence.
2. Read one sentence before it.
3. Read one sentence after it only if the relationship remains unclear.
4. Check the paragraph's opening or closing claim.
5. Expand to the full paragraph only when the argument still cannot be reconstructed.
6. Reopen a larger passage section only for cross-paragraph questions.

Before rereading, ask:

> What exact information am I trying to locate?

## Question-type translations

### Function or purpose

Do not ask only, "What is this sentence about?"

Ask:

> Why did the author include this here?

> What larger claim does this example, quotation, person, or paragraph support?

Examples usually support a claim stated nearby. Read upward first when a person, case, quotation, or historical event is introduced.

### Analogy

Match the underlying mechanism, not the topic.

Reduce the passage relationship to a simple structure, then find the answer choice with the same structure.

### Inference or implication

Move one supported step beyond the text. Do not upgrade the claim into a broader philosophical statement.

### Assumption

Identify what must be true for the stated reasoning to work. Separate a necessary foundation from merely relevant information.

### Main idea or theme

Prefer the passage's payoff over its setup. Broad introductory material may establish context without being the author's final claim.

### "Based mainly on"

Translate as:

> What evidence is doing the most work for this claim?

This asks for evidentiary support, not the historical origin of the phrase.

## Scope-control rules

- "Different" does not automatically mean inferior, weaker, simpler, or more complex.
- "Can" does not mean should, must, always, or first.
- Evidence for some cases does not establish all cases.
- A criticism of one human ability does not establish that human intelligence is generally unreliable.
- Avoid answer choices that introduce intention when the passage establishes only effect.
- Prefer the narrow claim the author defended over a grander statement that merely sounds insightful.

## Paragraph-purpose check

After each paragraph, retain only a brief mental label:

- claim
- contrast
- cause
- example
- history
- objection
- response
- conclusion

Do not create a detailed written map unless the passage genuinely requires one.

## Answer-change rule

Change an answer only when there is a concrete reason:

- a missed contrast word
- a stem misread
- a scope mismatch
- direct contradiction from the passage
- a clearer mechanism match

Discomfort alone is not evidence.

## Operational summary

> Core claim first. Logical hinge always. Modifier second. Reread with a target. Match scope and mechanism.

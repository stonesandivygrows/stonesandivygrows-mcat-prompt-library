# CARS Operating System — May 11, 2026

Purpose: convert the recent CARS practice reflections into a usable test-day decision system. This is not a full passage review. It is the live strategy selector for deciding what to do while reading, while answering, and while confused.

Source basis: Jack Westin running guide through May 11, 2026, recent CARS practice reflections, and repeated patterns from April–May review sessions.

---

## 1. Core Diagnosis

The main issue is not a lack of CARS ability. The repeated issue is strategy selection under pressure.

When performance drops, it is usually because one of these happens:

- The question asks for a relationship, but I answer from topic familiarity.
- The question asks what a sentence or detail is doing, but I summarize what it says.
- The question asks for a soft inference, but I make the answer too broad, too moralized, or too abstract.
- I use a strategy that worked on a previous question even though the new question type needs a different strategy.
- I reread too much when confused, then lose time and track of skipped questions.
- Dense punctuation, names, titles, quotes, parentheticals, and commas overload the sentence before I identify the core meaning.

Operational rule:

**Do not ask, “What strategy do I remember?” Ask, “What kind of question is this, and what strategy fits this question?”**

## 2. The Play-by-Play Game Plan

### Before the Passage

1. **Reset.**
   - Am I tilted from the last passage?
   - Am I rushing?
   - Am I trying to prove I can do it fast instead of doing it correctly?

2. **Read the title/source.**
   - Only get topic + field + possible tone.
   - Do not decode the title deeply.
   - Do not panic at names, book titles, or unfamiliar fields.

3. **Check number of questions for that passage.**
   - Use the number only to set pace.
   - Do not scan the whole test to choose passage order unless specifically practicing that. Previous reflection: full-test scanning became overwhelming.

### During Passage Reading

4. **Read paragraph 1 for the setup.**
   Ask: **What problem, debate, or contrast is being introduced?**

5. **For each paragraph, ask one job question:**
   - Is this giving background?
   - Giving an example?
   - Introducing a counterargument?
   - Explaining the author’s real claim?
   - Showing why an old view is wrong?
   - Giving evidence?

6. **Highlight only function words and main-idea lines.**
   Keep:
   - however / but / although / instead / therefore
   - author thesis sentences
   - contrast words
   - definition pivots

   Stop:
   - long highlighting runs
   - highlighting whole sentences unless it is clearly the main claim
   - highlighting every name/title/detail

7. **Names and titles rule.**
   - Names = labels.
   - Titles = topic/tone clues.
   - Convert proper nouns into roles.

   Examples:
   - Descartes → old split view
   - Candace Pert → evidence against split
   - Black Elk → idealized Indigenous example
   - Tarantino → case study of music hiding violence

8. **Dense punctuation rule.**
   When a sentence has em dashes, commas, parentheses, quotes, or interruptions:
   - remove the interruption first
   - read the core sentence
   - then add the extra phrase only if needed

   Script: **Subject → Verb → Point.**

### Before Answer Choices

9. **Read the question stem and name the task.**
   Do this mentally, not necessarily in writing.

10. **Translate the stem into plain English.**
   - suggests = what the passage points toward
   - implies = one small step from the text
   - assumes = what must already be true
   - function = why this sentence/detail is here
   - analogy/application = same relationship in a new situation
   - weaken/strengthen = what damages or supports the author’s reasoning

11. **Predict the answer’s job, not the answer wording.**
   Example: “I need an answer showing practical experience made music teachers valuable.”

### During Answer Choices

12. **Eliminate by task first.**
   Ask: **Does this answer do the job the question asked?**

13. **Then eliminate by scope.**
   - too broad
   - too narrow
   - too extreme
   - true but not the task
   - related topic but wrong relationship

14. **If stuck between two answers:**
   - Which one needs fewer assumptions?
   - Which one is closer to the paragraph/main idea?
   - Which one answers the exact stem wording?
   - Which one is less moralized, less extreme, or less outside-world-based?

15. **If still stuck after 60–75 seconds:**
   - choose the best-supported answer
   - flag if possible
   - move on

## 3. Strategy Boundaries: What Works Where

### Main Idea / Primary Purpose

Good strategies:
- paragraph jobs
- author attitude
- thesis direction
- broad but passage-specific summary

Bad strategies:
- hunting one detail
- choosing the answer that only matches a memorable sentence
- over-focusing on examples

Rule: **Main idea answers should explain the passage’s overall job, not the most interesting detail.**

### Detail / Retrieval

Good strategies:
- go back to the specific line or paragraph
- read the sentence before and after
- paraphrase locally

Bad strategies:
- using only the main idea
- making a philosophical leap
- assuming because an answer sounds compatible, it is supported

Rule: **Detail questions are the only questions where direct lookup is the main strategy.**

### Inference / Implies / Suggests

Good strategies:
- stay close to the text
- take one small step
- ask what the paragraph points toward

Bad strategies:
- making the answer global
- choosing an answer because it sounds deep
- importing outside knowledge
- treating “suggests” as certainty

Rule: **Inference is not imagination. It is supported extension.**

### Assumption

Good strategies:
- ask what must already be true
- negate the answer and see whether the argument weakens
- look for the hidden bridge

Bad strategies:
- choosing what follows from the passage
- choosing a conclusion instead of a needed premise

Rule: **Assumption works backward. Implication works forward.**

### Function / “Why Mention?”

Good strategies:
- ask why the author included it here
- connect the detail to the paragraph job
- identify whether it supports, contrasts, illustrates, defines, or complicates

Bad strategies:
- only defining the detail
- choosing an answer that is true but not why it was included
- treating the sentence as isolated

Rule: **Function questions ask what the detail DOES, not only what it SAYS.**

### Application / Analogy

Good strategies:
- extract the relationship first
- ignore surface topic
- match structure, not content

Bad strategies:
- picking the answer with the same subject matter
- overvaluing familiar examples
- ignoring the direction of the relationship

Rule: **Analogy = same relationship, new clothes.**

### Strengthen / Weaken

Good strategies:
- identify the author’s argument first
- ask what would make that argument more or less believable
- check whether the answer targets the main reasoning, not a side example

Bad strategies:
- choosing something true but irrelevant
- attacking a minor detail
- choosing an answer that changes the topic

Rule: **A weaken answer must hit the argument’s support beam, not a decoration.**

### Vocab in Context

Good strategies:
- replace the word with each answer choice
- read the surrounding sentence
- match tone and function

Bad strategies:
- using only dictionary meaning
- using emotional association
- ignoring local context

Rule: **CARS vocab means “what does it mean here?” not “what does it usually mean?”**

## 4. Confusion Recovery System

### If I do not understand a paragraph

Do not reread immediately.

Ask:
- Who or what is this about?
- Is the author agreeing or disagreeing?
- Is this old view or new view?
- Is this example, evidence, or main claim?

If still confused:
- label it mentally as “setup,” “example,” or “counterpoint”
- move on

Reason: later paragraphs often clarify earlier ones.

### If I do not understand a question stem

Translate the verb:
- suggests / implies → what follows?
- assumes → what must be true first?
- why mention → what is the job?
- most supports / weakens → what affects the argument?
- analogous → what relationship repeats?

Then reread only the relevant part.

### If all answers look wrong

Ask:
- Which answer is closest to the passage?
- Which answer adds the least extra information?
- Which answer answers the exact task?
- Which answer is not just true, but useful?

### If two answers are close

Compare:
- verbs
- strength of language
- scope
- relationship direction
- whether one is true but irrelevant

### If I skipped a question

Do not rely on memory.

Use the navigation/checklist to return later. Past issue: when skipped questions accumulate, I forget what is unfinished and lose track of time.

## 5. Whiteboard / Scratchpad Role

The board is a dashboard, not a notebook.

Use it for:
- timing anchors
- skip marks
- emergency stem-word reminders
- maybe question-type abbreviations if they reduce confusion

Do not use it for:
- full paragraph maps
- rewriting the passage
- long notes during reading
- writing every question type beside every question

Preferred setup:

```text
CARS / 90 min / 53 Q
P1 80 | P2 70 | P3 60 | P4 50 | P5 40 | P6 30 | P7 20 | P8 10 | P9 0

STEMS:
suggests = points toward
implies = small step
assumes = missing bridge
function = why here?
analogy = same relationship

RULE:
Do not pick by topic. Pick by job.
```

## 6. Current Keep / Stop / Test List

### Keep Doing

- Reading in order instead of scanning the whole test when scanning increases overwhelm.
- Checking number of questions for the current passage.
- Using title/source for topic and tone only.
- Noticing last sentences and paragraph transitions.
- Compressing names into roles.
- Using finger tapping if it helps simulate touching the words without slowing reading.
- Reading silently rather than mouthing words.
- Treating “suggests” and “implies” as small-step inference stems.
- Asking what a paragraph or sentence is doing.

### Stop Doing

- Scanning the entire test when it creates overload.
- Writing full paragraph maps during timed passages.
- Highlighting too much.
- Going back to the passage for every question.
- Treating every question as a detail question.
- Choosing answers because they sound more sophisticated.
- Choosing broad moral/philosophical answers when the passage only supports a local claim.
- Changing answers at the last minute when the new choice is not passage-grounded.
- Treating names, titles, dates, and quotes as memory burdens.

### Test Next

- Mental paragraph map only: 1–3 words per paragraph in the head, not written.
- Dot-style highlighting for names/dates and one continuous highlight only for the main claim.
- Question-type checklist on the board as a reference, not as something to fill in.
- For each question, mentally say: “What job is this asking me to do?”
- For dense punctuation passages, practice stripping interruptions before rereading.

## 7. Question-Type Quick Chart

| Stem clue | Plain meaning | Best strategy | Strategy that may fail |
|---|---|---|---|
| primary purpose / main idea | whole passage job | paragraph roles + thesis | detail hunting |
| according to / states | direct support | line lookup | broad inference |
| implies / suggests | supported small step | passage-grounded extension | outside knowledge / extreme answer |
| assumes | needed hidden bridge | negate answer | forward inference |
| why mention / role / function | why author included it | paragraph job | literal summary only |
| analogous / similar to | same relationship elsewhere | extract relationship | matching topic only |
| weaken / strengthen | affects argument support | find support beam | attacking side detail |
| word means | meaning in context | local sentence | dictionary-only meaning |

## 8. Most Important Current Rule

**The next improvement jump will come from using fewer strategies, but choosing the correct one faster.**

Before answer choices, ask:

**“What kind of question is this?”**

Then ask:

**“What strategy works for that kind of question?”**

Do not let the passage topic, hard vocabulary, names, punctuation, or stress decide the strategy.

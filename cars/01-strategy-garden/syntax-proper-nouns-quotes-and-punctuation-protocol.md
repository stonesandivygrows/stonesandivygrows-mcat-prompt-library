# Syntax, Proper Nouns, Quotes, and Punctuation Protocol

## Purpose

Use this protocol when a CARS passage becomes difficult because of em dashes, commas, parentheses, quotations, names, book titles, dates, or long descriptive interruptions.

> **Find the sentence spine first. Add details back only when they change the meaning.**

## Sentence-spine method

When a sentence feels overloaded:

1. Find the subject.
2. Find the main verb.
3. State the core claim.
4. Temporarily remove interruptions.
5. Preserve all logic-changing words.
6. Reinsert only the details needed to understand the claim.

Ask:

- Who or what is this sentence about?
- What are they doing, claiming, or becoming?
- Why does this sentence matter to the paragraph?

## Punctuation translations

### Em dash

Usually signals an interruption, explanation, or aside.

First pass:

- read the sentence without the dash material
- confirm the core claim
- return to the interruption only if it changes tone, scope, or logic

### Parentheses

Usually provide extra explanation, an example, a translation, or a definition.

First pass:

- defer the parenthetical when the sentence remains complete without it
- keep it when it defines a key term needed for later reasoning

### Commas

A comma may separate:

- a removable description
- a list
- an introductory phrase
- a contrast
- a clause that changes the claim

Do not treat every comma as a new idea. Check whether the material between commas only describes a nearby noun.

### Colon

The material after the colon usually:

- explains
- defines
- lists
- gives the result promised before the colon

### Semicolon

A semicolon usually connects two complete but closely related ideas.

Ask how the second idea develops, contrasts with, or explains the first.

### Quotation marks

A quotation is not automatically the author's own position.

Ask:

1. Who is speaking?
2. Does the author agree, disagree, mock, qualify, or merely report?
3. Why was this quote included?
4. What claim does it support or illustrate?

## Logical hinges that cannot be skipped

Always notice words that change direction, certainty, comparison, or scope:

- but
- however
- although
- yet
- rather
- not
- only
- except
- despite
- therefore
- because
- instead
- even if
- primarily
- merely
- unless
- while

A decorative interruption may be deferred. A logical hinge may not.

## Names

> **Names are labels. Ideas and roles matter more.**

Convert names into roles:

- Descartes → old mind-body separation theorist
- Pert → modern researcher supporting connection
- Bartholomew → poorly informed historical example
- critic → opposing view
- historian → background source
- philosopher → theory source

Remember:

- role
- stance
- idea attached to the person
- whether the author agrees

Do not spend working memory on spelling or full names unless the question asks for that person directly.

## Book, article, and artwork titles

Treat titles as quick clues, not memorization tasks.

A title may reveal:

- topic
- tone
- viewpoint
- historical placement

Ask:

1. What subject does the title point toward?
2. Does it sound approving, critical, alarmed, or neutral?
3. Is the title itself part of the argument, or merely the source label?

Do not force a deep interpretation before reading the passage.

## Dates and places

Usually remember only their job:

- earlier vs later
- before vs after a change
- one region compared with another
- historical setup vs current example

Exact dates and place names matter only when the question makes them the target.

## Quotations, names, and examples: function rule

When a question asks about a quote, person, title, or example, ask:

> **Why did the author include this here?**

Common jobs:

- evidence
- illustration
- historical setup
- counterexample
- contrast
- source of authority
- example of ignorance or error
- support for the author's tone

Do not answer only with what the item literally says.

## Targeted rereading ladder

Do not reopen the whole passage without a target.

1. Read the target sentence.
2. Read one sentence before it.
3. Read one sentence after it if needed.
4. Check the paragraph's opening or closing claim.
5. Expand to the whole paragraph only if the relationship remains unclear.
6. Reopen a larger section only for cross-paragraph reasoning.

Before rereading, ask:

> **What exact information am I trying to find?**

## When grammar analysis is worth the time

Use full sentence breakdown when:

- the question turns on who performed the action
- a modifier could attach to two different nouns
- a comparison is unclear
- the answer choices reverse cause and effect
- removing the interruption changes the meaning

Do not fully diagram every difficult sentence during the first read.

## Fast survival card

- **Name:** role, not spelling
- **Title:** topic/tone clue
- **Quote:** speaker + purpose
- **Em dash:** remove interruption first
- **Parentheses:** usually extra
- **Comma phrase:** check whether it only describes a noun
- **Colon:** explanation follows
- **Semicolon:** linked complete ideas
- **Hinge word:** never skip
- **Reread:** target sentence first, not whole passage

## Operational summary

> **Subject → verb → core claim. Names become roles. Quotes need a speaker and a job. Titles give clues, not homework.**

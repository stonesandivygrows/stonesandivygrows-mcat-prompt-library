# Gametogenesis, Fertilization, and Development Terminology

## Core distinction

These terms describe different categories:

- **Organs:** ovary, testis, uterus
- **Support structures:** ovarian follicle, corpus luteum
- **Developing germ cells:** oogonium, oocyte, spermatogonium, spermatocyte, spermatid
- **Mature gametes:** ovum, spermatozoon
- **Postfertilization stages:** zygote, morula, blastocyst, embryo, fetus

## Female germ-cell lineage

**Oogonium (2n)**
- Diploid fetal germ cell.
- Divides by mitosis and develops into a primary oocyte.

↓

**Primary oocyte (2n; replicated chromosomes)**
- Begins meiosis I before birth.
- Arrests in **prophase I**.
- Located inside an ovarian follicle.

↓ completion of meiosis I

**Secondary oocyte (n; replicated chromosomes)**
- Receives most of the cytoplasm; the other product is the first polar body.
- Begins meiosis II and arrests in **metaphase II**.
- This is the cell released during **ovulation**.
- It is commonly called an “egg,” although the precise MCAT term is **secondary oocyte**.

↓ sperm entry triggers completion of meiosis II

**Ovum (n; unreplicated chromosomes)**
- Mature female gamete produced when meiosis II finishes.
- The other product is the second polar body.
- The ovum stage is brief because fertilization proceeds toward formation of the zygote.

## Male germ-cell lineage

**Spermatogonium (2n)**
- Diploid stem cell in the seminiferous tubules.
- Divides by mitosis.

↓

**Primary spermatocyte (2n; replicated chromosomes)**
- Enters meiosis I.

↓ meiosis I

**Secondary spermatocytes (n; replicated chromosomes)**
- Haploid cells that quickly enter meiosis II.

↓ meiosis II

**Spermatids (n; unreplicated chromosomes)**
- Haploid, immature cells.
- Differentiate during **spermiogenesis** without another cell division.

↓ spermiogenesis

**Spermatozoa / sperm (n)**
- Mature male gametes.
- Motile cells specialized to deliver paternal DNA.

## Ovary, follicle, oocyte, and ovum

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Ovary** | Female gonad containing many follicles and producing sex hormones. |
| **Ovarian follicle** | Supporting structure surrounding one developing oocyte. |
| **Oocyte** | Developing female germ cell undergoing meiosis. |
| **Secondary oocyte** | Cell that is ovulated and normally encountered by sperm. |
| **Ovum** | Mature female gamete formed after meiosis II completes. |
| **Egg** | Informal, imprecise term that may refer to the secondary oocyte or ovum depending on context. |

**Memory model:** the **ovary** contains many **follicles**; each follicle houses an **oocyte**.

## Follicle lineage

**Primordial follicle**
- Primary oocyte surrounded by one layer of flattened follicular cells.

↓

**Primary follicle**
- Growing primary oocyte with cuboidal granulosa cells.

↓

**Secondary/antral follicle**
- Developing follicle with multiple granulosa layers and a fluid-filled antrum.

↓

**Mature (Graafian) follicle**
- Preovulatory follicle containing the secondary oocyte.

↓ ovulation

**Corpus luteum**
- Forms from the ruptured follicle after ovulation.
- Secretes mainly **progesterone**, along with estrogen.

↓ without pregnancy

**Corpus albicans**
- Fibrous scar produced when the corpus luteum degenerates.

**Correction:** the corpus luteum does **not** turn back into a follicle.

## Fertilization and early development

**Secondary oocyte**

↓ sperm penetrates; cortical reaction helps block polyspermy; meiosis II completes

**Ovum + male and female pronuclei**

↓ pronuclear fusion / restoration of diploidy

**Zygote**
- One diploid cell formed by fertilization.
- First cellular stage of the new organism.

↓ cleavage without substantial overall growth

**Morula**
- Solid ball of blastomeres, commonly described as roughly 16–32 cells.

↓ cavitation

**Blastocyst**
- Hollow structure containing:
  - **Inner cell mass:** gives rise mainly to the embryo proper.
  - **Trophoblast:** contributes substantially to the placenta and implantation structures.
- Implantation begins at the blastocyst stage.

↓ gastrulation and organogenesis

**Embryo**
- Human developmental term used through the end of week 8 after fertilization.
- Major body plan and organ systems are established during this period.

↓

**Fetus**
- Begins at week 9 after fertilization and continues until birth.
- Characterized mainly by growth and maturation of existing structures.

↓

**Neonate**
- Newborn during the first 28 days after birth.

## High-yield chromosome terminology

- **Diploid (2n):** two homologous sets of chromosomes.
- **Haploid (n):** one set of chromosomes.
- After DNA replication, chromosome number does not change; each chromosome simply consists of two sister chromatids.
- Therefore, a primary oocyte or primary spermatocyte is still **2n**, whereas a secondary oocyte or secondary spermatocyte is **n** even though each chromosome still has two chromatids.

## MCAT sequence summary

### Female

**Oogonium → primary oocyte → secondary oocyte → ovum**

- Primary oocyte: arrested in **prophase I**.
- Secondary oocyte: arrested in **metaphase II** and ovulated.
- Sperm entry: triggers completion of meiosis II.

### Male

**Spermatogonium → primary spermatocyte → secondary spermatocyte → spermatid → spermatozoon**

### Development

**Secondary oocyte → ovum → zygote → morula → blastocyst → embryo → fetus → neonate**

## Common MCAT traps

- Ovulation releases a **secondary oocyte**, not technically an ovum.
- A **follicle** is a multicellular ovarian structure; it is not the oocyte itself.
- The **corpus luteum** is the remnant of the ovulated follicle, not a reverted follicle.
- The **zygote** is already diploid and forms after the maternal and paternal genetic material combine.
- The **morula** is solid; the **blastocyst** contains a fluid-filled cavity.
- Implantation occurs at the **blastocyst** stage, not the zygote stage.
- “Embryo” and “fetus” are developmental periods, whereas “zygote,” “morula,” and “blastocyst” are specific early structural stages.

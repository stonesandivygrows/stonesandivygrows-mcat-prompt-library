# Kinetic vs Thermodynamic Product Control

## Scope

This note preserves the corrected reasoning from the Organic Chemistry review discussion on kinetic and thermodynamic products, especially enolate formation.

Source alignment:

- Kaplan MCAT General Chemistry: kinetic and thermodynamic control
- Kaplan MCAT Organic Chemistry: enolates
- UWorld MCAT Organic Chemistry: product control and reaction-coordinate diagrams

---

## 1. The central distinction

### Kinetic product

The kinetic product is the product that forms **fastest**.

It is reached through the pathway with the lower activation-energy barrier:

\[
E_{a,kinetic}<E_{a,thermodynamic}
\]

It may be less stable after it forms.

### Thermodynamic product

The thermodynamic product is the product with the **lower final free energy** and greater stability.

It may form more slowly because its pathway can require a larger activation-energy barrier.

---

## 2. What the reaction-coordinate diagram shows

A reaction-coordinate diagram plots:

- y-axis: free energy
- x-axis: reaction progress/reaction coordinate

For competing products:

- the kinetic pathway has the lower transition-state peak
- the thermodynamic product sits in the lower final-energy valley

Do not confuse:

- **height of the hill** = activation energy and reaction rate
- **depth of the final valley** = product stability and thermodynamic favorability

---

## 3. Why low temperature favors the kinetic product

At lower temperature, fewer molecules have enough energy to cross the larger activation barrier.

Therefore, the lower-barrier pathway dominates:

\[
low\ temperature\rightarrow easiest\ barrier\rightarrow fastest\ product
\]

Low temperature does not make the kinetic product intrinsically more stable. It makes the easier pathway comparatively more accessible.

If the reaction is also short and effectively irreversible, the first-formed product becomes trapped.

Memory line:

**Cold and fast traps the easiest product.**

---

## 4. Why higher temperature favors the thermodynamic product

Higher temperature does not magically transform the reaction into a thermodynamic pathway.

It contributes in two important ways:

1. More molecules have enough energy to cross the larger activation barrier.
2. Reverse reactions become more accessible, allowing products to interconvert and approach equilibrium.

Under reversible conditions, the less stable kinetic product can revert toward reactants and re-form as the more stable thermodynamic product.

Thus:

\[
higher\ temperature+time+reversibility\rightarrow equilibrium\ control
\]

At equilibrium, the lower-free-energy product is present in greater abundance.

Memory line:

**Hot and reversible lets the system search for the most stable product.**

---

## 5. Temperature is not the only variable

Thermodynamic control generally requires:

- sufficient temperature
- sufficient time
- reversible conditions
- a pathway for interconversion

Kinetic control is generally favored by:

- lower temperature
- short reaction time
- strongly selective or irreversible conditions
- rapid trapping of the first-formed intermediate/product

Therefore, do not memorize only:

**low temperature = kinetic; high temperature = thermodynamic**

Use the deeper rule:

**Kinetic control traps the fastest pathway. Thermodynamic control requires equilibration.**

---

## 6. Enolate example

An unsymmetrical carbonyl compound may contain two different alpha positions.

### Kinetic enolate

- base removes the most accessible, least hindered alpha hydrogen
- forms faster
- often has the less substituted double bond
- commonly favored by a strong bulky base such as LDA
- commonly favored at low temperature

### Thermodynamic enolate

- proton removal/reprotonation is reversible
- equilibrium favors the more stable enolate
- often has the more substituted double bond
- commonly favored by smaller/weaker base, higher temperature, and longer time

The more substituted alkene-like enolate is often more stable through greater hyperconjugation and alkyl substitution.

---

## 7. Why a bulky base favors the kinetic enolate

A bulky base has difficulty approaching a sterically hindered alpha hydrogen.

It therefore removes the more exposed proton more rapidly.

This is a rate/steric argument:

\[
less\ hindered\ proton\rightarrow faster\ abstraction
\]

The base is not selecting the less stable product because it “prefers instability.” It is selecting the proton that is easiest to reach.

---

## 8. Why reversibility changes the answer

If deprotonation is reversible, the system can repeatedly:

1. form an enolate
2. reprotonate
3. form the alternative enolate

The product ratio then depends less on which enolate forms first and more on which enolate is lower in free energy.

This is the same broad principle used throughout equilibrium chemistry:

**Repeated forward and reverse reactions allow the most stable distribution to emerge.**

---

## 9. Kinetics and thermodynamics are separate questions

A reaction can be:

- thermodynamically favorable but kinetically slow
- kinetically fast but produce a less stable product

Thermodynamics asks:

\[
Where\ is\ the\ lower\ free-energy\ state?
\]

Kinetics asks:

\[
How\ difficult\ is\ the\ barrier\ to\ get\ there?
\]

Catalysts change kinetics by lowering activation energy. They do not change the equilibrium free-energy difference between reactants and products.

---

## MCAT traps

- Higher temperature does not automatically mean the thermodynamic product unless the reaction can equilibrate.
- The kinetic product is not necessarily the higher-energy transition state; it is reached through the lower-energy transition state.
- The thermodynamic product is not necessarily formed faster.
- Product stability is read from the final energy level, not the transition-state height.
- Bulky base favors the more accessible proton, not necessarily the most stable enolate.
- A catalyst speeds forward and reverse pathways and does not change the equilibrium constant.

## Final memory rule

**Kinetic = lower hill, faster arrival.**  
**Thermodynamic = deeper valley, more stable destination.**  
**Cold/fast traps the first route; hot/reversible allows equilibration.**

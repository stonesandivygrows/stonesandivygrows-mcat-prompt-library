## 2. Halo, reverse halo, and attractiveness stereotype

The user's original question asked for the difference among three concepts that genuinely overlap.

| Concept | Exact boundary | Example cue |
|---|---|---|
| Halo effect | One positive trait or overall positive impression spills into unrelated judgments | “Kind, so probably intelligent and competent” |
| Devil effect or reverse halo | One negative trait or poor impression spills into unrelated negative judgments | “Rude once, so probably dishonest and lazy” |
| Physical-attractiveness stereotype | Attractive people are assumed to possess other positive traits | “Attractive, therefore capable and friendly” |

Hierarchy:

    Impression-formation bias
    ├── halo effect
    │   └── physical-attractiveness stereotype is a specific positive halo
    └── devil effect or reverse halo is the negative spillover pattern

MCAT choice rule:

- If the stem explicitly emphasizes **physical beauty**, choose physical-attractiveness stereotype.
- If any one favorable impression spreads broadly, choose halo effect.
- If a negative impression spreads broadly, choose devil/reverse halo.

## 3. Social cognition and attribution

### Attribution map

    Behavior observed
    ├── dispositional attribution → person or trait cause
    └── situational attribution → environment or context cause

    Kelley cues
    ├── consistency
    ├── distinctiveness
    └── consensus

### Bias distinctions

| Bias | Correct boundary |
|---|---|
| Fundamental attribution error | Overweight disposition and underweight situation when judging others |
| Actor-observer bias | Explain own behavior situationally but others' behavior dispositionally |
| Self-serving bias | Attribute successes internally and failures externally |
| False consensus | Overestimate how much others share one's beliefs or behavior |
| Projection bias | Assume others' mental states/preferences resemble one's own |
| Confirmation bias | Seek or interpret evidence to support existing belief |
| Hindsight bias | After outcome, believe it was more predictable |
| Just-world hypothesis | Believe people generally receive outcomes they deserve |
| Optimism bias | Underestimate one's own likelihood of negative events |
| Illusory correlation | Perceive an association that is weak or absent |

### Stereotype, prejudice, and discrimination

    stereotype = cognitive belief
    prejudice = affective attitude
    discrimination = behavior

Stigma is a socially discrediting label/status and can contribute to all three levels.

## 4. Influence is the umbrella; persuasion is one route

The user asked where Cialdini's principles fit within the larger social-psychology map.

Correct hierarchy:

    Social influence
    ├── conformity
    ├── compliance
    ├── obedience
    ├── persuasion
    ├── minority influence
    └── group processes

Persuasion is **message-based attitude or behavior change**. Influence is the broader category.

### Conformity, compliance, and obedience

| Term | Source of pressure |
|---|---|
| Conformity | Align with group norms |
| Compliance | Agree to a request |
| Obedience | Follow command from authority |

### Cialdini's six principles

| Principle | Cue | Larger connections |
|---|---|---|
| Reciprocity | Return favors | Compliance and exchange |
| Commitment/consistency | Act consistently with prior choice | Cognitive dissonance and self-perception |
| Social proof | Others doing it signals correctness | Conformity |
| Authority | Expert/status cue | Obedience |
| Liking | Persuader is attractive/familiar/similar | Interpersonal attraction and halo |
| Scarcity | Limited availability raises value | Motivation and reactance |

Mnemonic present in the deck: **RSSCAL**, with order varying by mnemonic form. Learn the six names rather than depending on one order.

### Elaboration Likelihood Model

| Route | Processing | More likely when |
|---|---|---|
| Central | Evaluate argument quality | Motivation and ability are high |
| Peripheral | Use superficial/emotional/status cues | Motivation or ability is low |

Attractiveness and authority can function as peripheral cues, but an expert's evidence can also enter central processing when the argument itself is evaluated.

## 5. Attitudes and behavior change

    Attitude
    ├── affective component
    ├── behavioral component
    └── cognitive component

Cognitive dissonance:

- Inconsistency among beliefs, attitudes, or behavior produces discomfort.
- People may change attitude, behavior, or interpretation to reduce it.
- Effort justification increases perceived value of an outcome after costly effort.

Theory distinctions:

| Theory | Main question |
|---|---|
| Theory of planned behavior | How attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived control form intention |
| Self-perception theory | How people infer attitudes from observing their own behavior |
| Cognitive dissonance | How inconsistency motivates reduction of discomfort |

## 6. Group behavior

| Concept | Boundary |
|---|---|
| Social facilitation | Presence of others improves well-learned/simple performance but may impair difficult/new performance |
| Social loafing | Individual effort decreases when contribution is pooled and less identifiable |
| Deindividuation | Reduced self-awareness/restraint in group anonymity |
| Group polarization | Discussion strengthens the group's initial tendency |
| Groupthink | Desire for consensus suppresses critical evaluation |
| Bystander effect | Helping decreases as perceived responsibility diffuses |
| Diffusion of responsibility | Each person feels less personally responsible |

Do not collapse:

- Social facilitation concerns **audience presence and task difficulty**.
- Social loafing concerns **reduced accountable effort in a group product**.

## 8. Culture, subculture, microculture, syncretism, and amalgamation

### Culture hierarchy

    Culture
    ├── dominant culture
    ├── subculture
    ├── counterculture
    └── microculture

| Term | Correct boundary |
|---|---|
| Subculture | Group with distinctive values/practices within larger culture; may be broad and enduring |
| Counterculture | Subculture that rejects important dominant norms |
| Microculture | Small, localized, or often temporary cultural setting |

The Pankow statement that a microculture “cannot support someone throughout the lifespan” should not become a universal law. The tested distinction is that microcultures are typically smaller and more temporary, while subcultures can be larger and enduring.

### Syncretism versus amalgamation

| Term | What merges |
|---|---|
| Syncretism | Beliefs, traditions, or religious/cultural practices combine into a blended system |
| Amalgamation | Groups combine through intermarriage and social blending, producing a new group identity |

Memory boundary:

- **Syncretism blends systems of meaning.**
- **Amalgamation blends populations/groups.**

Do not use them as exact synonyms for assimilation. Assimilation involves adoption of a dominant culture and loss/reduction of distinctiveness.

## 9. Operant conditioning: use two independent questions

The user's confusion came from trying to interpret “positive” as good and “negative” as bad.

Decision sequence:

1. Does behavior increase or decrease?
2. Is a stimulus added or removed?

| | Add stimulus | Remove stimulus |
|---|---|---|
| Behavior increases | Positive reinforcement | Negative reinforcement |
| Behavior decreases | Positive punishment | Negative punishment |

Corrections:

- Reinforcement always increases behavior.
- Punishment always decreases behavior.
- Positive means add.
- Negative means remove.

Examples:

- Add candy to increase studying → positive reinforcement.
- Remove an aversive seatbelt alarm after buckling to increase buckling → negative reinforcement.
- Add a shock to reduce a behavior → positive punishment.
- Remove phone privileges to reduce a behavior → negative punishment.

The phrase “positive reinforcement to discourage” is internally contradictory; it describes punishment if the behavior decreases.

## 10. Reinforcement schedules and extinction

| Schedule | Requirement | Example structure |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed ratio | Set number of responses | Reward every fifth response |
| Variable ratio | Unpredictable number around an average | Gambling |
| Fixed interval | First response after fixed time | Regularly scheduled interval |
| Variable interval | First response after unpredictable time | Random checks |

Patterns:

- Continuous reinforcement teaches quickly but extinguishes more easily.
- Variable-ratio schedules generate high, persistent responding.
- Extinction occurs when reinforcement stops.
- Extinction burst is a temporary spike in the old behavior when reinforcement first disappears.

## 11. Habituation, sensitization, and sensory adaptation

| Term | Level of change |
|---|---|
| Sensory adaptation | Receptor or early neural response decreases during constant stimulation |
| Habituation | Learned decrease in behavioral response after repeated exposure |
| Sensitization | Increased response after repeated or intense stimulation |
| Dishabituation | Renewed response after a change in stimulation |

The deck sometimes uses “habituation” broadly for tuning out a stimulus. On the integrated map, preserve the behavioral-learning meaning and explicitly contrast it with receptor-level adaptation.

## 12. Sensation, perception, and signal detection

    sensation = detection and transduction
    perception = CNS organization and interpretation

### Thresholds

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Absolute threshold | Minimum intensity detected 50% of the time |
| Difference threshold or JND | Minimum detectable difference 50% of the time |
| Weber's law | JND is proportional to starting intensity |

### Signal Detection Theory

Signal Detection Theory asks how people make decisions under uncertainty.

| Reality | Response yes | Response no |
|---|---|---|
| Signal present | Hit | Miss |
| Signal absent | False alarm | Correct rejection |

Two separable ideas:

- **Sensitivity, d-prime:** ability to distinguish signal from noise.
- **Criterion, c:** response bias or willingness to say “signal present.”

A cautious criterion reduces false alarms but may increase misses. A liberal criterion increases hits and false alarms.

## 13. Memory and aging

### Memory taxonomy

    Long-term memory
    ├── explicit or declarative
    │   ├── semantic facts
    │   └── episodic events
    └── implicit or nondeclarative
        ├── procedural skills
        ├── priming
        └── conditioning

### Recall versus recognition

- Free recall requires generating information without the target in view.
- Recognition requires identifying previously encountered information among options.

Aging pattern from the project:

- Episodic, source, and some working-memory performance commonly decline.
- Semantic knowledge and procedural memory are relatively preserved.
- Fluid intelligence tends to decline earlier than crystallized intelligence.

| Oral | 0-1 |
| Anal | 1-3 |

Correction and scope:

- The deck claim that phallic fixation causes homosexuality is false and must be excluded.
- Personality being heavily shaped in the first five years is Freud's claim, not a universal scientific conclusion.

### Kohlberg

    Preconventional

    Conventional
    ├── interpersonal conformity or “good person”
    └── law and order

    Postconventional
    ├── social contract
    └── universal ethical principles

Kohlberg stages concern **moral reasoning**, not whether the person's behavior is morally good.

## 15. Aging theories and demographic age

| Theory | Core claim |
|---|---|
| Disengagement theory | Mutual withdrawal from roles with aging |
| Activity theory | Continued engagement supports satisfaction |
| Continuity theory | Maintain earlier patterns and identity |
| Life-course perspective | Historical/social timing shapes aging |

Primary versus secondary aging:

- Primary aging is intrinsic biological aging.
- Secondary aging reflects disease, behavior, and environmental exposures.

Demographic correction:

- Crude birth rate uses live births divided by **total population**, usually per 1,000 per year, not divided only by women.
- Dependency ratio compares dependent-age groups with working-age population.

## 16. Consciousness, psychoactive drugs, and reward

### Broad drug classes

| Class | General pattern |
|---|---|
| Depressants | Reduce CNS activity |
| Stimulants | Increase arousal and CNS activity |
| Hallucinogens | Alter perception and cognition |
| Opioids | Analgesia, euphoria, sedation, respiratory depression |

Heroin:

- semisynthetic opioid derived from morphine
- mu-opioid receptor agonist
- rapidly enters brain
- dangerous respiratory depression

Reward pathway:

    ventral tegmental area
    → dopamine
    → nucleus accumbens
    → reinforcement and reward learning

Tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal must remain distinct:

- Tolerance: more drug needed for same effect.
- Physical dependence: adaptation produces withdrawal when stopped.
- Substance-use disorder: maladaptive use despite harm; not identical to tolerance alone.

## 18. Research-method connections

High-yield boundaries:

- Correlation does not establish causation.
- Statistical significance does not prove practical importance.
- A graph that appears different must be checked for error bars, scale, and reported testing.
- Confounder relates to both independent and dependent variables.
- Reliability is consistency; validity is measuring what is intended.
- Cross-sectional compares groups at one time; longitudinal follows the same participants over time.

## 19. Personalized behavioral-science error engine

Before choosing a term:

1. Is this cognition, emotion, behavior, or social structure?
2. Is pressure coming from a group, request, authority, or persuasive message?
3. Does “positive/negative” mean add/remove rather than good/bad?
4. Is reduced response occurring at a receptor or behavioral-learning level?
5. Is the theory describing age, cognition, morality, personality, or social roles?
6. Is the answer a broad umbrella or a specific subtype?
7. Is the statement the theorist's historical claim or a modern scientific conclusion?

Highest-priority repairs:

- **Influence is broader than persuasion.**
- **Attractiveness stereotype is a specific halo.**
- **Reinforcement increases; punishment decreases.**
- **Positive adds; negative removes.**
- **Sensation detects; perception interprets.**
- **Adaptation is receptor-level; habituation is behavioral learning.**
- **Piaget formal operations contain abstract deductive reasoning.**

## 2. Tissue, ECM, adhesion, and cytoskeleton

### Basement membrane

| User thought or confusion | Correction |
|---|---|
| Basement membrane sounded like another plasma membrane. | It is a specialized extracellular matrix sheet beneath epithelium that anchors and separates epithelium from connective tissue. |
| Pseudostratified epithelium looked multilayered. | All cells contact the basement membrane, so it is one epithelial layer even though nuclei lie at different heights. |

### ECM versus cell-adhesion molecules

    Extracellular matrix
    ├── collagen
    ├── elastin
    ├── laminin
    ├── fibronectin
    └── proteoglycans

    Cell-adhesion molecules
    ├── cadherins → cell-to-cell adhesion
    └── integrins → cell-to-ECM adhesion

    Intracellular structural protein
    └── keratin → intermediate filament

Correction:

- **Collagen** is extracellular and provides tensile strength.
- **Elastin** is extracellular and provides recoil.
- **Keratin** is intracellular and strengthens epithelial cells.
- In a tissue scaffold question, “adhesion” often means cell-to-scaffold through integrins, not cell-to-cell adhesion.

### Cytoskeleton

| System | Protein | Structure and main jobs |
|---|---|---|
| Microfilaments | Actin | Solid thin filaments; movement, cell shape, cleavage furrow, muscle thin filaments |
| Microtubules | Alpha/beta tubulin | Hollow tracks; intracellular transport, spindle, cilia, flagella |
| Intermediate filaments | Keratin, lamins, desmin, vimentin | Rope-like tensile support |

Memory set **TACKE** belongs to structural proteins:

- Tubulin
- Actin
- Collagen
- Keratin
- Elastin

Do not treat all five as cytoskeletal: collagen and elastin are ECM.

## 6. Resting potential and action potentials

### Resting conditions

- Resting membrane potential is approximately -70 mV.
- K+ concentration is high inside; Na+ concentration is high outside.
- K+ leak permeability is a major immediate determinant of resting voltage.
- Na+/K+ ATPase maintains gradients by moving 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ in per ATP.

### Action-potential sequence

    threshold reached at trigger zone
    → voltage-gated Na+ channels open
    → Na+ influx depolarizes
    → Na+ channels inactivate
    → voltage-gated K+ channels open
    → K+ efflux repolarizes
    → delayed K+ closure causes hyperpolarization
    → resting conductances restore baseline behavior

### Corrected distinctions

| Confusion | Correction |
|---|---|
| EPSP and action potential felt like the same electrical signal. | EPSP is graded and local. An action potential is all-or-none and regenerates along the axon. |
| Na+/K+ pump was treated as directly creating each action-potential phase. | The pump maintains long-term gradients. Voltage-gated channels create rapid phases. |
| Refractory period meant the neuron had no ions left. | Absolute refractory period follows Na+ channel inactivation. Relative refractory period reflects hyperpolarization and incomplete channel recovery. |
| Myelin itself carries the signal. | Myelin reduces current leak; action potentials regenerate at nodes of Ranvier. |

## 7. Chemical synapses

    action potential reaches terminal
    → voltage-gated Ca2+ channels open
    → Ca2+ enters
    → vesicles fuse
    → neurotransmitter diffuses
    → receptor binds
    → postsynaptic potential or cascade occurs
    → transmitter is removed or degraded

Ionotropic versus metabotropic:

| Receptor | Mechanism | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Ionotropic | Ligand directly opens an ion channel | Fast |
| Metabotropic/GPCR | G-protein cascade changes channels, enzymes, or transcription | Slower, amplified |

GPCRs are not “only for cAMP” and not “only for neurons.” They connect hormone signaling, neurotransmission, metabolism, vision, smell, and taste.

## 8. Autonomic nervous system

### Meaning of preganglionic and postganglionic

The user asked what “pre” and “post” meant and wanted the path from brain to target.

    CNS preganglionic cell body
    → preganglionic axon
    → autonomic ganglion
    → postganglionic cell body
    → postganglionic axon
    → target organ

Correction:

> Preganglionic and postganglionic are defined relative to the **ganglion**, not relative to the organ and not simply before/after any synapse.

### Transmitters

- All autonomic **preganglionic** neurons release acetylcholine onto nicotinic receptors.
- Parasympathetic postganglionic neurons usually release acetylcholine onto muscarinic receptors.
- Sympathetic postganglionic neurons usually release norepinephrine.
- Sympathetic sweat-gland innervation is a major cholinergic exception.
- Adrenal medulla receives preganglionic acetylcholine and releases mostly epinephrine plus norepinephrine into blood.

### Fiber lengths

- Sympathetic: generally short preganglionic, long postganglionic.
- Parasympathetic: generally long preganglionic, short postganglionic.

### Functional pattern

| Sympathetic | Parasympathetic |
|---|---|
| Increased heart rate | Decreased heart rate |
| Bronchodilation | Bronchoconstriction |
| Pupil dilation | Pupil constriction |
| Reduced GI activity | Increased secretion and peristalsis |
| Mobilized glucose and blood flow to muscle | Storage, digestion, elimination |

## 9. Neurotransmitters, drugs, and disorders

| Signal | High-yield role | Required links |
|---|---|---|
| Acetylcholine | NMJ, all autonomic preganglionic, parasympathetic postganglionic | Nicotinic versus muscarinic |
| Norepinephrine | Most sympathetic postganglionic signaling; arousal | Adrenergic receptors |
| Epinephrine | Adrenal-medulla hormone; fight-or-flight | Beta-adrenergic GPCRs |
| Dopamine | Movement, reward, cognition | Parkinson disease, schizophrenia, stimulants |
| Serotonin | Mood, sleep, appetite | SSRIs and mood disorders |
| GABA | Major inhibitory CNS transmitter | Benzodiazepines |
| Glutamate | Major excitatory CNS transmitter | Learning, memory, excitotoxicity |
| Endorphins/enkephalins | Endogenous opioid signaling | Pain and reward |

### Heroin classification correction

The drug is **heroin**, not “heroine.”

- Heroin is diacetylmorphine, a semisynthetic opioid derived from morphine.
- Strictly, natural morphine and codeine are opiates; “opioid” is the broader receptor-based category.
- MCAT resources may use “opiate” loosely to include heroin.
- Mu-opioid receptor agonism produces analgesia, euphoria, sedation, miosis, constipation, and dangerous respiratory depression.

Map hierarchy:

    Opioids
    ├── natural opiates: morphine, codeine
    ├── semisynthetic: heroin, oxycodone
    └── synthetic: fentanyl, methadone

## 10. Sensation, perception, and sensory adaptation

    stimulus
    → sensory receptor transduction
    → afferent neural signal
    → CNS processing
    → perception
    → emotion, memory, or behavior

Corrections:

- **Sensation** is detection/transduction of stimulus energy.
- **Perception** is organization and interpretation.
- **Sensory adaptation** is reduced receptor/neural response during persistent stimulation.
- **Habituation** is decreased behavioral response after repeated exposure.

GPCR sensory links:

- Vision uses rhodopsin and transducin signaling.
- Smell uses olfactory GPCR cascades.
- Sweet, umami, and bitter tastes use GPCRs.
- Salty and sour transduction relies more directly on ion channels.

## 11. Cardiovascular system

### Myogenic versus neurogenic

The user's question “What is myogenic?” becomes:

- **Myogenic** means activity originates in muscle tissue itself.
- The SA node spontaneously depolarizes, so the heart has an intrinsic rhythm.
- Autonomic nerves modify rate and conduction; they do not normally create each beat.
- Skeletal-muscle contraction is neurogenic because somatic motor-neuron input initiates it.
- Many smooth-muscle systems can be myogenic, but control varies by organ.

### Cardiac conduction

    SA node
    → atrial depolarization
    → AV node delay
    → bundle of His
    → bundle branches
    → Purkinje fibers
    → coordinated ventricular contraction

### Frank-Starling mechanism

    ↑ venous return
    → ↑ end-diastolic volume
    → ↑ myocardial fiber stretch
    → stronger contraction
    → ↑ stroke volume

This is an intrinsic cardiac relationship, not the same as sympathetic stimulation.

### Vascular resistance

- Arterioles are the major resistance vessels.
- Poiseuille reasoning gives resistance strongly inverse to radius to the fourth power.
- Small changes in arteriolar radius produce large changes in resistance and flow.

### Gauge blood pressure

- Reported blood pressure is pressure above atmospheric pressure.
- 120 mmHg gauge corresponds to roughly 880 mmHg absolute at 760 mmHg atmosphere.
- Clinical interpretation uses gauge values.

## 12. Respiratory system and innate barriers

### Cilia versus peristalsis

| Process | Motor system | Location/example |
|---|---|---|
| Ciliary transport | Microtubule-based cilia move material over epithelium | Mucociliary escalator in airways |
| Peristalsis | Coordinated smooth-muscle contraction | Esophagus and GI tract |

The esophagus moves a food bolus mainly through peristalsis, not cilia.

Respiratory cilia move mucus and trapped particles toward the pharynx.

### Respiratory depression from opioids

Mu-opioid activation suppresses brainstem respiratory drive. This is the lethal mechanism in opioid overdose and must cross-link the drug and respiratory maps.

## 13. Digestive system

### Movement

    swallowing
    → esophageal peristalsis
    → gastric mixing
    → intestinal segmentation and peristalsis

### Innate protection

- Gastric acid directly destroys many pathogens.
- **Gastrin** is a hormone that stimulates acid-related gastric activity; gastrin itself is not the antimicrobial acid.
- Mucus, enzymes, commensal organisms, and immune tissue contribute to GI defense.

### Starch and glycogen terminology

- Glycogen is the animal storage polymer.
- Amylopectin is the branched component of plant starch.
- Both contain alpha-1,4 chains and alpha-1,6 branches.
- Intracellular glycogen debranching and digestive starch breakdown must not be collapsed into one unqualified enzyme statement.

## 14. Immune system

### Innate versus adaptive

    Innate
    ├── immediate
    ├── nonspecific pattern recognition
    ├── barriers and soluble factors
    └── neutrophils, macrophages, dendritic cells, NK cells

    Adaptive
    ├── antigen specific
    ├── slower primary response
    ├── memory
    └── B and T lymphocytes

### Defensins versus interferons

| Molecule | Main action |
|---|---|
| Defensins | Antimicrobial peptides that disrupt microbial membranes and form pores |
| Interferons | Cytokine signals that induce antiviral states and coordinate immune responses |
| Lysozyme | Enzyme that cleaves bacterial peptidoglycan |
| Complement | Protein cascade supporting opsonization, inflammation, and membrane attack |

The user's correction:

- Defensins can “poke holes.”
- Interferons do **not** primarily poke holes; they warn and reprogram neighboring cells.

### Blood and immune naming traps

| Terms | Boundary |
|---|---|
| Plasma | Liquid component of blood |
| Plasma cell | Antibody-secreting differentiated B cell |
| Leukocyte | Any white blood cell |
| Lymphocyte | B cell, T cell, or NK cell |
| B cell | Can differentiate into plasma and memory cells |
| Phagocyte | Includes cells such as macrophages and neutrophils; B cells are not primarily phagocytes |

### MHC

- MHC I appears on almost all nucleated cells and presents endogenous antigen to CD8 cytotoxic T cells.
- MHC II appears on professional antigen-presenting cells and presents exogenous antigen to CD4 helper T cells.
- Cytotoxic T cells kill abnormal infected or malignant cells; antibodies do not perform that same direct cellular role.

## 15. Renal and fluid regulation

Key integration map:

    low renal perfusion
    → renin
    → angiotensin II
    → vasoconstriction plus aldosterone
    → sodium retention
    → water retention
    → increased blood volume and pressure

Required hormone boundaries:

- ADH increases water permeability in the collecting duct through aquaporin insertion.
- Aldosterone increases sodium reabsorption and potassium secretion.
- ANP opposes volume expansion by promoting sodium/water loss and reducing renin-aldosterone effects.

Glycogen cross-link:

- Kidney can express glucose-6-phosphatase and contribute to glucose production, although MCAT shorthand usually emphasizes liver.

## 16. Endocrine integration

GPCR examples:

- Beta-adrenergic receptor → Gs → cAMP → PKA
- Glucagon receptor → Gs → cAMP → glycogenolysis and gluconeogenic signaling
- Muscarinic receptors → Gi or Gq depending subtype

Correction:

- A receptor pathway is not defined by the organ where it appears.
- The same GPCR class can regulate heart rate, metabolism, gland secretion, neural activity, or sensation depending on ligand and cell context.

## 17. Reproduction and development

### Meiosis and segregation

- Homologous chromosomes separate during anaphase I.
- Sister chromatids separate during anaphase II.
- Mendel's law of segregation reflects separation of allele-bearing homologs into gametes.

### Testes

- Seminiferous tubules support spermatogenesis.
- Sertoli cells support germ cells and respond to FSH.
- Leydig cells produce testosterone in response to LH.

### Ovarian cycle

    LH surge → ovulation
    → corpus luteum forms
    → progesterone supports endometrium

    no implantation
    → LH support declines
    → corpus luteum regresses
    → progesterone and estrogen fall
    → endometrium sheds
    → negative feedback falls and GnRH resumes

## 19. Personalized biological-systems error engine

Before answering:

1. Is the term a cell, tissue, molecule, process, or control pathway?
2. Does the process originate intrinsically or require neural/hormonal initiation?
3. Is the motion caused by cilia, smooth muscle, skeletal muscle, or fluid pressure?
4. Is the immune factor directly killing, forming pores, signaling, presenting antigen, or producing antibodies?
5. Does the receptor open a channel directly or activate a cascade?
6. Is the word “plasma” describing blood fluid or an antibody-secreting cell?
7. Is the pressure relative to atmosphere or absolute?

Highest-priority repairs:

- **Myogenic means initiated by muscle, not unaffected by nerves.**
- **Cilia move surface material; peristalsis moves luminal contents by muscle.**
- **Defensins make pores; interferons signal antiviral defense.**
- **Basement membrane is ECM beneath epithelium.**
- **Preganglionic/postganglionic is defined around the ganglion.**
- **All autonomic preganglionic neurons release acetylcholine.**
- **EPSP is graded; action potential is all-or-none.**
- **Heroin is a semisynthetic opioid; respiratory depression is the lethal effect.**

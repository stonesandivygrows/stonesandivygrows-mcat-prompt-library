# MCAT Psych/Soc: Easily Confused Social Perception and Culture Terms

## Halo Effect

The **halo effect** occurs when an overall impression of a person influences judgments about that person's specific traits.

- A favorable overall impression can lead someone to assume additional positive qualities.
- An unfavorable overall impression can lead someone to assume additional negative qualities.
- The defining feature is that a **global impression spills over into separate trait judgments**.

### Example

A student seems confident and articulate, so an interviewer also assumes that the student is intelligent, dependable, and competent without separate evidence for each trait.

## Physical Attractiveness Stereotype

The **physical attractiveness stereotype** is the tendency to assume that physically attractive people also possess other socially desirable qualities.

Common inferred traits include:

- friendliness
- intelligence
- competence
- trustworthiness
- sociability

This is best understood as an **attractiveness-based example of the halo effect**.

### Example

A physically attractive applicant is assumed to be more capable or personable even when appearance provides no evidence about those traits.

## Reverse Halo Effect / Devil Effect

The **reverse halo effect**, sometimes called the **devil effect** or **horn effect**, refers to a negative overall impression causing negative assumptions about unrelated traits.

### Example

A person behaves rudely during one interaction, so an observer assumes that the person is also unintelligent, dishonest, and irresponsible.

### MCAT Caution

For MCAT purposes, the ordinary **halo effect** may already include both positive and negative global impressions. Therefore:

- Choose **physical attractiveness stereotype** when attractiveness specifically causes positive trait assumptions.
- Choose **halo effect** when one broad impression affects several trait judgments.
- Choose **reverse halo effect**, **devil effect**, or **horn effect** only when that term is explicitly offered and the question emphasizes a negative impression spreading to unrelated judgments.

## Comparison Table

| Term | Trigger | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Halo effect | General positive or negative impression | Other traits are judged in the same direction |
| Physical attractiveness stereotype | Perceived physical attractiveness | Positive personality or ability traits are inferred |
| Reverse halo / devil / horn effect | General negative impression | Other negative traits are inferred |

## Microculture vs. Subculture

### Microculture

A **microculture** is a small cultural group whose norms and influence are usually limited to a particular setting, activity, or period of life.

Examples may include:

- a college sorority
- a boarding school
- a youth organization
- a temporary workplace group

A microculture **cannot support a person throughout the entire lifespan** in the sociological sense because it does not provide a sufficiently broad and enduring cultural framework across all life stages.

This does **not** mean members receive no emotional, practical, or social support. It means the group does not organize a person's entire life from childhood through old age.

### Subculture

A **subculture** is a larger and more enduring cultural group within a broader society. It may shape identity, norms, values, relationships, and behavior across much or all of a person's life.

Examples may include:

- an ethnic community
- a religious community
- a regional culture
- a long-standing occupational or social community

A subculture may be sufficiently broad and stable to support members throughout the lifespan.

## Comparison Table

| Term | Scope | Duration | Lifespan support? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Microculture | Narrow setting or activity | Often temporary or stage-specific | Usually no |
| Subculture | Broad group within society | More enduring | Can be yes |

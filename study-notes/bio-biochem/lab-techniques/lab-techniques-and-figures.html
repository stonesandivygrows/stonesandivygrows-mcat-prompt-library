Section 1 — Gel Electrophoresis & Related

Gel Agarose Gel Electrophoresis
Separates:DNA or RNA fragments by size (negatively charged → migrate toward + pole)
Matrix:Agarose gel; pores act as sieve
How to read the gel image:
— Ladder (marker) lane: known size bands for comparison
— Smaller fragments = migrate farther (toward + end of gel)
— Larger fragments = stay near wells (top of gel, − end)
— Band intensity ∝ amount of DNA (brighter = more)
— Single band = one fragment size; smear = RNA degradation
Migration direction is from − (top, well end) to + (bottom). Small = fast = far. Big = slow = near top.
PAGE SDS-PAGE vs Native PAGE
SDS-PAGE:SDS denatures & coats proteins with negative charge; separates by molecular weight only
Native PAGE:No denaturant; separates by charge + shape + size — native conformation intact
Reading SDS-PAGE:
— Molecular weight ladder in one lane
— Protein band position → compare to ladder → get MW in kDa
— Coomassie/silver stain: all proteins visible
— Western blot follows SDS-PAGE: adds antibody specificity
SDS-PAGE separates by MW only — you cannot determine charge or activity. To preserve activity → native PAGE or gel filtration.
PCR PCR — Polymerase Chain Reaction
Purpose:Amplify a specific DNA sequence exponentially
3 steps:(1) Denature 95°C → (2) Anneal primers ~55°C → (3) Extend 72°C (Taq polymerase)
RT-PCR:mRNA → cDNA (reverse transcriptase) → then PCR; detects gene expression
qPCR:Quantitative PCR; uses fluorescent probe; Ct value ∝ starting template amount
Reading PCR gel output:
— Run amplicons on agarose gel
— Band at expected bp → amplification worked
— No band → primers failed, no template, or denaturation issue
— Extra bands → non-specific amplification
PCR requires primers flanking the target — primers determine specificity. Taq has no 3'→5' proofreading. For high-fidelity cloning, use Pfu/Phusion polymerase instead.

Section 2 — Blotting Techniques

Southern Southern Blot — Detects DNA
Target:DNA — detects specific DNA sequence in a genome
Steps:Digest (restriction enzymes) → gel electrophoresis → transfer to membrane → hybridize with labeled probe → visualize
Reading the blot:
— Band = sequence present; position = fragment size after restriction digest
— No band = sequence absent or restriction site changed
— Multiple bands = restriction site cuts within the target
Southern = DNA. Don't confuse with Northern (RNA). If the question mentions restriction enzymes + probe hybridization to detect a gene → Southern.
Northern Northern Blot — Detects RNA
Target:RNA/mRNA — measures gene expression level and mRNA size
Steps:Extract RNA → gel electrophoresis → transfer to membrane → probe hybridization → visualize
Reading the blot:
— Band presence = gene is being transcribed (expressed)
— Band intensity = expression level (brighter = more mRNA)
— Band position = mRNA size (compare to RNA ladder)
— Multiple bands = alternative splicing isoforms
Northern = RNA expression. No restriction enzyme needed (RNA doesn't need digestion). To detect protein → Western. To quantify expression more precisely → RT-qPCR.
Western Western Blot — Detects Protein
Target:Protein — confirms presence and size of a specific protein
Steps:SDS-PAGE → transfer to PVDF/nitrocellulose → block → primary antibody → secondary antibody (HRP or fluorescent) → detect
Reading the blot:
— Band = protein present; position → MW (kDa, compare to ladder)
— Intensity = relative protein amount
— No band = protein absent, wrong antibody, or sample problem
— Loading control (β-actin, GAPDH) confirms equal loading
Western detects proteins but NOT activity — an inactive mutant protein still gives a band. For activity → enzyme assay. Primary antibody specificity determines what you detect.

Section 3 — ELISA

ELISA ELISA — Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay
Purpose:Quantify a specific antigen (or antibody) in solution — liquid sample, not on gel
Types:Direct (1 antibody), Indirect (2 antibodies, more sensitive), Sandwich (capture + detection Ab), Competitive
Signal:Enzyme on secondary antibody converts colorless substrate → colored product; absorbance measured by spectrophotometer
Reading ELISA results:
— Standard curve: known concentrations → plot absorbance vs concentration → linear range
— Sample absorbance → interpolate from curve → get concentration
— Higher absorbance = more antigen present (direct relationship in sandwich ELISA)
— Competitive ELISA: MORE antigen in sample → LESS signal (inverse relationship)
In competitive ELISA the relationship is INVERSE: high antigen → blocks labeled antigen from binding → low signal. Don't apply direct-ELISA logic to competitive ELISA.

Section 4 — Chromatography

Chrom Gel Filtration (Size-Exclusion)
Separates by:Molecular size
Mechanism:Small molecules enter pores → delayed (slow). Large molecules excluded → elute first.
Reading elution profile:
— X-axis: elution volume (or fraction number)
— Y-axis: absorbance (protein concentration)
— First peak = largest protein
— Later peaks = smaller proteins
Elution ORDER is OPPOSITE to SDS-PAGE order. Large comes off FIRST in gel filtration; large stays at TOP in SDS-PAGE. Both are size-based but opposite direction.
Chrom Ion Exchange Chromatography
Separates by:Charge
Mechanism:Charged resin binds oppositely charged proteins. Elute with increasing salt (NaCl) gradient — higher charge proteins elute last.
Types:Cation exchange: negative resin, binds + proteins. Anion exchange: positive resin, binds − proteins.
Reading elution:
— Protein elutes when salt concentration matches its binding affinity
— pH affects protein charge; changing pH can elute without salt
Changing pH can change protein charge → changes when it elutes. Don't assume only salt gradient elutes. Also: a protein at its pI has zero net charge → won't bind ion exchange resin.
Chrom Affinity Chromatography
Separates by:Specific binding interaction (ligand-target)
Mechanism:Column contains immobilized ligand. Target protein binds specifically; all others wash through. Elute with competing ligand, low pH, or high salt.
Example:His-tag protein + Ni-NTA column; GST-tag + glutathione column; antibody + antigen column
Most selective purification — one-step can give near-pure protein. The "wash-through" fraction contains EVERYTHING that doesn't bind. Elution fraction = your target.

Section 5 — Spectrophotometry & Beer-Lambert

Spec Spectrophotometry & Beer-Lambert Law
Law:A = εlc   (Absorbance = molar absorptivity × path length × concentration)
Transmittance:A = −log(T) where T = I/I₀; A = 1 → 10% transmittance; A = 2 → 1% transmittance
DNA:Absorbance at 260 nm. A₂₆₀/A₂₈₀ ratio: pure DNA ≈ 1.8; pure RNA ≈ 2.0; lower ratio = protein contamination
Protein:Absorbance at 280 nm (Trp, Tyr residues); also Bradford assay (dye binding, 595 nm)
Reading a standard curve / absorbance spectrum:
— Spectrum: absorbance vs wavelength → peak = λmax (color absorbed = complementary to color observed)
— Standard curve: known concentrations → fit line → unknown concentration from absorbance
— Beer-Lambert is LINEAR only in a working range; saturates at high concentrations (A > 1.5 typically)
A solution that LOOKS blue absorbs ORANGE/RED light (complementary colors). The measured wavelength is the one being ABSORBED, not the color you see.

Section 6 — Centrifugation

Cent Differential Centrifugation
Separates by:Size and density using sequential speed increases
Order:Low speed → pellet cells & debris → medium speed → pellet nuclei → high speed → pellet mitochondria → ultraspeed → pellet ribosomes/microsomes
Reading results:
— Each pellet = organelle fraction at that speed
— Supernatant after each spin = smaller particles
— Purity requires multiple washes
Differential centrifugation fractions are ENRICHED, not pure. A "nuclear fraction" still contains some mitochondria that co-pellet at that speed.
Cent Density Gradient Centrifugation
Separates by:Buoyant density (equilibrium/isopycnic) or sedimentation rate (rate-zonal)
CsCl gradient:Used for DNA; particles float at their isodensity point. Heavy DNA (e.g., ¹⁵N-labeled) sinks lower than light DNA (¹⁴N).
Sucrose gradient:Rate-zonal; separates by size/shape; ribosomal subunits (40S, 60S, 80S) shown as peaks
Meselson-Stahl experiment (classic MCAT):
— ¹⁵N heavy DNA → grown in ¹⁴N → CsCl gradient
— Conservative: 2 bands (heavy + light)
— Semiconservative: 1 hybrid band after 1 gen; 1 hybrid + 1 light after 2 gen
— Dispersive: 1 intermediate band (both gens)
— Result = semiconservative (1 hybrid band)
In CsCl gradient: heavier density = lower position in the tube. ¹⁵N DNA is denser than ¹⁴N → pellets lower. Hybrid (¹⁵N/¹⁴N) is intermediate.

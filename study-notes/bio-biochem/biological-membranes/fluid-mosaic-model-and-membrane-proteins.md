# Fluid Mosaic Model and Membrane Protein Hierarchy

## Core model

The plasma membrane is a **dynamic, selectively permeable phospholipid bilayer**.

- **Fluid** describes lateral movement and flexibility within the membrane.
- **Mosaic** describes the mixture of lipids, proteins, and carbohydrates associated with the bilayer.
- **Selective permeability** describes how the membrane controls what enters and leaves the cell.

The phospholipid bilayer is the structural foundation. The other components modify its movement, stability, signaling, recognition, and transport functions.

---

## Phospholipid bilayer

### Amphipathic organization

Each phospholipid is amphipathic:

- **Hydrophilic phosphate head** faces an aqueous environment.
- **Hydrophobic fatty-acid tails** face inward, away from water.

A plasma membrane contains two phospholipid **leaflets**:

- **Outer leaflet** faces extracellular fluid.
- **Inner leaflet** faces the cytosol.

### Terminology

- **Leaflet:** one phospholipid layer within a bilayer
- **Monolayer:** one phospholipid layer total
- **Bilayer:** two phospholipid leaflets arranged tail-to-tail

### Selective barrier

The hydrophobic interior favors passage of small nonpolar molecules and opposes passage of charged or strongly polar substances.

- Cross relatively easily: O2, CO2, steroid hormones, other small nonpolar molecules
- Cross more slowly: some small uncharged polar molecules
- Usually require membrane proteins: ions, glucose, amino acids, and other large or charged solutes

## Fluid branch

### Lateral movement

Membrane lipids and many membrane proteins can move **side-to-side within the same leaflet**.

This lateral motion allows the membrane to:

- bend
- self-seal
- fuse with vesicles
- redistribute signaling components
- change shape without breaking

### Restricted flip-flop

Spontaneous movement from one leaflet to the other is uncommon because a polar head group would have to cross the hydrophobic core.

Enzymes such as flippases can move selected phospholipids between leaflets and help maintain membrane asymmetry.

### Membrane asymmetry

The two leaflets are not identical.

- Carbohydrates are generally displayed on the extracellular surface.
- Proteins have fixed orientations.
- Particular phospholipids may be concentrated in one leaflet.
- Asymmetry supports signaling, recognition, polarity, and apoptosis-related processes.

### Fluidity regulation

#### Temperature

- Higher temperature generally increases fluidity.
- Lower temperature generally decreases fluidity.

#### Fatty-acid saturation

- **Saturated tails:** straight, pack tightly, decrease fluidity
- **Unsaturated tails:** contain cis double-bond kinks, pack less tightly, increase fluidity

#### Fatty-acid tail length

- Longer tails have stronger intermolecular interactions and decrease fluidity.
- Shorter tails have weaker interactions and increase fluidity.

#### Cholesterol

Cholesterol acts as a **fluidity buffer**.

- At low temperature, it disrupts tight phospholipid packing and helps prevent solidification.
- At high temperature, it restrains phospholipid movement and limits excessive fluidity.

Cholesterol therefore stabilizes the membrane rather than simply always increasing or always decreasing fluidity.

## Mosaic branch

### Lipid components

#### Phospholipids

- form the main bilayer
- create the hydrophobic barrier
- permit lateral membrane movement

#### Cholesterol

- stabilizes the membrane
- buffers fluidity across temperature changes
- contributes to ordered membrane regions

#### Sphingolipids

- contribute to membrane structure
- are prominent in some signaling regions
- commonly participate in recognition and lipid-raft organization

### Carbohydrate components

Membrane carbohydrates are generally located on the extracellular surface.

#### Glycoproteins

Carbohydrate groups attached to proteins.

Common roles:

- cell recognition
- receptor interactions
- immune identification
- adhesion

#### Glycolipids

Carbohydrate groups attached to lipids.

Common roles:

- cell identity
- surface protection
- recognition

#### Glycocalyx

The carbohydrate-rich outer coating formed by glycoproteins, glycolipids, and associated carbohydrates.

Common roles:

- protection
- cell-cell recognition
- immune recognition
- adhesion

## Membrane proteins organized by location and common jobs

Location and function should remain visually connected, but they are not identical classifications.

Use phrases such as **common jobs**, **can function as**, or **often acts as** to avoid implying that every protein in one location performs the same function.

### Peripheral membrane proteins

#### Location

- attached to the membrane surface
- do not enter the hydrophobic core
- often bind integral proteins, cytoskeletal proteins, or polar phospholipid heads

#### Common jobs

- signaling support
- enzymatic activity
- scaffolding
- cytoskeletal anchoring
- maintenance of cell shape

### Integral membrane proteins

#### Location

- embedded within the lipid bilayer
- contain hydrophobic regions that interact with phospholipid tails
- may be partially embedded or span the entire membrane

#### Common jobs

- transport
- receptor activity
- enzymatic activity
- adhesion
- recognition
- attachment to intracellular or extracellular structures

### Transmembrane proteins

Transmembrane proteins are a subtype of integral membrane protein.

#### Location

- cross the entire bilayer
- contain hydrophilic regions exposed to extracellular fluid and cytosol
- contain a hydrophobic region within the membrane core

#### Common jobs

##### Channels

- form hydrophilic pores
- allow selected ions or water to move through the membrane
- usually mediate passive movement down an electrochemical gradient
- can be gated by voltage, ligands, stretch, or other signals

##### Carriers

- bind a specific solute
- undergo a conformational change
- move fewer particles per unit time than open channels
- may participate in passive or active transport

##### Pumps

- perform primary active transport
- directly use energy, usually from ATP
- move substances against an electrochemical gradient

Example: Na+/K+ ATPase

##### Receptors

- bind extracellular ligands
- transmit information across the membrane
- may activate channels, enzymes, G proteins, or second-messenger pathways

##### Adhesion and recognition proteins

- connect cells to other cells or extracellular matrix
- help cells identify self, nonself, and cell type
- are often glycosylated on the extracellular side

### Lipid-anchored proteins

#### Location

- covalently attached to a lipid inserted into one leaflet
- remain associated with one side of the membrane
- do not necessarily span the bilayer

#### Common jobs

- signaling
- enzyme regulation
- membrane localization
- assembly of protein complexes

## Transport terminology

### Transporter

**Transporter** is a broad functional term for a membrane protein involved in moving a substance across a membrane.

Depending on the source and context, transporter may include carriers, cotransporters, exchangers, pumps, and sometimes channels.

### Channel

- creates a pore
- does not bind and carry each solute through one molecule at a time
- passive
- moves solute down an electrochemical gradient

### Carrier

- binds a solute
- changes conformation
- can support facilitated diffusion or active transport
- is saturable because it has a limited number of binding sites

### Pump

- directly couples transport to an energy source
- commonly uses ATP
- moves a solute against its gradient

### Cotransport proteins

Secondary active transport uses the energy stored in one gradient to move another substance.

- **Symporter:** transported substances move in the same direction
- **Antiporter:** transported substances move in opposite directions

---

## Double-label rule

A membrane protein can be described using both a location label and a function label.

- Aquaporin = **transmembrane integral protein + channel**
- Na+/K+ ATPase = **transmembrane integral protein + pump**
- GPCR = **transmembrane integral protein + receptor**
- Cytoskeletal adaptor = **peripheral protein + anchoring/signaling protein**

### High-yield relationship

**All transmembrane proteins are integral membrane proteins, but not all integral membrane proteins are transmembrane.**

## Mind-map construction rules established from this topic

- Use statement headings rather than question headings.
- Use a question only when a branch represents a genuine decision split.
- Arrange information from broad concept to subtype to detail.
- Keep function connected to location when that supports visual learning.
- Mark functional branches as **common jobs** rather than absolute definitions.
- Avoid placing location labels and function labels at the same taxonomic level without showing their relationship.

---

## Common corrections

- **More compact** is not the same as **more dense** when describing protein shape.
- **Integral** and **transmembrane** are not synonyms: transmembrane is a subtype of integral.
- **Channel**, **carrier**, and **pump** are functional terms, not location categories.
- A channel is not automatically open; many channels are gated.
- A carrier is not automatically passive; carriers can participate in passive or active transport.
- Cholesterol does not have one fixed effect on fluidity; its effect depends on temperature.

Carbohydrate Oxidation & Reducing Sugar Comparison

Part 1 — Master Comparison Table

Term What gets oxidized / modified, and where Key structural feature Why it does or doesn't reduce Benedict's / Tollens' MCAT Trap
Reducing Sugar Anomeric carbon (C1) has a FREE hydroxyl (hemiacetal form). The aldehyde opens and reduces the test reagent. Free –OH on anomeric carbon → ring can open to aldehyde form ✅ Reduces both Benedict's (Cu²⁺ → Cu₂O) and Tollens' (Ag⁺ → Ag°). Sugar is the reducing agent; reagent is the oxidizing agent. Monosaccharides with a FREE anomeric –OH are reducing. If the anomeric –OH is involved in a glycosidic bond → non-reducing.
Non-Reducing Sugar Anomeric carbon is locked in glycosidic bond (acetal/ketal). Cannot open to aldehyde form. Both anomeric carbons locked (e.g., sucrose) OR anomeric C in glycosidic bond ❌ Does NOT reduce Benedict's or Tollens'. Ring cannot open; no free aldehyde. Sucrose is the ONLY common non-reducing disaccharide. Both anomeric C1 of glucose AND C2 of fructose are in the 1→2 glycosidic bond. Maltose and lactose ARE reducing.
Aldonic Acid
e.g., gluconic acid		 C1 aldehyde oxidized to carboxylic acid (–COOH). Only C1 is oxidized. Carboxylic acid at C1; primary alcohol intact at C6 ❌ Aldonic acid can no longer ACT as reducing sugar (already oxidized), but was once a reducing sugar before oxidation. The test detects the sugar, not the product. "Ald-onic" = aldehyde end (C1) is oxidized. Stronger oxidizing agents (nitric acid, HNO₃) go further → aldaric acid.
Aldaric Acid
e.g., glucaric acid (saccharic acid)		 Both C1 AND C6 oxidized to –COOH. Stronger oxidation than Benedict's or Tollens'. Dicarboxylic acid (COOH at both ends); formed by HNO₃ (nitric acid) oxidation ❌ Completely oxidized; not what Benedict's or Tollens' detect. Requires stronger acid oxidant (HNO₃), not Cu²⁺ or Ag⁺. "Al-dar-ic" = aldehyde (C1) + C6 both oxidized. Nitric acid is needed. This is stronger than aldonic acid formation. MCAT: HNO₃ → aldaric, not aldonic.
Uronic Acid
e.g., glucuronic acid, galacturonic acid		 C6 primary alcohol oxidized to –COOH. C1 aldehyde STAYS as aldehyde (intact). COOH at C6; aldehyde still at C1. Glycosidically polymerizable and pH-responsive. ✅ Still reducing! The C1 aldehyde is intact, so it can still reduce Benedict's/Tollens'. The C6 end is what's been modified. "Ur-onic" = urine-related; uronic acids are in glycosaminoglycans and glucuronide conjugates for drug metabolism. C1 is still free → still reducing. The trap: students confuse uronic with aldaric (both involve C6, but aldaric ALSO modifies C1).
Cellulose Polymer of β-D-glucose via β-1→4 glycosidic bonds. Humans cannot digest it (no cellulase). β-1→4 linkages; linear, forms hydrogen bonds between chains → structural rigidity. Each glucose residue internally has free C1... wait: in polysaccharide chains, C1 of internal residues ARE in glycosidic bonds. Effectively non-reducing in practice (only the reducing end of the entire polymer can open; the vast majority of C1s are locked). Not relevant in MCAT test reactions. Cellulose = β-1→4 (flat, strong). Starch/glycogen = α-1→4 + α-1→6 (coiled, digestible). The β bonds are what make cellulose indigestible to humans. Dietary fiber = cellulose.
Hemiacetal vs Acetal (Ketal) Hemiacetal: free anomeric –OH, ring in equilibrium with open chain. Acetal (ketal for ketoses): anomeric –OH reacted with –OH of another sugar → glycosidic bond, locked. Hemiacetal = reducing (can open). Acetal/ketal = non-reducing (locked). Fructose in pyranose form can appear as ketal. Hemiacetal → reducing (Benedict's+, Tollens'+). Acetal/ketal → non-reducing (both tests negative). Tollens' cannot detect fructose in its ketal form in certain contexts. But fructose IS a reducing sugar because it can isomerize to an enediol form that reduces. MCAT: fructose is still listed as reducing. The ketal note is a nuance for structure questions only.
Mutarotation Interconversion of α and β anomers via ring opening (to linear aldehyde) and reclosing. Only occurs when anomeric C1 –OH is FREE. Free anomeric –OH; equilibrium between α and β forms; optical rotation changes over time. Mutarotation means the ring IS opening (even transiently) → the sugar IS reducing. Glycoside (locked anomeric C) cannot mutarotate. Mutarotation is blocked when the anomeric –OH forms a glycosidic bond. Glycosides do NOT mutarotate. This is a clue on MCAT: if a sugar mutarotates → it's a reducing sugar.

Memory rule:
Aldonic = C1 | Uronic = C6 | Aldaric = C1+6
Benedict's / Tollens' detect a FREE anomeric –OH that lets the ring open to an aldehyde. They do NOT test for what end of the sugar is oxidized. A uronic acid (C6 oxidized) is still reducing because C1 is still free. An aldonic acid (C1 oxidized) is no longer a sugar in the ring form and is detected as an acid product, not a reducing sugar.

Part 3 — Quick Reference Cards

Benedict's Test
Cu²⁺ (blue) → Cu₂O (brick-red precipitate)
+ = reducing sugar present
− = no reducing sugar (stays blue)
Detects ALL reducing sugars (not glucose-specific)
Tollens' Test
Ag⁺ → Ag° (silver mirror on glass)
+ = reducing sugar or aldehyde
− = ketal/non-reducing sugar stays negative
Fructose: positive (isomerizes to enediol, then opens)
Aldonic vs Aldaric
Aldonic: C1 → COOH (mild oxidant, Br₂)
Aldaric: C1 + C6 → COOH (HNO₃, strong)
Both are oxidation products, not test reagents
Uronic Acid
C6 → COOH; C1 aldehyde INTACT
Still reducing (C1 still free)
Examples: glucuronic acid (liver conjugation), galacturonic acid (pectin)
Part of glycosaminoglycans (heparin, hyaluronic acid)
Cellulose vs Starch
Cellulose: β-1→4 (flat, H-bonds, structural)
Starch: α-1→4 + α-1→6 (helical, digestible)
Humans: no cellulase → cellulose = dietary fiber
β-amylase: cleaves α-1→4 from reducing end → maltose
Mutarotation Rule
Mutarotation occurs → anomeric C is FREE → reducing
No mutarotation → glycoside (locked) → non-reducing
Blocks: glycoside bond formation, methylation of anomeric –OH
Example: methyl glucoside does NOT mutarotate

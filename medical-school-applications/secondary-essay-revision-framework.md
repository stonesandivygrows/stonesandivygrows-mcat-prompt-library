# Secondary Essay Revision Framework

Use this framework for medical-school secondary prompts that ask how an applicant has demonstrated several institutional values and where the applicant still needs to grow.

## Core structure

Organize the essay into one paragraph per value or pillar. Each paragraph should have a distinct job while contributing to one developmental arc.

1. **Past foundation:** the experience or pattern that shaped the value.
2. **Present interpretation:** what the experience taught and how it changed later choices.
3. **Future application:** how the value connects to the school's community, curriculum, or programs.

For a three-pillar prompt, a reliable order is:

- **Commitment:** formative adversity or sustained responsibility
- **Curiosity:** questions and scholarly work that grew from those experiences
- **Leadership:** demonstrated experience, honest limitation, and intended growth

This sequence creates a natural progression:

> lived experience → questions arising from experience → responsibility to act

## Paragraph balance

For a paragraph of roughly 150–165 words, use an approximate three-part balance:

- **One-third — event:** describe only the facts needed to understand the situation.
- **One-third — meaning:** explain how the response demonstrates the pillar.
- **One-third — connection:** connect the lesson to medicine, the target community, and the school.

Do not allow the event narrative to consume the paragraph. The admissions value lies primarily in the applicant's interpretation, choices, and future application.

## Commitment paragraph

### Purpose

Show sustained action under difficult or uncertain conditions rather than simply describing hardship.

### Recommended progression

1. Identify the instability, responsibility, or obstacle.
2. State what the applicant repeatedly did despite it.
3. Name the capacities developed: structure, steadiness, persistence, willingness to accept support, or sensitivity to hidden burdens.
4. Connect those capacities to patients or communities facing related barriers.

### Key distinction

**Hardship is context; commitment is the pattern of action within that context.**

Avoid ending with resilience alone. Translate resilience into a clinical or community-facing capacity, such as recognizing quiet signs of strain before they become visible in a formal encounter.

## Curiosity paragraph

### Purpose

Show that lived observations generated questions, and that those questions led to disciplined investigation.

### Recommended progression

1. Use a bridge from the preceding experience: “Those experiences also led me to ask…”
2. State the central question or intellectual concern.
3. Select two or three research or scholarly examples that answer different parts of that question.
4. Explain the skills developed through those efforts.
5. Connect the inquiry to specific school or regional priorities.

### Key distinction

Do not present research projects as an inventory. Each project should appear as evidence of one continuing line of inquiry.

## Leadership paragraph

### Purpose

Answer the growth portion of the prompt without minimizing prior leadership.

### Recommended progression

1. State directly that leadership offers the most room for growth.
2. Establish prior evidence through selected roles and responsibilities.
3. Identify the limitation precisely.
4. Explain the kind of leader the applicant wants to become.
5. Name a school program, structure, or community partnership that models that growth.

### Useful distinction

A strong formulation separates two leadership stages:

- **Reliable contributor:** coordinates, teaches, supports, communicates, and sustains team goals.
- **System-shaping leader:** helps set priorities, directs change, distributes responsibility, and builds structures with affected communities.

The growth claim should be that the applicant is moving from the first stage toward the second—not that the applicant lacks leadership experience entirely.

## Transition framework

Transitions should clarify chronology, causation, or development. They should not merely decorate sentences.

### Between events

- “As the pandemic progressed…”
- “During the following academic year…”
- “Later, in a role that required…”
- “By the time I began…”

### From event to interpretation

- “Persisting through those years required…”
- “Taken together, these experiences taught me…”
- “What began as adaptation became…”
- “Beyond endurance, the experience sharpened…”

### Between paragraphs

- “Those experiences also shaped my curiosity.”
- “The same instability that tested my commitment prompted me to ask…”
- “While commitment sustained me and curiosity guided my questions, leadership remains the area in which I have the most room to grow.”

### From school fit to future growth

- “In that setting…”
- “Through this model…”
- “This approach would allow me to…”
- “There, I hope to grow from…”

## Chronology audit

Before finalizing, verify that the reader can answer:

- What happened first?
- Which later activity grew from that experience?
- Which roles occurred during college, after graduation, or abroad?
- What did the applicant already demonstrate?
- What remains a future growth goal?

Use dates sparingly, but include temporal signals whenever roles overlap or the developmental sequence may be unclear.

## School-specificity audit

Every named school resource should perform one of three functions:

- provide a setting where an existing strength can be applied;
- address the specific growth area identified;
- connect the applicant's experience to a documented regional need.

Avoid adding a program name only to prove research was conducted. Explain the mechanism of fit.

## Compression rules

When reducing word count:

- cut repeated descriptions of the same hardship;
- replace lists of duties with one representative responsibility and its significance;
- remove generic conclusions such as “this made me resilient” unless followed by a specific application;
- preserve interpretation and school connection before preserving scene detail;
- combine overlapping sentences when one provides evidence and the next merely restates it.

## Final quality-control checklist

- [ ] Each paragraph has one dominant pillar.
- [ ] The opening sentence states that paragraph's purpose.
- [ ] Events are concise and chronologically clear.
- [ ] Reflection occupies more space than description.
- [ ] Research experiences form a coherent inquiry rather than a résumé list.
- [ ] The growth area is honest but not self-disqualifying.
- [ ] School programs are linked to a specific developmental need.
- [ ] Community references explain relevance rather than serving as geographic decoration.
- [ ] Transitions show progression: experience → inquiry → leadership.
- [ ] The conclusion describes a future capacity, not merely admiration for the institution.

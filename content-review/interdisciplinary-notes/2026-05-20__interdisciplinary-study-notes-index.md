## Core interdisciplinary anchors

### 1. Metabolism as a whole-body logistics system

Primary subjects: Biochemistry, Biology, General Chemistry, Physics.

Key ideas:
- Blood glucose is treated as a regulated power grid.
- Liver acts as the central bank / altruistic organ for systemic blood glucose.
- Skeletal muscle acts as the selfish worker because it lacks glucose-6-phosphatase.
- Cardiac muscle acts as the marathon runner because it relies heavily on aerobic beta-oxidation.
- Brain acts as the demanding VIP because it consumes a disproportionate share of oxygen and glucose.
- Glucose-6-phosphate is the central traffic node for glycolysis, glycogenesis, gluconeogenesis, and pentose phosphate pathway routing.

MCAT trap layer:
- Muscle glycogen cannot maintain blood glucose.
- Acetyl CoA carbons from even-chain fatty acids cannot become glucose.
- High ATP can be both a substrate signal and an allosteric inhibition signal depending on enzyme site context.

### 2. Membrane architecture and lipid transport

Primary subjects: Biochemistry, Biology, Organic Chemistry, General Chemistry.

Key ideas:
- Lipoproteins solve the hydrophobic cargo / aqueous blood problem.
- Cell membranes use phospholipid bilayers because water exists on both sides.
- Lipoproteins use phospholipid monolayers because the inside is hydrophobic fat cargo, not water.
- Apolipoproteins function as structural scaffolds, docking labels, and receptor recognition tools.
- LDL accumulation in arterial walls is pathological, but LDL itself is physiologically necessary.
- HDL performs reverse cholesterol transport and depends on ApoA1 / LCAT chemistry.

MCAT trap layer:
- Do not reduce LDL to simply bad or HDL to simply good; mechanism and location matter.
- Cholesterol is mostly hydrophobic but slightly amphipathic due to its hydroxyl group.
- Esterification removes cholesterol’s polar handle and forces it into the hydrophobic lipoprotein core.

### 3. Thermodynamics, ATP coupling, and fuel choice

Primary subjects: Biochemistry, General Chemistry, Physics.

Key ideas:
- ATP is useful as short-term cellular currency because it releases manageable amounts of free energy.
- Lipids are long-term storage because they are highly reduced and hydrophobic.
- Coupling ties an exergonic reaction to an endergonic reaction so the net process becomes favorable.
- Respiratory quotient links stoichiometry to fuel selection.
- Carbohydrates have RQ near 1.0; lipids have RQ near 0.7 because they require more oxygen per CO2 produced.
- Thioester bonds, such as in acetyl CoA and succinyl CoA, are high-energy because sulfur provides poor resonance stabilization compared with oxygen.

MCAT trap layer:
- Energy released by ATP hydrolysis does not vanish; excess energy becomes heat.
- Uncouplers disrupt ATP production by collapsing the proton gradient while increasing heat production.
- Do not confuse thermodynamic favorability with reaction speed.

### 4. Structural biology through physics

Primary subjects: Physics, Biochemistry, Biology.

Key ideas:
- Diffraction explains how wave behavior becomes a structural biology tool.
- X-ray crystallography works because X-ray wavelengths are close to atomic spacing.
- A protein crystal acts as a 3D diffraction grating.
- Interference patterns allow mathematical reconstruction of atomic coordinates.
- This links optics, waves, molecular structure, DNA, and protein function.

MCAT trap layer:
- Prisms separate colors by refraction / dispersion.
- Diffraction gratings separate colors by wavelength-dependent interference angles.
- Thin film interference is reflection-based, not diffraction-based.

### 5. Osmosis, gradients, and renal physiology

Primary subjects: Biology, General Chemistry, Physics.

Key ideas:
- Loop of Henle depends on countercurrent multiplication.
- Descending limb: permeable to water, not salt.
- Ascending limb: permeable to salt, not water.
- Medullary salt gradient physically draws water out of filtrate.
- Water movement follows osmotic gradients across semipermeable membranes.

MCAT trap layer:
- Do not memorize nephron segments as isolated anatomy; track permeability rules and gradient direction.
- Filtrate concentration changes because water and solute permeability are separated by segment.

### 6. Calcium, phosphorylation, and muscle control

Primary subjects: Biology, General Chemistry, Physics, Biochemistry.

Key ideas:
- Calcium ions act as universal intracellular messengers.
- Skeletal muscle uses troponin / tropomyosin regulation.
- Smooth muscle uses calmodulin and myosin light-chain kinase.
- Phosphorylation changes protein behavior by adding a charged phosphate group.
- Smooth muscle latch state sustains tension with low ATP cost.

MCAT trap layer:
- Speed is not always the goal; smooth muscle prioritizes sustained, energy-efficient tension.
- The Ca2+ notation is not cosmetic; charge and electrostatics explain signaling behavior.

### 7. Molecular biology and biotechnology as mechanism chains

Primary subjects: Biochemistry, Biology, Organic Chemistry.

Key ideas:
- DNA to RNA to protein is an information logistics system.
- Eukaryotic primer replacement uses RNase H and DNA polymerase delta.
- CRISPR can be understood as programmable sequence targeting, conceptually related to restriction enzyme logic.
- Homologous recombination can be used to guide repair after a targeted cut.

MCAT trap layer:
- Always separate prokaryotic and eukaryotic enzyme names.
- Restriction enzymes are sequence-specific but not programmable in the same way CRISPR is.

### 8. Behavioral science bridges physical stimulus to experience

Primary subjects: Psychology/Sociology, Biology, Physics.

Key ideas:
- Sensation translates physical energy into neural signals.
- Perception is active construction, not passive recording.
- Bottom-up processing starts with raw stimuli: photons, vibrations, chemicals, pressure, temperature.
- Top-down processing uses memory, expectation, and gestalt principles to interpret sensory input.
- Conditioning links behavior, reinforcement, prediction, and neurobiology.

MCAT trap layer:
- Do not treat P/S as pure vocabulary; many passages test experimental design, biology, and stimulus-response logic.
- Positive/negative in operant conditioning refers to adding/removing a stimulus, not good/bad.

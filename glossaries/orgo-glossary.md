# MCAT Organic Chemistry Glossary
*Created: 2026-07-22 | Focus: C/P section high-yield terms*

---

## Functional Groups

**Alkene** — C=C double bond; sp² hybridized carbons; planar around the double bond. Undergoes addition reactions (electrophilic or radical). MCAT: E/Z isomerism assigned by CIP priorities; Markovnikov rule governs addition of H–X (H adds to the carbon with more H's; X adds to the more substituted carbon).

**Alcohol** — R–OH. Classified as 1° (–CH₂OH), 2° (–CHOH–), or 3° (–C(OH)–). MCAT: 1° oxidizes → aldehyde → carboxylic acid; 2° oxidizes → ketone; 3° cannot be oxidized without C–C cleavage. Higher boiling point than analogous alkane due to H-bonding.

**Aldehyde** — R–CHO; carbonyl at chain terminus; sp² carbon. MCAT: More easily oxidized than ketone; Tollens' test (Ag⁺ → silver mirror) and Benedict's test both positive for aldehydes and reducing sugars. Forms hemiacetals and acetals with alcohols.

**Ketone** — R–CO–R′; carbonyl between two carbons. MCAT: Cannot be oxidized under normal conditions; Tollens' test negative. Reduced by NaBH₄ or LiAlH₄ to 2° alcohol.

**Carboxylic Acid** — R–COOH; pKa ≈ 4–5. Strong H-bond donor. MCAT: IR shows broad O–H stretch (~3200 cm⁻¹) and carbonyl stretch (~1710 cm⁻¹). Converted to ester by Fischer esterification (acid catalyst, reversible) or to amide by reaction with amine.

**Ester** — R–COOR′. MCAT: Saponification = base-catalyzed hydrolysis → carboxylate + alcohol (irreversible); acid-catalyzed hydrolysis is reversible. Phosphate esters form the backbone of DNA and RNA.

**Amide** — R–CONR₂. Least reactive acyl derivative because the N lone pair donates into C=O (resonance). MCAT: Peptide bonds are amides; require harsh conditions (strong acid/base + heat) for hydrolysis. IR carbonyl ~1680 cm⁻¹ (lower than other carbonyls due to resonance).

**Amine** — R–NH₂ (1°), R₂NH (2°), R₃N (3°). Basic due to N lone pair; conjugate acid pKa ~10. MCAT: Amines are nucleophiles and bases. Aromatic amines (e.g., aniline) are less basic because the lone pair is delocalized into the ring.

**Thiol** — R–SH. MCAT: Oxidized to disulfide bond (R–S–S–R); critical for protein tertiary structure (Cys–Cys bridges). Lower boiling point than analogous alcohol due to weaker H-bonding.

**Ether** — R–O–R′. Low reactivity; common solvent. MCAT: No exchangeable proton → no IR O–H stretch; resistant to most reagents. THF and diethyl ether are typical lab solvents.

**Phenol** — Aromatic ring with –OH directly attached. pKa ≈ 10. MCAT: More acidic than alcohols (pKa ~16) but less acidic than carboxylic acids (pKa ~5); conjugate base (phenoxide) stabilized by resonance with the ring.

**Nitrile** — R–C≡N. MCAT: Hydrolysis → carboxylic acid; reduction → amine. Triple bond stretch visible by IR (~2200 cm⁻¹).

## Reactions

**SN2** — One-step, backside attack by nucleophile; inversion of configuration at the reaction center. Rate = k[Nu][substrate]. MCAT: Favored by 1° substrates, strong/unhindered nucleophiles, polar aprotic solvents (DMSO, DMF, acetone). 3° substrates give only elimination under SN2 conditions.

**SN1** — Two-step; carbocation intermediate; racemization at reaction center. Rate = k[substrate] only. MCAT: Favored by 3° (or resonance-stabilized) substrates, weak nucleophiles, polar protic solvents (H₂O, ROH). Carbocation rearrangements (hydride/methyl shifts) possible. Competes with E1.

**E2** — One-step elimination; requires anti-periplanar arrangement of H and leaving group. MCAT: Favored by strong bulky bases (t-BuOK), 2° or 3° substrates. Zaitsev product = more substituted alkene; Hofmann product = less substituted (with bulky base).

**E1** — Two-step elimination; carbocation intermediate; same conditions as SN1. MCAT: Competes with SN1 at higher temperatures. Gives Zaitsev product. No specific anti-periplanar requirement.

**Markovnikov Addition** — Electrophilic addition to alkene: H⁺ adds to the carbon bearing more H's; electrophile adds to the more substituted carbon (via more stable carbocation). MCAT: HBr without peroxide = Markovnikov (Br on more substituted carbon). HBr with peroxide = anti-Markovnikov via radical mechanism (Br on less substituted carbon).

**Halogenation of Alkene** — Br₂ adds across alkene via bromonium ion intermediate; anti addition (the two Br atoms end up on opposite faces). MCAT: Anti addition gives specific diastereomers; racemic mixture for symmetric substrates.

**Aldol Condensation** — Enolate of one carbonyl attacks C=O of another → β-hydroxy carbonyl (aldol addition); dehydration → α,β-unsaturated carbonyl. MCAT: Base-catalyzed; retroaldol reaction is catalyzed by aldolase in glycolysis (step 4: fructose-1,6-bisphosphate → DHAP + G3P).

**Claisen Condensation** — Ester enolate attacks another ester → β-ketoester. MCAT: Requires strong alkoxide base. Biosynthetic analog: thioester condensation of acetyl-CoA units during fatty acid synthesis.

**Fischer Esterification** — Acid-catalyzed: carboxylic acid + alcohol ⇌ ester + H₂O. Reversible; driven toward ester by excess alcohol or removing water. MCAT: Mechanism involves protonation of carbonyl → nucleophilic attack by alcohol → loss of water.

**Saponification** — Base hydrolysis of ester → carboxylate salt + alcohol; irreversible. MCAT: Used in soap production (triacylglycerol + NaOH → fatty acid salts + glycerol). Acid hydrolysis of esters is reversible; base hydrolysis is not (carboxylate is a poor electrophile).

**Grignard Reaction** — R–MgBr adds to carbonyls; strong C-nucleophile. MCAT: Cannot tolerate protic solvents (water destroys the reagent). Adds to aldehyde → 2° alcohol; ketone → 3° alcohol; CO₂ → carboxylic acid (after workup).

**Diels-Alder** — [4+2] cycloaddition; conjugated diene (must be s-cis) + dienophile (electron-poor alkene); syn addition. MCAT: Product is a cyclohexene; endo product is kinetically favored. Diene must adopt s-cis conformation — ring-locked s-trans dienes cannot react.

**Reduction: NaBH₄ vs. LiAlH₄** — NaBH₄ (mild): reduces only aldehydes and ketones. LiAlH₄ (strong): reduces carboxylic acids, esters, amides, aldehydes, and ketones. MCAT: Neither reagent reduces isolated C=C bonds. LiAlH₄ reacts violently with water; must be used in dry ether.

**Oxidation Levels of Carbon** — Most reduced → most oxidized: alkane → alcohol → aldehyde/ketone → carboxylic acid. MCAT: Each gain of O or loss of H = oxidation; adding H or removing O = reduction. PCC (mild): 1° alcohol → aldehyde (stops there), 2° → ketone. KMnO₄ (strong, acidic): 1° alcohol → carboxylic acid, 2° → ketone, cleaves alkenes.

## Stereochemistry

**Chiral Center** — sp³ carbon bonded to 4 different substituents. MCAT: Number of possible stereoisomers = 2ⁿ where n = number of chiral centers (minus any meso compounds).

**R/S Assignment** — Assign CIP priority 1→4 by atomic number. If 1→2→3 clockwise with #4 away from viewer = R; counterclockwise = S. MCAT: If #4 group points toward you, swap the result. All L-amino acids are S configuration except Cys (R, due to sulfur's high atomic number).

**Enantiomers** — Non-superimposable mirror images; same physical/chemical properties except optical rotation sign and reaction rates with chiral reagents. MCAT: Racemic mixture (50:50) is optically inactive. SN1 reactions via achiral carbocation produce racemic mixtures.

**Diastereomers** — Stereoisomers that are NOT mirror images; differ at some but not all stereocenters. Have different physical properties. MCAT: Epimers differ at one of many stereocenters; anomers (α/β) differ at anomeric C of cyclic sugars; cis/trans alkene isomers are diastereomers.

**Meso Compound** — Has stereocenters but an internal plane of symmetry → optically inactive overall. MCAT: Classic: meso tartaric acid (2R,3S). A meso compound is its own mirror image (superimposable).

**E/Z Isomers** — Restricted rotation around C=C. E = opposite high-priority groups; Z = same side. MCAT: Assigned by CIP priorities on each sp² carbon. These are diastereomers (different physical properties).

## Spectroscopy

**IR Spectroscopy** — Identifies functional groups by bond vibration. MCAT: Key stretches to know: broad O–H ~3200–3550 cm⁻¹; N–H ~3300–3500 cm⁻¹; C≡N ~2200 cm⁻¹; C=O ~1670–1735 cm⁻¹ (aldehyde ~1730, ketone ~1715, carboxylic acid ~1710, ester ~1735, amide ~1680); C–H ~2850–3000 cm⁻¹.

**¹H NMR** — Chemical shift (δ) reflects electron density around proton. MCAT: Deshielded (downfield, higher δ): aldehyde CHO ~9–10 ppm; aromatic ~6.5–8 ppm; vinyl ~4.5–6 ppm; adjacent to O/N ~3–5 ppm. Shielded (upfield): alkyl ~0.5–3 ppm. n+1 splitting rule: n neighboring H's split signal into n+1 peaks. Integration gives relative number of H's.

**Mass Spectrometry** — M⁺ = molecular ion (molecular weight). MCAT: Common losses: 15 = CH₃, 17 = OH, 29 = CHO, 31 = OCH₃. Base peak = most intense fragment. α-cleavage (bond adjacent to heteroatom or carbonyl) is the most common fragmentation.

**UV-Vis** — Detects conjugated π systems. MCAT: More conjugation → longer wavelength absorption (bathochromic/red shift). DNA absorbs at 260 nm; proteins at 280 nm (Tyr, Trp, Phe residues).

## Separations

**TLC** — Silica stationary phase (polar); organic mobile phase. MCAT: Rf = distance compound traveled ÷ distance solvent traveled. More polar compound = lower Rf (adheres more tightly to silica). Used to monitor reaction progress.

**Column Chromatography** — Normal phase: polar stationary (silica); nonpolar compounds elute first. MCAT: Reverse phase (C18): polar compounds elute first. Ion-exchange chromatography: separates by charge (used in amino acid analysis, protein purification).

**Distillation** — Separates by boiling point. MCAT: Simple = large bp difference (>25°C); fractional = small difference. Azeotrope = mixture that distills at fixed composition without separation (cannot be purified by distillation alone).

**Liquid-Liquid Extraction** — Separates based on differential solubility in two immiscible layers. MCAT: Amines extracted into aqueous acid (ammonium salt); carboxylic acids extracted into aqueous base (carboxylate salt); neutral compounds stay in organic layer.

**Recrystallization** — Dissolve in hot solvent; cool slowly; purified compound crystallizes; impurities stay in solution. MCAT: Works when the compound and impurities have significantly different solubility profiles at different temperatures.

## Aromatic Chemistry

**Aromaticity** — Must be planar, cyclic, fully conjugated, satisfy Hückel's rule: 4n+2 π electrons (n = 0,1,2...). MCAT: Benzene (6 π e⁻), pyrrole (6 π e⁻ including N lone pair), pyridine (6 π e⁻, N lone pair NOT in ring) are all aromatic. Antiaromatic = 4n π electrons (cyclobutadiene = 4 π e⁻).

**EAS (Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution)** — Aromatic ring acts as nucleophile to attack an electrophile. MCAT: Activating groups (–OH, –OR, –NR₂, alkyl): increase e⁻ density → faster EAS → ortho/para directors. Deactivating groups (–NO₂, –CN, –CF₃, –COOH): withdraw e⁻ density → slower EAS → meta directors. Halogens: deactivating but ortho/para directors (lone pair donation overrides inductive withdrawal for position).

**Pyridine vs. Pyrrole** — Both aromatic. MCAT: Pyridine N lone pair not part of ring (sp² N points outside ring) → pyridine IS a base (pKa of conjugate acid ~5). Pyrrole N lone pair IS part of ring (needed for aromaticity) → pyrrole is NOT basic (lone pair unavailable for protonation).

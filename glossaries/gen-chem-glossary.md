# MCAT General Chemistry Glossary
*Created: 2026-07-22 | Focus: C/P section high-yield terms*

---

## Atomic Structure

**Quantum Numbers** — n (principal): energy level (1, 2, 3...); l (azimuthal): subshell shape (0=s, 1=p, 2=d, 3=f); mₗ (magnetic): orbital orientation (−l to +l); mₛ (spin): +½ or −½. MCAT: Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers. Each orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons. s=1 orbital; p=3 orbitals; d=5 orbitals; f=7 orbitals.

**Electron Configuration** — Fill lowest energy orbitals first (Aufbau); Hund's rule: maximize unpaired (parallel) spins in degenerate orbitals before pairing; Pauli: max 2 e⁻ per orbital with opposite spins. MCAT: Exceptions: Cr = [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ (half-filled d is more stable than 3d⁴4s²); Cu = [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹ (fully-filled d more stable). Valence electrons are in the outermost shell and determine reactivity.

**Periodic Trends** — Across a period (left → right): atomic radius ↓ (more protons pull electrons in), ionization energy ↑, electron affinity ↑ (more negative), electronegativity ↑. Down a group: atomic radius ↑, IE ↓, EA ↓, electronegativity ↓. MCAT: Exceptions: IE of N > O (N has stable half-filled 2p; O has paired 2p electrons that repel); IE of Mg > Al (Mg has filled 2s subshell). F has highest electronegativity (3.98); Cs lowest. F has highest EA magnitude.

## Chemical Bonding

**Bond Types** — Ionic: large electronegativity difference (>1.7); complete electron transfer; forms ordered crystal lattice; high melting points. Covalent: small ΔEN (<1.7); electron sharing (polar covalent if 0–1.7 ΔEN). MCAT: Polar covalent bonds create dipole moments; molecules may be nonpolar overall if symmetric (CO₂, BF₃, CCl₄). Bond polarity ≠ molecule polarity. Metallic: delocalized electrons in metal lattice → conductivity.

**Formal Charge** — FC = (valence e⁻) − (lone pair e⁻) − ½(bonding e⁻). MCAT: Best Lewis structure: minimize formal charges; put negative FC on more electronegative atom; prefer formal charges of 0. Used to identify the most representative resonance structure.

**VSEPR (Molecular Geometry)** — Electron pairs (bonding + lone) repel each other; geometry minimizes repulsion. MCAT: 2 pairs: linear (180°); 3 bonding, 0 lone: trigonal planar (120°); 4 pairs, 0 lone: tetrahedral (109.5°); 4 pairs, 1 lone: trigonal pyramidal (107°); 4 pairs, 2 lone: bent (104.5°). Lone pairs take up more angular space than bonding pairs → compress bond angles.

**Hybridization** — sp: linear (2 regions of e⁻ density, 180°; alkynes, CO₂, BeCl₂); sp²: trigonal planar (3 regions, 120°; alkenes, carbonyls, benzene, BF₃); sp³: tetrahedral (4 regions, 109.5°; alkanes, water, ammonia, NF₃). MCAT: Number of hybrid orbitals = number of regions of electron density (count σ bonds + lone pairs, NOT π bonds separately).

**Intermolecular Forces** — Rank of strength: H-bonds > dipole-dipole > London dispersion. H-bonds: require H bonded to F, O, or N; interact with lone pair on F, O, or N of another molecule. London/van der Waals: present in ALL molecules; increase with larger molecular weight and surface area; branched molecules have lower bp than linear isomers. MCAT: H-bonds explain water's anomalously high bp (100°C), surface tension, and density anomaly (solid floats). Boiling point correlates with intermolecular force strength.

## Thermodynamics

**Enthalpy (ΔH)** — Heat exchange at constant pressure. Exothermic: ΔH < 0 (releases heat, products more stable). Endothermic: ΔH > 0 (absorbs heat). MCAT: Bond breaking requires energy (+ΔH); bond forming releases energy (−ΔH). ΔH°rxn = ΣΔH°f(products) − ΣΔH°f(reactants). Hess's law: ΔH is a state function (path-independent); reactions can be added algebraically.

**Entropy (ΔS)** — Measure of disorder/energy dispersal. MCAT: Entropy increases: gas produced from liquid/solid; more moles of gas produced; dissolution in solution (usually); heating; complex molecule → simpler fragments. ΔS°rxn = ΣS°(products) − ΣS°(reactants). S(gas) >> S(liquid) > S(solid).

**Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG)** — ΔG = ΔH − TΔS; negative ΔG = spontaneous; positive = non-spontaneous; zero = equilibrium. MCAT: Spontaneity matrix: ΔH(−), ΔS(+) → always spontaneous; ΔH(+), ΔS(−) → never spontaneous; ΔH(−), ΔS(−) → spontaneous only at low T; ΔH(+), ΔS(+) → spontaneous only at high T. Temperature crossover: T = ΔH/ΔS (where ΔG = 0 = sign change).

**ΔG and K Relationship** — ΔG° = −RT ln K; ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q. MCAT: ΔG° < 0 → K > 1 (products favored at equilibrium). ΔG° > 0 → K < 1 (reactants favored). At equilibrium, ΔG = 0 (not ΔG°). ΔG° is fixed for a given reaction at a given temperature; ΔG changes with Q.

**Heat Capacity and Phase Changes** — q = mcΔT; q = mΔH_phase (during phase transition, temperature doesn't change). MCAT: Water: c = 4.18 J/(g·°C); ΔH_fusion = 334 J/g; ΔH_vaporization = 2260 J/g. Heating curve: flat regions = phase changes; sloped regions = temperature change in a single phase. Water's high specific heat moderates temperature changes in biological systems.

## Kinetics

**Rate Law** — rate = k[A]^m[B]^n; m and n are experimentally determined reaction orders (NOT from stoichiometric coefficients). MCAT: Overall order = m+n. Zero order: rate = k (rate independent of [A]; linear [A] vs. time). First order: rate = k[A]; half-life constant: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k (logarithmic [A] vs. time). Second order: rate = k[A]². Units of k depend on overall order.

**Arrhenius Equation** — k = Ae^(−Ea/RT); k increases exponentially with T. MCAT: Increasing temperature by 10°C approximately doubles the reaction rate for many reactions. Catalysts lower Ea → increase k → faster reaction; they do NOT change ΔH, ΔG, ΔG°, or K. Enzyme kinetics: same concept as chemical catalysis.

**Rate-Determining Step** — The slowest elementary step; controls the overall reaction rate. MCAT: The rate law for the overall reaction reflects the steps up to and including the rate-determining step. Intermediates formed before the slow step can be substituted out using a pre-equilibrium assumption. Increasing concentration of a substance that appears after the slow step has no effect on rate.

## Equilibrium

**Equilibrium Constant (K)** — K = [products]^stoich / [reactants]^stoich; pure solids and pure liquids are omitted (activity = 1); only dissolved species (aqueous) and gases are included. MCAT: Large K (>>1) → equilibrium lies toward products. Small K (<<1) → reactants favored. K changes only with temperature; unaffected by concentration, pressure, or catalysts.

**Reaction Quotient (Q)** — Same mathematical form as K but using non-equilibrium concentrations/pressures. MCAT: Q < K → system has too many reactants → shifts right (forward reaction) to reach equilibrium. Q > K → shifts left (reverse reaction). Q = K → at equilibrium.

**Le Chatelier's Principle** — System at equilibrium opposes any imposed stress by shifting in the direction that partially relieves the stress. MCAT: Stress analysis: add reactant → shift right; remove product → shift right; increase pressure → shift toward fewer moles of gas; increase temperature → shifts in endothermic direction. Catalyst: speeds up approach to equilibrium but does NOT shift the position (same K).

**Kp vs. Kc** — Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn_gas (Δn_gas = moles of gaseous products − moles of gaseous reactants). MCAT: If Δn_gas = 0 → Kp = Kc. For gas-phase reactions, Kp is often more relevant (partial pressures). Kc uses molar concentrations.

## Acid-Base Chemistry

**Brønsted-Lowry Acids and Bases** — Acid = proton (H⁺) donor; base = proton acceptor. Every acid-base reaction involves conjugate pairs. MCAT: Stronger acid → weaker conjugate base (more stable anion). Amphoteric species act as both acid and base (H₂O, HCO₃⁻, HPO₄²⁻, H₂PO₄⁻). Lewis acid = electron pair acceptor (broader category; e.g., BF₃, Fe³⁺, Al³⁺).

**Ka, Kb, and Kw** — Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1×10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C; pH + pOH = 14 at 25°C. For conjugate acid-base pair: Ka × Kb = Kw; pKa + pKb = 14. MCAT: Strong acids (HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄) and strong bases (NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂) fully dissociate. Weak acids have Ka << 1 and meaningful pKa values (2–12 range for MCAT purposes).

**pH Calculations** — Strong acid: pH = −log[HA] (complete dissociation). Weak acid: pH = ½(pKa − log[HA]) (approximate, when Ka << 1 and [HA] >> Ka). Weak base: pOH = ½(pKb − log[B]); pH = 14 − pOH. MCAT: Memorize: pH = −log[H⁺]; each 10-fold change in [H⁺] = 1 pH unit. Buffer: pH ≈ pKa when [A⁻] ≈ [HA].

**Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation** — pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]). MCAT: Buffer capacity is greatest at pH = pKa (ratio [A⁻]/[HA] = 1). Effective buffering range: pH = pKa ± 1. Each full pH unit from pKa = 10-fold ratio change. Clinical relevance: bicarbonate buffer (pKa₁ = 6.1 for H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻) maintains blood pH; phosphate buffer (pKa₂ = 7.2) is important intracellularly.

**Titration** — Addition of titrant (known concentration) to analyte to determine unknown concentration. MCAT: Strong acid/strong base: equivalence point pH = 7 (neutral salt solution). Weak acid/strong base: equivalence point pH > 7 (basic; conjugate base is a weak base). Weak base/strong acid: equivalence point pH < 7. Half-equivalence point: exactly half the acid neutralized → [A⁻] = [HA] → pH = pKa. Indicator changes color at its own pKa ± 1.

**Polyprotic Acids** — Multiple ionizable protons; each ionization has its own Ka (pKa₁ < pKa₂ < pKa₃). MCAT: Phosphoric acid: pKa₁ ≈ 2.1, pKa₂ ≈ 7.2, pKa₃ ≈ 12.4 (important for biological buffers). Carbonic acid: pKa₁ ≈ 6.4, pKa₂ ≈ 10.3 (blood buffer). Each successive pKa is larger because removing proton from increasingly negative species is harder.

## Electrochemistry

**Oxidation States** — Rules (priority order): F always −1; O usually −2 (except peroxides −1, OF₂ +2); H is +1 with nonmetals (−1 in metal hydrides); sum of OS = overall charge of molecule/ion. MCAT: OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons); Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). The substance oxidized = reducing agent; substance reduced = oxidizing agent.

**Standard Reduction Potential (E°)** — Measured at standard conditions (25°C, 1 M, 1 atm) vs. standard hydrogen electrode (SHE, E° = 0 by definition). MCAT: More positive E° = greater tendency to be reduced = better oxidizing agent (sits higher in reduction potential table). E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode. Spontaneous cell (galvanic): E°cell > 0.

**Galvanic Cell** — Spontaneous electrochemical cell; converts chemical energy → electrical energy; ΔG < 0; E°cell > 0. MCAT: Anode = oxidation (loses electrons; negative electrode in galvanic cell); cathode = reduction (gains electrons; positive electrode). Electrons flow through external wire from anode to cathode. Salt bridge maintains electrical neutrality (anions migrate toward anode, cations toward cathode). Galvanic cell produces current; electrolytic cell consumes current.

**Electrolytic Cell** — Non-spontaneous; requires external power source to drive reaction; ΔG > 0; E°applied needed. MCAT: AnOx RedCat holds in BOTH galvanic AND electrolytic cells (anode = oxidation; cathode = reduction, regardless of cell type). Used for electroplating, aluminum production, electrolysis of water. At cathode: reduction → metal deposits. Minimum required voltage = E°cell for the reverse reaction.

**Nernst Equation and ΔG** — ΔG° = −nFE°cell (F = 96,485 C/mol e⁻ ≈ 96,500). E = E° − (RT/nF)ln Q = E° − (0.0592/n)log Q at 25°C. MCAT: ΔG° = −nFE° links thermodynamics and electrochemistry. At equilibrium: E = 0; Q = K → allows calculating K from E°cell. If E°cell > 0 → ΔG° < 0 → spontaneous → K > 1.

**Faraday's Law of Electrolysis** — Moles of product = (I × t)/(n × F); where I = current (amps), t = time (seconds), n = electrons per ion, F = 96,500 C/mol. MCAT: 1 ampere = 1 coulomb/second; charge = I × t. Calculate moles of e⁻ transferred → divide by electrons per atom to get moles of product → multiply by molar mass for grams.

## Gases & Solutions

**Ideal Gas Law** — PV = nRT; R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K) or 8.314 J/(mol·K). MCAT: STP = 0°C and 1 atm → 1 mol ideal gas = 22.4 L. Dalton's law of partial pressures: P_total = ΣP_i; P_i = X_i × P_total (X_i = mole fraction). Graham's law: r₁/r₂ = √(M₂/M₁); lighter gas effuses faster. Real gas deviation: at high P (molecules occupy real volume → PV > nRT) or low T (intermolecular attractions → PV < nRT).

**Concentration Units** — Molarity (M) = mol solute/L solution. Molality (m) = mol solute/kg solvent. Mole fraction (X) = mol component/total mol. MCAT: Dilution: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂. Percent by mass, volume, or mass/volume are also used. Molality is temperature-independent (used for colligative properties); molarity changes with T.

**Colligative Properties** — Depend only on number of dissolved particles (not identity). MCAT: Van't Hoff factor i: strong electrolyte NaCl → i = 2 (two ions); CaCl₂ → i = 3; non-electrolytes → i = 1. Boiling point elevation: ΔT_b = iK_b·m. Freezing point depression: ΔT_f = iK_f·m. Osmotic pressure: Π = iMRT. Vapor pressure lowering: Raoult's law: P_solution = X_solvent × P°_solvent (solute lowers vapor pressure of solvent). These principles underlie IV fluid tonicity calculations.

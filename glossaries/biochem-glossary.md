## Amino Acids & Proteins

**Amino Acid Classes** — Nonpolar/hydrophobic: Gly, Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Pro, Phe, Met, Trp. Polar uncharged: Ser, Thr, Cys, Tyr, Asn, Gln. Positively charged at pH 7: Lys (+), Arg (+), His (partial +). Negatively charged at pH 7: Asp (−), Glu (−). MCAT: Charged AAs are hydrophilic → found on protein surface. Aromatic AAs (Phe, Trp, Tyr) absorb UV at 280 nm. Cys forms disulfide bonds. Pro creates β-turns and disrupts α-helices (no N–H for H-bonding).

**Isoelectric Point (pI)** — pH at which an amino acid has no net charge (zwitterion form). MCAT: For neutral AAs: pI = (pKa₁ + pKa₂)/2. For basic AAs (Lys, Arg, His): pI shifted to higher pH. For acidic AAs (Asp, Glu): pI shifted to lower pH. At pI, the AA has minimum solubility and does not migrate in an electric field (used in isoelectric focusing).

**Primary Structure** — Sequence of amino acids linked by peptide bonds (amide bonds). MCAT: Peptide bonds are rigid and planar due to partial double bond character (resonance delocalization). Rotation occurs around Cα bonds (φ and ψ angles). Sequence entirely determines all higher-order structure.

**Secondary Structure** — Local regular patterns. α-helix: right-handed; H-bond between C=O of residue n and N–H of residue n+4; 3.6 residues per turn; side chains point outward. β-sheet: H-bonds between extended strands (parallel or antiparallel). MCAT: Pro disrupts α-helices (N in ring has no H for H-bonding); Gly is most flexible (smallest side chain, found at turns).

**Tertiary Structure** — 3D folding of a single polypeptide; stabilized by hydrophobic interactions (dominant driving force), disulfide bonds (covalent), H-bonds, and salt bridges. MCAT: Denaturation disrupts non-covalent interactions (and disulfide bonds if a reducing agent is used) without breaking peptide bonds. Denaturing agents: heat, extreme pH, detergents (SDS), urea/guanidinium, heavy metals.

**Quaternary Structure** — Assembly of multiple polypeptide subunits. MCAT: Hemoglobin (2α + 2β subunits) shows cooperativity: sigmoidal O₂ dissociation curve. Myoglobin (single chain): hyperbolic curve. Bohr effect: CO₂ and H⁺ shift Hb curve right (↓O₂ affinity in metabolically active tissue). 2,3-BPG also shifts curve right. Fetal Hb (HbF) has higher O₂ affinity than adult HbA (shifts curve left).

## Enzyme Kinetics

**Michaelis-Menten Kinetics** — v = Vmax[S]/(Km + [S]). MCAT: At [S] >> Km → 0th order (v ≈ Vmax, rate independent of [S]). At [S] << Km → 1st order (v proportional to [S]). When [S] = Km → v = Vmax/2 (definition of Km).

**Km** — Michaelis constant = [S] at half-maximal velocity. Approximates substrate affinity for most enzymes. MCAT: Lower Km = tighter binding = higher affinity. Competitive inhibition increases apparent Km; noncompetitive does not change Km; uncompetitive decreases Km.

**Vmax** — Maximum rate when all enzyme active sites are saturated; reflects enzyme abundance and catalytic rate constant (k_cat). MCAT: Competitive inhibition does NOT change Vmax (can be overcome by excess substrate). Noncompetitive and irreversible inhibitors decrease Vmax.

**Competitive Inhibition** — Inhibitor resembles substrate; binds active site reversibly. MCAT: Increases apparent Km; Vmax unchanged. Lineweaver-Burk (double reciprocal plot): lines intersect on y-axis (same Vmax; y-intercept = 1/Vmax); x-intercept shifts toward origin (apparent Km changes).

**Noncompetitive Inhibition** — Inhibitor binds allosteric site equally well whether substrate is present or not. MCAT: Decreases Vmax; Km unchanged. Lineweaver-Burk: lines intersect on x-axis (same Km; x-intercept = −1/Km); y-intercept shifts (changed Vmax).

**Uncompetitive Inhibition** — Inhibitor binds only the enzyme-substrate complex. MCAT: Decreases both Km AND Vmax proportionally. Lineweaver-Burk: parallel lines (same slope but different intercepts). Rare on MCAT but tested.

**Allosteric Regulation** — Effector binds a site other than the active site; changes enzyme conformation. MCAT: Positive allosteric effectors lower Km or increase Vmax. Negative effectors do opposite. Key allosteric enzymes are at metabolic branch points (e.g., PFK-1 in glycolysis: inhibited by ATP/citrate; activated by AMP/ADP/fructose-2,6-bisphosphate).

**Cofactors vs. Coenzymes** — Cofactors: inorganic metal ions (Fe²⁺/³⁺, Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺) required for enzyme function. Coenzymes: organic molecules that carry groups between reactions. MCAT: Vitamins are coenzyme precursors: B₃ (niacin) → NAD⁺; B₂ (riboflavin) → FAD; B₁ (thiamine) → TPP; B₅ (pantothenic acid) → CoA; biotin → carboxylase cofactor; B₆ (pyridoxal phosphate) → amino acid metabolism; B₁₂ → methyl transfers.

**Feedback Inhibition** — End product of a pathway inhibits an early enzyme. MCAT: Classic: isoleucine inhibits threonine deaminase (first step in its own synthesis). Usually allosteric and reversible. Prevents overproduction of intermediates and products.

## Carbohydrate Metabolism

**Glycolysis** — Glucose (6C) → 2 pyruvate (3C) in cytoplasm. Net yield: 2 ATP, 2 NADH. MCAT: Investment phase uses 2 ATP (steps 1 and 3); payoff phase produces 4 ATP + 2 NADH. Key regulated enzymes: hexokinase (step 1), PFK-1 (step 3, rate-limiting; inhibited by ATP/citrate; activated by AMP/ADP/F-2,6-BP), pyruvate kinase (step 10). Irreversible steps 1, 3, and 10 are bypassed in gluconeogenesis.

**Pyruvate Dehydrogenase Complex (PDC)** — Pyruvate → acetyl-CoA + CO₂ + NADH; in mitochondrial matrix. MCAT: Irreversible; bridges glycolysis to TCA cycle. Inhibited by NADH and acetyl-CoA (products); activated by CoA, NAD⁺, AMP, pyruvate. Requires five cofactors: TPP, lipoic acid, CoA, FAD, NAD⁺.

**TCA Cycle (Krebs Cycle)** — Acetyl-CoA (2C) + oxaloacetate (4C) → citrate (6C); two carbons lost as CO₂; OAA regenerated. Per acetyl-CoA turn: 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 GTP, 2 CO₂. MCAT: Location: mitochondrial matrix. Key regulated enzymes: isocitrate dehydrogenase (inhibited by NADH/ATP; activated by ADP/Ca²⁺); α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase (similar). OAA replenished by pyruvate carboxylase (anaplerotic reaction): pyruvate + CO₂ → OAA (requires biotin, ATP).

**Oxidative Phosphorylation (ETC)** — NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to ETC in inner mitochondrial membrane → proton gradient across membrane → ATP synthase (Complex V) produces ATP. MCAT: NADH enters at Complex I (yields ~2.5 ATP); FADH₂ at Complex II (yields ~1.5 ATP). Total per glucose oxidation: ~30–32 ATP. Inhibitors: cyanide (Complex IV); rotenone (Complex I); oligomycin (ATP synthase). Uncouplers (e.g., DNP): dissipate proton gradient as heat; no ATP produced but O₂ consumption increases.

**Gluconeogenesis** — Synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors (pyruvate, lactate, glucogenic amino acids, glycerol) in liver and kidney cortex. MCAT: Requires 6 ATP equivalents per glucose. Bypasses three irreversible glycolytic steps: pyruvate carboxylase + PEPCK (bypass pyruvate kinase); fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (bypass PFK-1); glucose-6-phosphatase (bypass hexokinase; liver-specific). Stimulated by glucagon and cortisol during fasting.

**Glycogen Metabolism** — Glycogenesis: glucose → glycogen in liver (blood glucose homeostasis) and muscle (local fuel). Glycogenolysis: glycogen → glucose-1-phosphate via glycogen phosphorylase. MCAT: Hormonal control via cAMP cascade: glucagon/epinephrine → PKA → phosphorylates glycogen phosphorylase (activates) and glycogen synthase (inhibits). Insulin → activates glycogen synthase (promotes storage).

**Pentose Phosphate Pathway (PPP)** — G6P → NADPH + ribose-5-phosphate; in cytoplasm. MCAT: NADPH is used for fatty acid synthesis and to regenerate reduced glutathione (protects RBCs from oxidative damage). Ribose-5-phosphate for nucleotide synthesis. G6PD deficiency → impaired PPP → RBC hemolysis under oxidative stress (e.g., primaquine, fava beans).

## Lipid Metabolism

**Beta-Oxidation (Fatty Acid Oxidation)** — Fatty acids broken down in mitochondrial matrix; each round removes 2C as acetyl-CoA and produces 1 NADH + 1 FADH₂. MCAT: Fatty acids activated to acyl-CoA in cytoplasm; transported into mitochondria via carnitine shuttle (rate-limiting step; inhibited by malonyl-CoA). Odd-chain fatty acids produce propionyl-CoA in the last round (enters TCA as succinyl-CoA; requires vitamin B₁₂).

**Fatty Acid Synthesis** — Builds fatty acids from acetyl-CoA in cytoplasm; requires NADPH. MCAT: Acetyl-CoA must exit mitochondria as citrate (citrate shuttle). Rate-limiting enzyme: acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC; requires biotin; produces malonyl-CoA; inhibited by palmitoyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA inhibits carnitine shuttle → prevents simultaneous synthesis and oxidation). Fatty acid synthase (FAS) elongates chain by 2C per cycle. Occurs primarily in well-fed/high-insulin state.

**Ketone Bodies** — Acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate, acetone; produced in liver mitochondria from excess acetyl-CoA during fasting, starvation, or uncontrolled type 1 diabetes. MCAT: Brain and heart use ketone bodies when glucose is limited. High ketone bodies → metabolic acidosis (ketoacidosis). Acetone is volatile → fruity breath odor. The liver produces ketone bodies but cannot use them (lacks β-ketoacyl-CoA transferase/thiophorase).

## Nucleic Acids & Gene Expression

**DNA Replication** — Semi-conservative; occurs in S phase; synthesis always 5'→3'. MCAT: Key enzymes: helicase (unwinds), SSBPs (stabilize single strands), topoisomerase (relieves torsional strain ahead of fork; target of fluoroquinolones), primase (synthesizes RNA primer), DNA Pol III (E. coli; elongates 5'→3'), DNA Pol I (removes RNA primers), DNA ligase (seals nicks). Leading strand: continuous; lagging strand: discontinuous (Okazaki fragments). Telomerase (reverse transcriptase with RNA template) extends telomeres in germline and cancer cells.

**Transcription** — DNA → mRNA; RNA polymerase reads template strand 3'→5' and synthesizes mRNA 5'→3'. No primer needed. MCAT: Promoter in eukaryotes: TATA box (~25 bp upstream of TSS). mRNA processing (eukaryotes only): 5' 7-methylguanosine cap (protects, aids ribosome binding), poly-A tail (stability, export), intron removal by spliceosome (snRNAs). Alternative splicing generates protein diversity from one gene.

**Translation** — mRNA → protein; occurs at ribosomes. Eukaryotes: 80S (60S + 40S); prokaryotes: 70S (50S + 30S). MCAT: Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases charge tRNAs (one per amino acid; maintain genetic code accuracy). AUG = start codon (Met in eukaryotes, fMet in prokaryotes). Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA (no tRNA; recognized by release factors). A site: incoming aminoacyl-tRNA; P site: growing peptide chain; E site: exiting uncharged tRNA. Antibiotic targets: aminoglycosides/streptomycin (30S; prokaryotes), erythromycin/chloramphenicol (50S; prokaryotes), cycloheximide (60S; eukaryotes).

**Mutations** — Changes in DNA sequence. MCAT: Silent (synonymous): codon change → same amino acid (genetic code degeneracy; 3rd position most variable). Missense: codon → different amino acid (e.g., HbS: Glu→Val in β-globin). Nonsense: codon → stop codon → truncated protein (usually severe). Frameshift: insertion or deletion of non-multiple-of-3 nucleotides → alters all downstream amino acids (usually most severe). Transition: purine→purine or pyrimidine→pyrimidine. Transversion: purine↔pyrimidine.

**DNA Repair** — MCAT: Nucleotide excision repair (NER): bulky lesions (thymine dimers from UV); defect = xeroderma pigmentosum. Base excision repair (BER): deamination, alkylation (single base damage). Mismatch repair (MMR): replication errors; defect = hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer (HNPCC/Lynch syndrome). Double-strand breaks: homologous recombination (accurate; uses sister chromatid) or NHEJ (error-prone; dominant in G1).

**Central Dogma** — DNA → RNA → Protein. MCAT: Exceptions: retroviruses use reverse transcriptase (RNA → DNA); prions are misfolded proteins that propagate without nucleic acid; ribozymes are catalytic RNAs. RNA can be template for RNA (RNA-dependent RNA polymerase in RNA viruses).

## Metabolic Regulation

**Insulin vs. Glucagon** — Insulin (secreted by β cells when blood glucose is high): promotes glucose uptake (GLUT4 in muscle/fat), glycolysis, glycogen synthesis, fatty acid synthesis; inhibits gluconeogenesis and lipolysis. Glucagon (secreted by α cells when blood glucose is low): promotes glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, ketogenesis; inhibits glycolysis and glycogen synthesis. MCAT: Insulin = anabolic/storage; glucagon = catabolic/mobilization. Both bind membrane receptors (peptide hormones).

**cAMP Second Messenger Pathway** — Glucagon/epinephrine → GPCR (Gs) → adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → PKA activation → phosphorylation of multiple targets. MCAT: PKA activates glycogen phosphorylase kinase → activates glycogen phosphorylase; PKA also phosphorylates and inactivates glycogen synthase. This pathway is rapid (seconds to minutes); steroid hormone effects require new protein synthesis (hours).

**Substrate-Level vs. Oxidative Phosphorylation** — Substrate-level: direct phosphate transfer in enzymatic reaction (glycolysis step 7: 1,3-BPG→3PG via PGK; step 10: PEP→pyruvate via PK; TCA: succinyl-CoA→succinate via succinyl-CoA synthetase, producing GTP). Oxidative: ATP from proton gradient via ETC. MCAT: Only substrate-level phosphorylation is possible under anaerobic conditions → fermentation in muscle (pyruvate → lactate) or yeast (pyruvate → ethanol + CO₂).

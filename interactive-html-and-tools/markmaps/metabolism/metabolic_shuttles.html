🔄 Malate-Aspartate Shuttle

Transfers NADH from cytoplasm to mitochondria

▼
Why it exists: NADH can't cross the inner mitochondrial membrane directly. This shuttle carries the reducing equivalents (electrons) into the matrix for oxidative phosphorylation.

Key molecules transported

  • Malate — carries electrons INTO mitochondria
  • Aspartate — carries amino group OUT of mitochondria
  • Oxaloacetate (OAA) — intermediate in both compartments
  • Glutamate — amino donor in cytoplasm
  • α-Ketoglutarate — accepts amino group in matrix

Enzymes involved

  • Malate dehydrogenase — cytoplasmic and mitochondrial versions
  • Aspartate aminotransferase (AST) — both compartments
  • Malate-α-ketoglutarate antiporter — membrane transporter
  • Glutamate-aspartate antiporter — membrane transporter

Mechanism (step by step)

  • 1Cytoplasmic NADH reduces OAA to malate (via malate DH)
  • 2Malate crosses inner membrane (antiporter with α-KG)
  • 3Matrix malate DH oxidizes malate back to OAA, generating NADH
  • 4OAA accepts amino from glutamate → aspartate (via AST)
  • 5Aspartate exits mitochondria (antiporter with glutamate)
  • 6Cytoplasmic AST converts aspartate back to OAA
Net result: Cytoplasmic NADH → Mitochondrial NADH (generates ~2.5 ATP)

Quick comparison

Energy yield

2.5 ATP per NADH

Location

Heart, liver, kidney

Speed

Slower but efficient

⚡ Glycerol-3-Phosphate Shuttle

Alternative NADH shuttle, faster but less ATP

▼
Why it exists: Faster than malate-aspartate but yields less ATP. Used when rapid glycolysis happens (muscle during intense exercise, brain).

Key molecules transported

  • Glycerol-3-phosphate (G3P) — carries electrons INTO mitochondria
  • Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) — acceptor in cytoplasm
  • FAD/FADH₂ — electron carrier in mitochondria

Enzymes involved

  • Glycerol-3-phosphate DH (cytoplasmic) — uses NAD⁺
  • Glycerol-3-phosphate DH (mitochondrial) — uses FAD, membrane-bound

Mechanism (simpler than malate)

  • 1Cytoplasmic NADH reduces DHAP to G3P (via cytoplasmic G3P DH)
  • 2G3P diffuses to outer mitochondrial membrane
  • 3Mitochondrial G3P DH (outer membrane) oxidizes G3P back to DHAP
  • 4Electrons go to FAD → FADH₂ (feeds into Complex II, not Complex I)
  • 5DHAP returns to cytoplasm to restart cycle
Net result: Cytoplasmic NADH → FADH₂ (generates only ~1.5 ATP, bypasses Complex I)

Quick comparison

Energy yield

1.5 ATP per NADH

Location

Brain, muscle, brown fat

Speed

Faster, irreversible

💧 Carnitine Shuttle

Transports long-chain fatty acids into mitochondria

▼
Why it exists: Activated fatty acids (acyl-CoA) can't cross the inner mitochondrial membrane. Carnitine acts as a carrier molecule for β-oxidation.

Key molecules transported

  • Acyl-CoA — activated fatty acid (in cytoplasm)
  • Acyl-carnitine — fatty acid attached to carnitine (crosses membrane)
  • Carnitine — the shuttle carrier itself
  • CoA — released in cytoplasm, regenerated in matrix

Enzymes involved

  • CPT I (Carnitine palmitoyltransferase I) — outer membrane
  • Carnitine-acylcarnitine translocase — inner membrane antiporter
  • CPT II (Carnitine palmitoyltransferase II) — inner membrane (matrix side)

Mechanism (three-step process)

  • 1CPT I on outer membrane transfers fatty acid from CoA to carnitine
  • 2Acyl-carnitine crosses inner membrane via translocase (antiporter with free carnitine)
  • 3CPT II on matrix side transfers fatty acid from carnitine back to CoA
  • 4Free carnitine returns to cytoplasm (via same translocase)
  • 5Acyl-CoA now in matrix undergoes β-oxidation
Regulation: CPT I is inhibited by malonyl-CoA (when making fatty acids, don't break them down)

Quick comparison

Substrate

Long-chain fatty acids

Regulation

Malonyl-CoA inhibits

Deficiency

Hypoglycemia, weakness

Memory tip: Malate and glycerol shuttles both move NADH equivalents (electrons). Carnitine is completely different — it moves fatty acids (carbon chains), not electrons. Don't mix them up!

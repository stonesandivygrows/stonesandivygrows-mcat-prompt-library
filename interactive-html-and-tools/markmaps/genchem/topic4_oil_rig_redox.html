Topic 4: OIL RIG & Electron Carriers

OIL RIG Mnemonic

  • Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons)
  • Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)

Alternatively: LEO the lion says GER

  • Lose Electrons = Oxidation
  • Gain Electrons = Reduction

The Confusion Explained

⚠️ Common Mistake with NADH

You wrote: "NADH losing an electron turning malate into oxaloacetate"

This is backwards! Here's what actually happens:

  1. Malate loses electrons → gets oxidized → becomes oxaloacetate
  2. NAD⁺ gains those electrons → gets reduced → becomes NADH

NADH is the PRODUCT, not the starting material. NADH doesn't "lose" anything in this reaction - it's what you GET when NAD⁺ gains electrons!

Terminology Breakdown

Key Terms:

Term Meaning
Substrate The molecule being chemically transformed (malate, pyruvate, etc.)
Carrier/Coenzyme The molecule that picks up or drops off electrons (NAD⁺/NADH, FAD/FADH₂)
Electron donor The molecule that LOSES electrons (gets oxidized)
Electron acceptor The molecule that GAINS electrons (gets reduced)
Oxidizing agent The molecule that CAUSES oxidation (accepts electrons). NAD⁺ is an oxidizing agent.
Reducing agent The molecule that CAUSES reduction (donates electrons). NADH is a reducing agent.

Example 1: Malate → Oxaloacetate (Krebs Cycle)

The Reaction

Malate + NAD⁺ → Oxaloacetate + NADH + H⁺

What's happening:

  1. Malate (has an -OH group) loses 2 hydrogens (2H = 2e⁻ + 2H⁺)
  2. The -OH becomes a C=O (ketone)
  3. This is OXIDATION - malate LOSES electrons
  4. NAD⁺ accepts those electrons (as H⁻ hydride, which is 2e⁻ + H⁺)
  5. NAD⁺ becomes NADH
  6. This is REDUCTION - NAD⁺ GAINS electrons

Who's who:

  • Malate = substrate, electron donor (gets oxidized)
  • NAD⁺ = carrier, electron acceptor, oxidizing agent (gets reduced)
  • Oxaloacetate = oxidized product
  • NADH = reduced product

Enzyme: Malate dehydrogenase (an oxidoreductase)

Example 2: Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA (Pyruvate Dehydrogenase)

The Reaction

Pyruvate + NAD⁺ + CoA-SH → Acetyl-CoA + NADH + CO₂

What's happening:

  1. Pyruvate is decarboxylated (loses CO₂)
  2. The remaining 2-carbon unit is oxidized
  3. This is OXIDATION - pyruvate LOSES electrons
  4. NAD⁺ accepts the electrons
  5. This is REDUCTION - NAD⁺ GAINS electrons → becomes NADH

Who's who:

  • Pyruvate = substrate, electron donor (gets oxidized)
  • NAD⁺ = carrier, electron acceptor (gets reduced)

What Happens Later: NADH at the Electron Transport Chain

Now NADH is the Donor!

When NADH arrives at Complex I of the ETC:

NADH + Complex I → NAD⁺ + Complex I⁻ (simplified)

  1. NADH LOSES electrons
  2. NADH is OXIDIZED back to NAD⁺
  3. Complex I GAINS those electrons
  4. Complex I is REDUCED
  5. Complex I then passes electrons to CoQ, and so on down the chain

Who's who NOW:

  • NADH = electron donor (gets oxidized)
  • Complex I = electron acceptor (gets reduced)
  • Eventually oxygen (O₂) is the final electron acceptor

The Carrier's Life Cycle

NAD⁺/NADH Cycle

KREBS CYCLE:
Malate --[loses e⁻]--> Oxaloacetate
        ↓
   NAD⁺ --[gains e⁻]--> NADH
   
NADH travels to ETC...

ELECTRON TRANSPORT CHAIN:
NADH --[loses e⁻]--> NAD⁺
        ↓
   Complex I --[gains e⁻]--> Complex I⁻
   
NAD⁺ returns to Krebs cycle...

Key insight: NAD⁺ and NADH are like rechargeable batteries:

  • NAD⁺ = empty battery (oxidized form), ready to accept electrons
  • NADH = charged battery (reduced form), ready to donate electrons

🔑 The Master Rule

In EVERY redox reaction:

  • One molecule is oxidized (loses electrons)
  • Another molecule is reduced (gains electrons)
  • They happen simultaneously - you can't oxidize without reducing something else

To track electrons:

  1. Identify what's being chemically transformed (substrate)
  2. Is it losing H atoms or gaining O atoms? → OXIDATION
  3. Is it gaining H atoms or losing O atoms? → REDUCTION
  4. Where do the electrons go? → That's being REDUCED
  5. Where do the electrons come from? → That's being OXIDIZED

Remember: The substrate (malate, pyruvate, etc.) is what you care about transforming. The carrier (NAD⁺/NADH) is just the delivery vehicle for electrons.

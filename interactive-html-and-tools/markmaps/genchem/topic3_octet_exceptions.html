Topic 3: Octet Rule Exceptions - The Anatomy

🔑 The Core Principle

Elements break the octet rule because of ORBITAL AVAILABILITY - not because they're "rule breakers"

The octet rule works for most elements, but physics limits or expands what's possible based on which orbitals exist.

The Four Types of Exceptions

1. Period 1 Elements: The Duet Rule

Elements: H, He

Why they break it:

  • Period 1 only has the 1s orbital
  • 1s can hold MAX 2 electrons
  • No 1p, 1d, or 1f orbitals exist (quantum mechanics won't allow it)
  • 8 electrons is physically impossible - there's nowhere to put them!

Examples:

  • H₂: Each H has 2 electrons (full 1s) = stable
  • He: Has 2 electrons = stable, noble gas

The anatomy: Not enough orbital space. They follow a "duet rule" instead.

2. Electron-Deficient Species: Incomplete Octets

Elements: Be, B (sometimes Al)

Why they break it:

  • These elements have FEW valence electrons (Be=2, B=3)
  • Yes, they have empty 2p orbitals available
  • But it costs MORE energy to force more bonds than the benefit gained
  • They're stable being "electron deficient"

Beryllium Example: BeCl₂

  • Be has 2 valence electrons
  • Forms 2 bonds to Cl atoms
  • Result: Only 4 electrons around Be
  • Could theoretically accept more, but doesn't need to for stability

Boron Example: BF₃

  • B has 3 valence electrons
  • Forms 3 bonds to F atoms
  • Result: Only 6 electrons around B
  • This makes BF₃ a strong Lewis acid (wants to accept electron pairs)
  • Can accept a lone pair to complete octet: BF₃ + F⁻ → BF₄⁻

The anatomy: Energetically favorable to stay electron-deficient rather than force more bonds.

3. Expanded Octets: Hypervalent Species

Elements: Period 3+ (P, S, Cl, Br, I, Xe)

Why they can expand:

  • Period 3+ elements have d orbitals available
  • For period 3: 3s, 3p, AND 3d orbitals can participate in bonding
  • Period 2 elements (C, N, O, F) CANNOT expand - no 2d orbitals exist!
  • With extra orbitals available, they can accommodate >8 electrons

Phosphorus Example: PCl₅

  • P has 5 valence electrons
  • Forms 5 bonds to Cl atoms
  • Uses: one 3s + three 3p + one 3d orbital
  • Result: 10 electrons around P
  • Geometry: Trigonal bipyramidal

Sulfur Example: SF₆

  • S has 6 valence electrons
  • Forms 6 bonds to F atoms
  • Uses: one 3s + three 3p + two 3d orbitals
  • Result: 12 electrons around S
  • Geometry: Octahedral

Chlorine Example: ClO₄⁻ (perchlorate)

  • Cl surrounded by 4 oxygen atoms
  • Can have 14 electrons in some resonance structures

The anatomy: d orbitals provide extra space for electrons beyond the octet.

Why can period 3 expand but not period 2?
Period 2 (C, N, O, F): Only have 2s and 2p orbitals. No 2d orbitals exist. Maximum 8 electrons.

Period 3 (P, S, Cl): Have 3s, 3p, AND 3d orbitals. The 3d orbitals provide extra bonding capacity. Can exceed 8 electrons.

4. Free Radicals: Odd Number of Electrons

Examples: NO, NO₂, ClO₂, O₂⁻ (superoxide)

Why they break it:

  • They have an ODD number of total electrons
  • Math: You can't pair an odd number!
  • One electron MUST remain unpaired
  • This unpaired electron makes them highly reactive

NO (nitric oxide) Example:

  • N has 5 valence electrons
  • O has 6 valence electrons
  • Total: 11 valence electrons
  • Can pair 10 of them, but 1 must remain alone
  • Result: Incomplete octet with a radical electron
  • Written as: •NO or NO•

NO₂ (nitrogen dioxide) Example:

  • N has 5 valence electrons
  • Two O atoms have 12 valence electrons
  • Total: 17 valence electrons (odd!)
  • One electron remains unpaired on the N

The anatomy: Odd electron count makes complete octets mathematically impossible.

Quick Reference Table

🔑 Remember:

  • The octet rule is a guideline, not a law
  • Exceptions are based on orbital availability and energy
  • Period 2 elements (C, N, O, F) can NEVER expand octets - no d orbitals!
  • Period 3+ elements CAN expand - they have d orbitals
  • Radicals are unavoidable when electron count is odd

Topic 5: Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS)

Part 1: How ROS Form Chemically

What Are ROS?

Reactive Oxygen Species = highly reactive molecules containing oxygen with unpaired electrons

Main types:

  • Superoxide (O₂•⁻) - oxygen with one extra electron (radical)
  • Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) - not a radical, but still reactive
  • Hydroxyl radical (•OH) - extremely reactive

Formation in Mitochondria

The normal ETC pathway:

NADH → Complex I → CoQ → Complex III → Cyt c → Complex IV → O₂ → H₂O

What's SUPPOSED to happen:

  1. Electrons travel through Complexes I, II, III, and IV
  2. O₂ arrives at Complex IV as the final electron acceptor
  3. Complex IV carefully adds 4 electrons to O₂, making 2 H₂O
  4. Clean, safe reduction: O₂ + 4e⁻ + 4H⁺ → 2H₂O

What ACTUALLY happens (~1-2% of the time):

  1. Electrons LEAK from Complex I and Complex III
  2. These leaked electrons hit O₂ BEFORE it reaches Complex IV
  3. O₂ + 1e⁻ → O₂•⁻ (superoxide)
  4. This is a premature, partial reduction of oxygen

Why does leaking happen?

  • At Complex III, ubiquinone (CoQ) forms an unstable radical intermediate called semiquinone (CoQ•⁻)
  • Semiquinone has an unpaired electron
  • This unpaired electron can react with O₂ that's dissolved in the membrane
  • Result: superoxide formation

Part 2: What ROS Do to Cells (Damage)

Three Main Targets

1. Lipid Peroxidation (Membrane Damage)

  • ROS steal electrons from lipids in cell membranes
  • This creates a chain reaction → lipid peroxidation
  • Result: Holes in membranes, loss of membrane integrity
  • Especially bad in mitochondria where ROS are made

2. Protein Oxidation

  • ROS oxidize amino acid side chains (especially cysteine, methionine)
  • Proteins unfold and lose function
  • Enzymes stop working
  • Result: Cellular dysfunction

3. DNA Damage

  • ROS oxidize DNA bases (especially guanine)
  • Creates 8-oxoguanine and other lesions
  • Result: Mutations, DNA breaks
  • Can trigger apoptosis if damage is severe

Cellular Defense Systems

Enzyme Reaction What it does
Superoxide dismutase (SOD) 2 O₂•⁻ + 2H⁺ → H₂O₂ + O₂ Converts superoxide to hydrogen peroxide
Catalase 2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂ Converts hydrogen peroxide to water
Glutathione peroxidase H₂O₂ + 2 GSH → 2 H₂O + GSSG Uses glutathione to neutralize H₂O₂

The defense strategy:

  1. SOD catches superoxide immediately → converts to H₂O₂
  2. Catalase converts H₂O₂ → harmless H₂O
  3. If this system is overwhelmed → oxidative stress → damage accumulates

Part 3: Evolutionary Story

The Great Oxygenation Event

~3.5 billion years ago: Anaerobic world

  • Earth's atmosphere had almost no oxygen
  • All life was prokaryotic (bacteria)
  • Energy: fermentation only (2 ATP per glucose)
  • Inefficient, but safe from oxygen toxicity

~2.4 billion years ago: Cyanobacteria evolve photosynthesis

  • Cyanobacteria start producing O₂ as a waste product
  • Oxygen accumulates in oceans, then atmosphere
  • This was TOXIC to most existing life (Great Oxygenation Event)
  • Massive extinction of anaerobic organisms

~2 billion years ago: Aerobic respiration evolves

  • Some bacteria evolve to USE oxygen (electron transport chain)
  • Aerobic respiration: ~30-32 ATP per glucose (15x more efficient!)
  • But comes with a cost: ROS production
  • These bacteria also evolve defenses (SOD, catalase)

~1.5 billion years ago: Endosymbiosis

  • A primitive eukaryotic cell engulfs an aerobic bacterium
  • Instead of digesting it, they form a symbiosis
  • The bacterium becomes the mitochondrion
  • Eukaryote gets: massive energy boost
  • Eukaryote inherits: the ROS problem

Eukaryotes vs Prokaryotes & ROS

Where Does Respiration Happen?

Cell Type Location of ETC ROS Production
Eukaryotes Inner mitochondrial membrane Yes - inside mitochondria
Aerobic prokaryotes Cell (plasma) membrane Yes - at the membrane
Anaerobic prokaryotes No ETC (fermentation only) Minimal/none

Key points:

  • Eukaryotes have mitochondria with their own membranes → ROS trapped inside organelle
  • Aerobic prokaryotes do ETC on their cell membrane → ROS at periphery
  • Both make ROS, just in different locations
  • Anaerobic prokaryotes avoid the problem by not using oxygen at all

🔑 The Big Picture

Why ROS exist:

  • Inevitable byproduct of using oxygen for energy
  • The ETC isn't perfect - electrons leak
  • About 1-2% of oxygen becomes superoxide instead of water

The evolutionary trade-off:

  • Benefit: 15x more ATP (aerobic vs anaerobic)
  • Cost: Constant ROS damage, need for defense systems
  • Natural selection favored efficiency despite the damage

Why eukaryotes have mitochondria:

  • Ancient endosymbiosis event (~1.5 billion years ago)
  • Aerobic bacterium became mitochondrion
  • Gave eukaryotes massive energy advantage
  • But also inherited the ROS production machinery

Connection to prokaryotes:

  • Aerobic prokaryotes make ROS too (on their cell membrane)
  • Anaerobic prokaryotes avoid oxygen entirely
  • Mitochondria ARE prokaryotes (endosymbiont theory)

Start here

Charge q

What it is: positive or negative electric property.

  • Protons / cations: positive
  • Electrons / anions: negative
  • Opposites attract; likes repel
Chem translation

Ionic bonds, salt formation, electron transfer, electrostatic potential maps, and redox all begin with charge separation.

Push / pull

Electrostatic Force F

F = k q₁q₂ / r²

Meaning: the attraction or repulsion between two charges.

  • Bigger charges → stronger force
  • Greater distance → weaker force
When to use in chem

Use for ionic bond strength, lattice energy logic, ion-ion attraction, and why higher charge/smaller distance usually strengthens electrostatic interactions.

Chem example

Ionic character vs lattice strength

Ionic character asks: how unequal is electron sharing?

ionic character ↑ as ΔEN ↑

Lattice strength asks: how strongly do the ions attract?

force ↑ as |q₁q₂| ↑ and r ↓

Do not use formal charge first when the question asks “percent ionic character.” Use electronegativity difference.

Influence zone

Electric Field E

E = F/q

E = kQ/r²

Meaning: the region around a source charge where another charge would feel force.

  • Field points away from + charges
  • Field points toward − charges
Chem translation

A polar bond or ion creates an electric influence around it. That is why partial charges can affect neighboring molecules, enzymes, water, and ion channels.

Energy per charge

Electric Potential V

V = U/q

V = kQ/r

Meaning: electric potential energy per unit charge at one location.

Potential is like asking: “How much electric energy would each coulomb have here?”

Potential vs field

Field is force-related. Potential is energy-related. Field tells direction of push; potential tells energy per charge.

Driving force

Voltage / Electric Potential Difference ΔV

ΔV = ΔU/q

Meaning: energy difference per charge between two points.

Unit: volt = joule/coulomb.

Chem translation

Voltage is like a gradient. It does not automatically mean charges are moving; it means there is a drive that can make charges move if a path exists.

Electrochem bridge

Cell potential is voltage

A redox reaction separates electron source and electron sink.

Anodeoxidation; electrons produced
Cathodereduction; electrons consumed
Electron flowanode → cathode
Cell potentialvoltage driving electron flow

Mnemonic: An Ox, Red Cat = Anode Oxidation, Reduction Cathode.

Moving charges

Current I

I = Q/t

Meaning: charge flow per time.

  • Conventional current: + to −
  • Electron flow: − to +
Chem translation

In a wire, electrons move. In solution, ions move. In a membrane, Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, and Cl⁻ can move down electrochemical gradients through channels.

Opposition

Resistance R

R = ρL/A

Meaning: opposition to current flow.

  • Longer path → more resistance
  • Bigger area → less resistance
  • Material matters through resistivity ρ
Use with your myelin question

Bigger axon diameter lowers internal resistance. Myelin mainly insulates the membrane and prevents charge leak; it does not increase the axoplasm’s conducting area.

Circuit rule

Ohm’s Law

V = IR

Translation: voltage pushes, resistance blocks, current is the resulting flow.

ChangeResult if other variable constant
V increasesI increases
R increasesI decreases
Membrane analogy

Ion movement in cells

Ion flow depends on the electrochemical gradient:

  • chemical gradient: concentration difference
  • electrical gradient: charge/potential difference
  • channel state: open vs closed

If the channel opens and the ion moves downhill, ion movement is passive even if gating required voltage, ligand binding, or regulation.

Bond polarity

Electronegativity → partial charges → electrostatic potential

ΔEN small→shared e⁻→covalent
ΔEN large→uneven e⁻→polar / ionic

Electrostatic potential maps show charge distribution: electron-rich areas vs electron-poor areas.

Common traps from your review

Trap Map

Trap 1: Ionic character vs strengthLeast ionic character = smallest electronegativity difference. Stronger lattice = charge size + ion size.
Trap 2: Voltage vs currentVoltage is the drive. Current is the actual movement.
Trap 3: Field vs forceField exists around a source charge. Force happens when another charge is placed there.
Trap 4: Potential vs potential differencePotential is one-point energy per charge. Voltage compares two points.
Trap 5: Passive vs gated channelsVoltage-gated means voltage opens the channel. Ion movement can still be passive if downhill.
Trap 6: Myelin and resistanceMyelin prevents membrane charge leak. Larger axon diameter lowers internal resistance, but myelin is not the same as larger conducting area.

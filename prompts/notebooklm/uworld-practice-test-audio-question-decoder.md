# NotebookLM Prompt — UWorld Practice Test Audio: Question Decoder Mode

Use this prompt for UWorld MCAT practice test review audios when the goal is to understand BOTH the science and the question itself.

## Prompt

You are creating an MCAT UWorld practice review audio for a student. The student is reviewing discrete questions and passage-based questions. Do not make this a broad summary. Make it a tutor-style walkthrough that teaches the student how to decode the question, the answer choices, the passage, and any visuals.

The student often misses questions not only from content gaps, but from misunderstanding what the question is asking, misunderstanding the answer choices, misreading figures, not knowing how to interpret graphs/tables, or not understanding how to use an equation. Treat every question as a decoding exercise first and a content review second.

For every question, whether discrete or passage-based:

1. Read or restate the question stem clearly.
2. Read or restate all answer choices clearly.
3. Translate the question into simpler language: what is it actually asking?
4. Identify the question type: content recall, passage reasoning, graph/table interpretation, figure interpretation, equation use, unit conversion, proportional reasoning, experimental design, pseudo-discrete, or trap answer.
5. Explain what information matters and what information is distraction.
6. Explain what the MCAT writer expected the student to notice.
7. Explain the correct answer.
8. Explain why each wrong answer is tempting and why it is wrong.
9. Identify whether the likely mistake was a content gap, wording misunderstanding, answer-choice misunderstanding, visual misunderstanding, math/setup issue, unit issue, or reasoning issue.
10. End with the move for next time: what should the student do when they see this kind of question again?

If the question is passage-based:

- Briefly explain the relevant passage context before answering.
- Show how the question connects to the passage.
- Do not assume the student automatically knows which sentence, figure, table, or experiment matters.
- Point out where students may overread, underread, or ignore the key clue.

If a figure, graph, table, pathway, molecule, reaction scheme, or diagram appears:

- Walk through how to read it from the beginning.
- Explain axes, labels, units, variables, controls, groups, trends, and comparisons.
- Say what should immediately attract attention.
- Explain what the visual is testing.
- Explain common misreads, such as reading the graph backward, ignoring units, confusing dependent and independent variables, or treating a trend as causation.

If an equation appears:

- Say the equation slowly.
- Explain what each variable means physically.
- Explain the units.
- Explain whether the equation is being used for calculation, proportional reasoning, or concept recognition.
- Show the setup step by step.
- For scientific notation and unit conversions, slow down and narrate every step.
- Mention whether math can be avoided by reasoning.

If answer choices are conceptually similar:

- Compare them directly.
- Explain the wording difference.
- Explain what each answer would imply.
- Say why the correct answer fits the stem better than the tempting alternative.

Audio style:

- Use two tutor-like speakers.
- Make the tone calm, clear, and practical.
- Think aloud like a tutor sitting beside the student.
- Use phrases such as: “Pause here,” “This wording is dangerous,” “This is where students misread the question,” “This looks like memorization, but it is really interpretation,” and “Here is the next-time move.”
- Do not rush through math, figures, graphs, or answer choices.
- Do not only teach the topic. Teach how to survive the question.

Priority areas to slow down for:

- Graph reading
- Figure interpretation
- Unit conversions
- Scientific notation
- C/P math setup
- Proportional reasoning
- Equations
- Redox
- Thermodynamics
- Kinetics
- Gases
- Spectroscopy
- Separations and purification
- Formal charge
- Experimental design

Final output structure:

1. Brief opening: what this review will train.
2. Passage or discrete block walkthroughs in order.
3. For each question: stem, answer choices, translation, clue, visual/equation decoding if relevant, answer-choice elimination, final answer logic, mistake type, next-time move.
4. Closing pattern review: recurring traps, recurring misunderstandings, and 3-5 rules the student should carry into the next UWorld block.

Do not remove detail to make the audio shorter. If the source is long, prioritize question-by-question clarity over summary.

## Q1 — Cell cycle, G0, and cancer drugs

**Result:** Correct

- **G1:** growth, protein production, organelle production, and preparation
- **S:** DNA synthesis = DNA replication
- **G2:** additional growth, preparation, and DNA quality control
- **M:** mitosis plus cytokinesis; the cell actually divides
- **G0:** living, functional, and metabolically active, but outside active cell-cycle progression

G0 does not mean dead or completely inactive. A G0 cell can transcribe genes, synthesize RNA and proteins, maintain organelles, communicate, and perform its specialized function. It is not progressing toward S phase or M phase.

**Sticky rule:** **G0 is alive and working, but not preparing to copy its genome and divide.**

## Q2 — Huntington disease, glutamate, GABA, and action potentials

**Result:** Correct

- Action potentials are all-or-none; a stronger stimulus does not produce a larger action potential.
- Stimulus intensity is commonly encoded through action-potential frequency.
- **Glutamate** is generally excitatory in the central nervous system.
- **GABA** is generally inhibitory in the central nervous system.
- Increased glutamate activity plus decreased GABA activity makes neurons more likely to fire repeatedly.

“Neurodegenerative” means progressive neuronal damage or loss. It does not require every immediate physiological effect to be reduced activity. Abnormal excitation can occur before or contribute to neuronal dysfunction and death.

**Sticky rule:** **Glutamate is the accelerator; GABA is the brake.**

## Q3 — Acrosomal reaction, cortical reaction, and polyspermy

**Result:** Initially changed away from the correct instinct

### Fertilization sequence

1. A sperm binds the zona pellucida.
2. The **acrosomal reaction** occurs in the sperm.
3. Acrosomal enzymes help the sperm penetrate the zona pellucida.
4. The sperm and oocyte membranes fuse.
5. A calcium-dependent **cortical reaction** occurs in the oocyte.
6. Cortical granules modify the zona pellucida and block additional sperm.

### Name and structure anchors

- **Acro-** means tip or extremity.
- The **acrosome** is the enzyme-containing cap at the tip of the sperm head.
- The acrosome develops from the **Golgi apparatus** during spermiogenesis.
- **Cortex** means outer layer.
- **Cortical granules** sit beneath the oocyte plasma membrane in the outer cytoplasm.
- Both reactions involve regulated **exocytosis**.

More than one sperm would contribute extra paternal chromosome sets and centrioles, producing an abnormal genome rather than a normal diploid zygote.

**Sticky rule:** **Acrosome gets one sperm in; cortical reaction keeps the others out.**

## Q4 — ADH, aquaporins, and renal transport direction

**Result:** Correct answer; transport direction needed repair

ADH increases **aquaporin-2** insertion into the apical membrane of collecting-duct principal cells. Water then moves:

`collecting-duct lumen/filtrate → epithelial cell → interstitium → blood`

This is **reabsorption**, because material moves from the nephron tubule back toward the blood.

- **Reabsorption:** tubule/filtrate → blood
- **Secretion:** blood → tubule/filtrate
- **Excretion:** material leaves the body in urine

### ADH versus aldosterone

- **ADH:** directly increases water permeability through aquaporins; decreases urine volume and concentrates urine.
- **Aldosterone:** steroid hormone that increases sodium reabsorption and potassium secretion; water follows sodium indirectly when permeability permits.

**Sticky rule:** **ADH adds aquaporins so water leaves the urine pathway and returns to blood.**

## Q5 — Heart-rate variability and parasympathetic input

**Result:** Incorrect

The question measured the **time interval between successive heartbeats**, not a series of heart-rate values in beats per minute.

- **Heart rate:** beats/min
- **Interbeat interval:** time/beat, usually milliseconds or seconds
- **Heart-rate variability:** variation in successive interbeat intervals

Increased parasympathetic or vagal input generally produces:

- decreased mean heart rate
- longer average interbeat interval
- increased beat-to-beat variability
- increased standard deviation of the interbeat intervals

A slow rhythm is not automatically a perfectly uniform rhythm. Vagal modulation can make the timing more flexible from beat to beat.

**Sticky rule:** **Identify the measured variable before interpreting its mean or standard deviation.**

## Cardiovascular variable and unit anchors

- **Heart rate (HR):** beats/min
- **Stroke volume (SV):** mL/beat
- **Cardiac output (CO):** `HR × SV`, usually L/min
- **Mean arterial pressure (MAP):** approximately `CO × SVR`
- **Systemic vascular resistance (SVR):** resistance opposing systemic blood flow
- **Heart-rate variability (HRV):** variation in time between beats, commonly measured in ms

### Important resistance correction

`R = ρL/A` is the standard **electrical-resistance** relation. It should not be used as the cardiovascular vessel-resistance equation.

For laminar flow through a cylindrical vessel, Poiseuille’s relationship gives:

`R = 8ηL/(πr^4)`

Therefore:

- increased viscosity increases resistance
- increased vessel length increases resistance
- increased vessel radius greatly decreases resistance because of the fourth-power relationship

**Sticky rule:** **Electrical resistance uses `ρL/A`; vascular resistance follows `ηL/r^4`.**

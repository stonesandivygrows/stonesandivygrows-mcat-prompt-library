## Q1 — Just Noticeable Difference vs Absolute Threshold

### What went well

The distinction was understood correctly.

### Correct rule

**Just noticeable difference (JND)** is about detecting a **difference or change between two stimuli**.

Common cues:

- compare
- distinguish
- changed
- increased/decreased
- different from
- two stimuli

**Absolute threshold** is about detecting whether **a stimulus is present at all**.

Common cues:

- minimum intensity
- detect 50% of the time
- hear/see/smell something at all
- stimulus first becomes detectable

### Durable note

> **JND = minimum difference between two stimuli. Absolute threshold = minimum stimulus needed to detect anything.**

## Q4 — Signal Detection Theory

### Error type

**Concept precision issue.**

The issue was not that the overall topic was impossible. The gap was not knowing the exact definition of signal detection theory.

### Correct rule

**Signal detection theory** explains how people decide whether a signal is present when conditions are uncertain, weak, or noisy.

It combines:

1. **Sensory ability** — how detectable the signal actually is.
2. **Response bias/criterion** — how willing the person is to say the signal is present.

### Four outcomes

| Reality | Person says “yes” | Person says “no” |
|---|---|---|
| Signal present | Hit | Miss |
| Signal absent | False alarm | Correct rejection |

### Why the original reasoning failed

The word “decision” can sound too broad. On the MCAT, signal detection theory is not general decision-making. It is specifically a decision about whether a weak or uncertain stimulus is present.

### Durable note

> **Signal detection theory = detection under uncertainty. It tests both sensory sensitivity and response bias. If the question involves deciding whether a weak/unclear signal is present, think hit, miss, false alarm, correct rejection.**

## Q6 — Graph / Significance Interpretation

### What went well

The chart was interpreted cautiously. The reasoning was: if the plotted lines or ranges are within/overlapping range, avoid claiming a meaningful difference.

### Correct MCAT rule

If error bars, confidence intervals, or ranges overlap heavily, do **not** overclaim a significant difference unless the passage explicitly states significance.

### Important wording caution

Overlapping error bars do not mathematically prove “not significant” in every statistical context. But for MCAT reasoning, visible overlap usually means:

> **Do not claim a clear difference unless the passage/statistics explicitly support it.**

### Durable note

> **Graph rule: overlap = be conservative. Do not infer significance from visual separation unless the passage gives statistical support.**

## Q9 — Feature Detection vs Parallel Processing

### What went well

The answer was reached by eliminating choices and recognizing that seeing/identifying specific visual elements points toward feature detection.

### Correct rule

**Feature detection** involves recognizing specific visual building blocks, such as:

- lines
- edges
- angles
- movement
- shape orientation

**Parallel processing** is broader. It refers to processing multiple visual features at the same time, such as:

- color
- motion
- depth
- form

### Durable note

> **Feature detection = detecting specific visual building blocks. Parallel processing = processing multiple visual features simultaneously.**

## Why this is a supplement

This session did not establish a replacement CARS strategy. It tested a narrower evolution of the existing controlled-skipping and paragraph-job method. Because the work was largely untimed and based on a single practice session, the new elements remain provisional until they transfer to timed AAMC material.

## New working hypothesis

Use different reading intensities across a passage:

- high intensity for the first paragraph's framing;
- medium intensity for argumentative turns and contrasts;
- lower intensity for examples, descriptive material, and removable interruptions;
- high intensity again for the final paragraph's conclusion, judgment, irony, or consequence.

The goal is not simply to skim faster. The goal is to preserve the author's argumentative structure while reducing overinvestment in details.

## Body-paragraph minimum

For each body paragraph, capture enough to identify its job:

- opening claim or opening clause;
- contrast or correction words;
- names, quotations, italicized terms, or unusual vocabulary;
- final sentence or final argumentative turn;
- one middle sentence only when the paragraph's function remains unclear.

Do not use the extreme version of reading only fragments or a random middle sentence. A random sentence may not represent the paragraph's role.

Working standard:

> Read enough to know what job the paragraph is doing.

If the paragraph cannot be labeled with a useful phrase, the skim was too aggressive.

## First- and last-paragraph emphasis

Read the first paragraph carefully to identify:

- subject;
- tension;
- initial framing;
- whether the paragraph is thesis, setup, background, or hook.

Read the last paragraph carefully because it may:

- resolve the tension;
- state the author's final claim;
- add irony;
- shift from evidence to judgment;
- explain why the passage matters.

This is emphasis, not a rule that the thesis must appear in the first or last paragraph. Middle-paragraph reversals still control when present.

## Highlighting update

Highlighting is allowed only as a retrieval and paragraph-separation system.

Prioritize:

- names;
- quotations;
- italicized terms;
- unusual vocabulary likely to support meaning-in-context questions;
- major contrasts;
- one short paragraph anchor when needed.

Do not highlight full evidence chains or every sentence that feels important. Too many marks erase the visual hierarchy and may cost more time than they save.

## Vocabulary and retrieval protocol

When a question names a paragraph, quotation, italicized term, or specific word:

1. Treat it as a narrow retrieval task.
2. Use the paragraph map, highlight, nearby name, or quotation as the locator.
3. Read the target sentence and local context.
4. Predict the meaning or function before comparing choices.
5. Use answer choices to resolve ambiguity, not to manufacture context.

Do not repeatedly scan the entire passage for one word.

## Answer-choice preview boundary

The existing limited answer-choice preview remains optional.

Use it only when an early question is clearly main idea, primary purpose, or central point and the choices help identify repeated thesis lanes.

Do not:

- preview every question;
- read all stems before the passage;
- stack answer-choice preview with automatic first-sentence, last-sentence, and full-section scanning rituals.

## What the session actually showed

Reported timing was approximately:

- 15 minutes for the first passage;
- 10–12 minutes for a later passage;
- 12–15 minutes for another passage.

These times are acceptable for learning an unfamiliar method but are not evidence of test-ready speed.

The session supports continued testing of:

- selective reading intensity;
- first/last-paragraph emphasis;
- paragraph-function mapping;
- targeted highlighting for later retrieval.

It does not yet prove that the method is faster or more accurate than the canonical strategy.

## Important correction about UWorld

The belief that UWorld CARS is harder than AAMC CARS is a training assumption, not a conclusion established here.

Use UWorld as challenging practice if useful, but use AAMC performance as the primary standard for whether the strategy transfers to the actual exam style.

## Next controlled trial

For the next passage set:

1. Use the selective-intensity method.
2. Record the approximate time when passage reading ends.
3. Limit highlighting to retrieval anchors and major contrasts.
4. For every miss, classify the source:
   - passage structure missed;
   - body detail skimmed too aggressively;
   - question type or scope misread;
   - answer-choice logic error;
   - slow or unsuccessful retrieval.
5. Compare with at least one AAMC passage set before promoting the method to permanent status.

## Promotion criteria

Keep or promote an element when:

- accuracy remains stable or improves;
- passage time decreases without increasing rereading;
- main-idea and inference performance remain stable;
- highlighting reliably shortens targeted retrieval.

Modify or reject an element when:

- paragraph labels remain vague;
- middle-paragraph reversals are repeatedly missed;
- answer choices are being used to reconstruct a passage that was not understood;
- highlighting becomes dense;
- speed improves while accuracy falls;
- the method works on UWorld but fails on AAMC.

## Current conclusion

The strongest new idea is selective reading intensity.

The weakest version is near-total non-reading of body paragraphs.

The current safe formulation is:

> Detailed framing, functional body mapping, detailed conclusion, and narrow retrieval.

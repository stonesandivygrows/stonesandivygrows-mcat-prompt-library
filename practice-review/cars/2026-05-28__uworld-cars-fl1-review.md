## Passage 1 — Göbekli Tepe

### Passage map

The passage presented a causal reversal:

> Traditional view: agriculture and food surplus -> settled civilization -> religion and large communal projects.
>
> Revised view suggested by Göbekli Tepe: shared religion and communal activity -> need to organize and feed workers -> controlled plant use/agriculture -> sedentary civilization.

The main idea was successfully recognized as a dispute about which came first: agriculture/civilization or religion/communal practice.

### Question 1 — Central point

Result: correct.

The correct reasoning distinguished the passage's true purpose from a vivid but narrower detail. The site's impressive engineering and builder resourcefulness were relevant, but the passage mainly used the site to revise the assumed relationship between religion, agriculture, and civilization.

Durable correction:

> A statement may be true and memorable without being the author's central purpose.

### Question 2 — Least supported by evidence or explanation

Result: incorrect.

#### Error

The question was treated as simple retrieval: find which claim lacked direct evidence. The construction theory involving logs, boards, and pulleys seemed unsupported because the passage did not provide physical proof.

#### Correct distinction

The question asked about **evidence or explanation**. Support may include a comparison or analogical reason, not only direct proof.

- The construction theory received an analogical explanation: a similar method is thought to have been used for later pyramid construction.
- The claim that the site appeared to be an unremarkable graveyard was merely reported. The passage did not explain what made it look like a graveyard or why the original researchers reached that conclusion.

Durable rule:

> For “least supported by evidence or explanation,” rank all forms of support: evidence, reason, context, analogy, and comparison. A weak analogy is still more support than a bare assertion.

### Question 3 — New sites that would reduce significance

Result: incorrect.

#### Error

The stem's referent was lost. The question was initially processed as asking what new fact about Göbekli Tepe itself would reduce its significance. It actually asked what fact about newly discovered similar sites would reduce Göbekli Tepe's significance.

#### Correct reasoning

Göbekli Tepe's special status depended heavily on being the oldest known religious site and on pushing religion/communal action farther back in time than scholars expected. A similar but earlier site would take over that special status.

Evidence of crop cultivation at another site would be thematically relevant but would not necessarily change the causal order unless timing and sequence were specified.

Durable rules:

> “Suppose another/additional/new…” requires explicit tracking of the original object versus the new object.

> To reduce significance, identify what makes the original example significant, then choose the new fact that defeats it on that exact dimension.

### Question 4 — Least important to the passage argument

Result: incorrect.

#### Error

The stem was not fully distinguished from strengthen, weaken, truth, or support questions. All answer choices could be true passage claims; the task was to rank their role in the argument.

#### Correct reasoning

The site's initial dismissal as an unremarkable graveyard explains the history of discovery but does not establish the causal reversal about religion and civilization. The argument would work the same way if the site had been recognized earlier.

By contrast, the fact that hunter-gatherers built the site is central. Without pre-civilized builders, the evidence would no longer challenge the idea that settlement and agriculture came before organized religion.

Durable rule:

> For “least important to the argument,” ask: Could the thesis still work without this claim?

Background can be true and interesting while doing little argumentative work.

### Question 6 — Not in accord with the passage argument

Result: incorrect in the recorded answer, although the retrospective reasoning correctly identified the incompatible statement.

#### Correct evaluation

- Need to feed a large workforce -> cultivation and sedentary living: in accord.
- Shared sacred beliefs -> collective construction of ritual sites: in accord.
- Agricultural surplus -> religion and communal activity: not in accord because it restates the traditional causal order rejected by the passage.

The selected answer may have resulted from a misclick, grouping error, or loss of the negative direction in a Roman numeral question.

Durable rule:

```text
I   ✓
II  ✓
III ✗
```

Then reread the direction word and match the answer grouping. Do not perform Roman numeral grouping only in working memory.

## Question-answering operating system

Before evaluating choices:

1. Read the entire stem slowly or subvocally.
2. Complete: “This question is asking me to judge ______.”
3. Identify the task/type.
4. Identify the scope: passage, paragraph, author, argument, phrase, or new scenario.
5. Identify direction: most, least, except, not, strengthen, weaken, support, or challenge.
6. Choose the answer that answers that exact task.

Compact rule:

> Type + scope + direction = real question.

## Passage 2 early observation

The next passage reinforced the importance of scope words. A question about what the **passage argument** concerning a paragraph example assumes should not automatically be restricted to that paragraph alone. “Assumption” identifies the reasoning task, but “passage argument” identifies the scope.

Durable rule:

> Question type alone is incomplete. Always combine it with the named scope.

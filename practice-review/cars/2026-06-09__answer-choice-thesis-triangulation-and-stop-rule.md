# CARS Answer-Choice Thesis Triangulation and Practice Stop Rule

## Why this file exists

Recent AAMC and Jack Westin practice produced a useful passage-entry strategy that is distinct from full question previewing, first-sentence scanning, or full passage mapping.

The strategy uses a brief scan of **answer-choice patterns** to identify the passage's likely testing center without reading full question stems or letting the choices determine the answer.

This is especially useful when test stress causes the passage direction to feel blank at the start.

## Core discovery

> Answer choices can reveal the passage's battlefield, but the passage decides which side wins.

The goal is not to solve the question before reading. The goal is to notice what subjects, actions, objects, and relationships keep appearing so the passage has an initial anchor.

## Exact protocol

### 1. Do not preview every question

Do not read all question stems. This scatters attention and creates unnecessary cognitive load.

### 2. Scan answer choices only

Briefly scan answer-choice sets until one looks **thesis-shaped**: the choices present competing broad views of the passage rather than narrow details.

A thesis-shaped set often contains repeated:

- subjects;
- verbs or actions;
- objects or outcomes;
- relationships;
- judgments about what the author supports, opposes, respects, criticizes, or explains.

### 3. Extract the SVO pattern

Use:

```text
S = Who or what keeps appearing?
V = What action or relationship keeps appearing?
O = What target, effect, or result keeps appearing?
```

Then form a rough anchor:

```text
This passage may be about how S does V to O.
```

The anchor does not need to be fully correct. It only needs to establish the likely arena of the passage.

### 4. Read one anchor sentence only if needed

Pair the answer-choice scan with at most one passage anchor sentence, such as:

- a pivot or claim in paragraph 2;
- the last sentence of the second-to-last paragraph;
- the first or last sentence of the conclusion.

Do not automatically read all of these locations. One useful sentence is enough.

### 5. Read the passage normally

Use the preview only as a direction cue. The passage remains the final authority.

## Guardrails

- Do not choose the answer with the most familiar passage words.
- Do not assume repeated nouns reveal the correct relationship.
- Do not read every full question stem before the passage.
- Do not keep searching for a perfect main-idea question.
- Stop the scan if no thesis-shaped set appears quickly.
- Do not stack this strategy with full-section scanning, automatic first-sentence scanning, automatic last-sentence scanning, and a written map.

Working reminder:

> Choices reveal the testing center, not the verdict.

## Time limit

The preview should be brief.

```text
Useful thesis-shaped set found quickly -> use it.
Nothing obvious after about 10-15 seconds -> stop and read.
```

The method should reduce uncertainty, not become another ritual.

## Question-type clarification

A question can require the passage's broad argument without being a pure main-idea question.

Example:

```text
The author's arguments about X imply that the author would oppose which option?
```

This is best labeled **global implication based on the author's argument**, not simply main idea.

The answer choices may still be useful for thesis triangulation because they reveal competing broad views.

## Thesis marination strategy

When the passage is partly understood but the thesis has not fully clicked:

1. Do not force the global question immediately.
2. Answer concrete questions first, such as detail, phrase, localized implication, or function questions.
3. Let those questions reactivate passage locations and relationships.
4. Return to the main-idea or global implication question after the passage has had time to settle.

This is not using other questions as answer clues. It is using retrieval and local reasoning to strengthen the internal passage model.

Working reminder:

> Local questions can help the thesis marinate.

## Interaction with syntax strategy

This method complements controlled skipping:

- answer choices provide an initial topic/relationship anchor;
- the passage read identifies the author's actual claim;
- interruptive syntax can be postponed when it does not alter the main clause;
- contrast, correction, causal, and judgment language must still be read;
- skipped details can be revisited only when a question gives a precise retrieval target.

## Evidence from recent practice

### AAMC practice

- Reading time: approximately **3:48**.
- Result: **5/6**.
- Observation: when the thesis did not click immediately, answering non-thesis questions gave the passage time to settle.
- Interface caveat: moving backward restarted the displayed question timer, so individual question times were not reliable.

### Jack Westin practice

- Result: **100%** on the passage set where the answer-choice scan was used.
- The preview involved answer choices only, not full question stems.
- Repeated terms provided a useful early expectation about the passage's treatment of ego, wickedness, respect, and villainous characters.
- The initial prediction was not the exact thesis, but it correctly identified the passage's argumentative arena.

## Practice stop rule

A separate recurring problem is taking additional CARS passages to prove a result.

The cycle can occur after either outcome:

```text
Poor passage -> take another to prove ability.
Strong passage -> take another to prove it was not a fluke.
```

Both reactions create a CARS hamster wheel and make practice emotionally reactive rather than diagnostic.

### Non-negotiable rule

> Stop after the planned CARS dose, even when the urge to prove the result is strong.

After the planned passages:

1. Record the score.
2. Record the strategy used.
3. Record one thing that worked and one thing to retest.
4. Switch subjects.

Do not use an extra passage to erase a bad result or validate a good result.

## Current status

### Keep

- scanning answer choices without reading full stems when a thesis-shaped set appears;
- extracting repeated subject-verb-object relationships;
- using one anchor sentence at most;
- allowing local questions to help a fuzzy thesis settle;
- stopping after the planned passage count.

### Continue testing

- whether the answer-choice scan works equally well on different AAMC passage styles;
- whether it remains useful when the first available answer-choice set is not global;
- whether 10-15 seconds is the best stopping limit;
- whether the best anchor sentence is usually paragraph 2 or a late conclusion sentence.

### Drop or avoid

- reading every question before the passage;
- hunting indefinitely for a main-idea question;
- stacking multiple prereading systems;
- treating the preview as evidence for a specific answer;
- taking extra passages for reassurance or self-proof.

## Compact test-day version

```text
1. Scan choices, not stems.
2. Find a thesis-shaped set.
3. Pull S -> V -> O.
4. Optional: read one anchor sentence.
5. Read passage; passage decides the thesis.
6. If thesis stays fuzzy, do local questions first.
7. Stop after the planned passage dose.
```

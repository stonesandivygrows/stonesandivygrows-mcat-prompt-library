# MCAT Physics — Waves & Sound Full Concept Map

Use this as the canonical text-format map for the Waves & Sound branch. Structure it around MCAT question triggers: what kind of wave is it, what variable changed, what relationship applies, and what trap is being tested.

## 1. Wave Concept

- Wave = transfers energy through space or matter.
- Waves do not require net mass transport.
- Mechanical waves require a medium.
- Sound is a mechanical longitudinal wave.
- Sound cannot travel through vacuum.
- Supernova in space can be visually bright but silent because light can travel through vacuum, while sound cannot.

## 2. Wave Type

### Transverse waves

- Particles oscillate perpendicular to wave travel.
- Examples: rope waves, water ripples, electromagnetic waves.
- Transverse waves can be polarized.

### Longitudinal waves

- Particles oscillate parallel to wave travel.
- Compression = region of high pressure and density.
- Rarefaction = region of low pressure and density.
- Sound is the main MCAT longitudinal-wave example.

## 3. Wave Variables

- Equilibrium position = resting midpoint.
- Displacement = distance from equilibrium.
- Amplitude A = displacement from equilibrium to peak.
- Do not measure amplitude from peak to trough.
- Crest = maximum positive displacement.
- Trough = maximum negative displacement.
- Wavelength λ = distance per cycle.
- Frequency f = cycles per second.
- Units of frequency = Hz = s⁻¹.
- Period T = time per cycle.
- T = 1/f.
- v = fλ.

## 4. Wave Speed

- Wave speed depends on the medium, not on how the wave is produced.
- For a fixed medium, wave speed is constant.
- If v is constant, increasing frequency decreases wavelength.
- If v is constant, decreasing frequency increases wavelength.
- Sound travels fastest in solids.
- Sound travels slower in liquids.
- Sound travels slowest in gases.
- Sound travels faster in warmer air.
- When sound changes medium, frequency stays the same, speed changes, and wavelength changes.

## 5. Intensity & Loudness

- Intensity = power per area.
- I = P/A.
- Units of intensity = W/m².
- Intensity is the physical strength of the sound wave.
- Intensity is proportional to amplitude squared.
- Doubling amplitude gives four times the intensity.
- Intensity decreases with distance because sound energy spreads over larger area.
- I ∝ 1/r².
- Double distance → one-fourth intensity.
- Quadruple distance → one-sixteenth intensity.
- Attenuation = energy loss from damping/friction/absorption.
- Loudness = perceived intensity.
- Sound level β = 10 log(I/I₀).
- Units of sound level = decibels.
- dB is not intensity; dB is a logarithmic sound-level scale based on intensity.

## 6. Pitch & Doppler Effect

- Pitch = perceived frequency.
- Doppler effect = observed frequency changes due to relative motion.
- Relative motion toward each other compresses wavefronts.
- Approaching source raises observed frequency and perceived pitch.
- Relative motion away from each other stretches wavefronts.
- Receding source lowers observed frequency and perceived pitch.
- If source and observer move in the same direction, apparent frequency depends on relative speeds.
- Doppler does not change the speed of sound in the medium.
- Doppler does not depend on distance alone; it depends on relative motion.
- Top sign = moving toward.
- Bottom sign = moving away.
- Zero shift means no relative approach or separation.

## 7. Shock Waves

- Shock wave = special Doppler-related case.
- Source moves at or above the speed of sound.
- Wavefronts pile up.
- Large pressure gradient forms.
- Sonic boom occurs when accumulated wavefronts reach the observer.

## 8. Superposition & Interference

- Superposition = overlapping waves add their displacements.
- In phase waves produce constructive interference.
- Constructive interference strengthens the wave.
- Crest plus crest increases amplitude.
- 180° out-of-phase waves produce destructive interference.
- Crest plus trough can yield zero displacement.
- Interference changes displacement pattern, not the identity of the waves themselves.

## 9. Resonance & Damping

- Resonance = driving force matches natural frequency.
- Resonance increases amplitude.
- Example: glass shattering when driven at its natural frequency.
- Damping = amplitude decreases due to nonconservative forces.
- Damping lowers amplitude and intensity.
- Damping does not change the fundamental frequency.

## 10. Standing Waves

- Standing waves are a special case of interference.
- Standing waves form when two waves of the same frequency travel in opposite directions and interfere.
- Nodes = no displacement.
- Antinodes = maximum displacement.
- Standing waves form only at harmonic frequencies.
- Fundamental frequency = lowest resonant frequency.
- Fundamental wavelength = longest resonant wavelength.

### String fixed at both ends

- Ends are nodes.
- λn = 2L/n.
- fn = nv/2L.
- n = number of antinodes.
- Count antinodes for string harmonics.

### Open pipe

- Open end = antinode.
- λn = 2L/n.
- Supports all harmonics.
- Count nodes for open-pipe harmonics if using the node-count shortcut from your map.

### Closed pipe

- Closed end = node.
- Open end = antinode.
- λn = 4L/n.
- Only odd harmonics.
- Do not use 2L/n for closed pipes.
- Closed pipes use quarter-wavelength logic.

## 11. Ultrasound / Medical Applications

- Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound.
- Ultrasound imaging relies on reflection at tissue boundaries.
- Doppler ultrasound detects blood flow by frequency shifts from moving blood cells.
- Therapeutic ultrasound can heat tissue.
- Focused ultrasound can break kidney stones.
- Focused ultrasound can ablate tumors.
- Ultrasound can be used for dental cleaning.
- Ultrasound can be used in cataract destruction / phacoemulsification.

## 12. Major Traps

- Sound is longitudinal, not transverse.
- Sound needs a medium.
- Electromagnetic waves do not need a medium.
- dB is not intensity; dB is sound level.
- Intensity is measured in W/m².
- Pitch tracks frequency, not amplitude.
- Loudness tracks intensity/amplitude.
- Distance affects intensity, not pitch directly.
- Doppler affects observed frequency/pitch.
- Damping lowers amplitude, not fundamental frequency.
- Closed pipes use quarter-wavelength logic.
- Do not use 2L/n for closed pipes.
- Measure amplitude from midline, not peak-to-trough.

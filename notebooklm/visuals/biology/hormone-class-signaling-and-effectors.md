# Hormone Classes, Signaling Pathways, and Effectors Prompt

## Purpose
Create an MCAT visual that connects hormone chemical class, receptor location, signaling speed, second messengers, effectors, tropic action, and downstream transcriptional effects.

## Source hierarchy
Use the student's Anki deck as the structural basis. Verify and fill gaps with Kaplan Biology Chapter 5, Kaplan Biochemistry Chapter 3, UWorld Biology/Biochemistry, and Khan Academy. Keep details at MCAT scope.

## Output requirements
- Organize by hormone class first, then compare mechanisms.
- Separate immediate use of existing proteins from slower gene-expression changes.
- Distinguish tropic/direct function from peptide/steroid chemical class.
- Include a full hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal example.

## Core definition
Effector = the final molecule, protein, channel, transporter, enzyme, transcription factor, or cellular machine that directly produces the physiological response.

## Fast versus slow responses

### Fast signaling
- Uses proteins that already exist
- May move transporters or channels to a membrane
- May open/close channels
- May phosphorylate or dephosphorylate enzymes
- Usually begins at a cell-surface receptor

### Slow signaling
- Changes transcription
- Produces more or less of a protein
- Requires transcription and often translation
- Usually associated with intracellular receptor signaling

## Peptide hormones
- Hydrophilic/polar
- Cannot freely cross the lipid bilayer
- Bind cell-surface receptors
- Use second messengers and signaling cascades
- Usually fast onset and shorter duration

### Required examples
- Insulin
- Glucagon
- ADH
- Oxytocin
- FSH
- LH
- ACTH
- TSH
- GH
- Prolactin
- PTH

### Mechanism examples
- Insulin → receptor signaling → preformed GLUT4 vesicles move to membrane → glucose uptake increases
- ADH → receptor signaling → preformed aquaporins inserted into collecting duct membrane → water reabsorption increases
- Glucagon/epinephrine → phosphorylation cascade → metabolic enzyme activity changes

## Steroid hormones
- Derived from cholesterol
- Lipid-soluble
- Cross plasma membrane
- Bind cytosolic or nuclear receptors
- Hormone-receptor complex acts as a transcription regulator
- Slower onset, longer-lasting effects

### Required examples
- Cortisol
- Aldosterone
- Estrogen
- Progesterone
- Testosterone

### Required explanation
Estrogen should be described as changing gene transcription in target tissues involved in reproductive function and secondary sex characteristics. Avoid reducing this to a single “body-shape gene.”

## Amino acid-derived hormones

### Catecholamines
- Derived from tyrosine
- Dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine
- Polar
- Act like peptide hormones through cell-surface receptors

### Thyroid hormones
- T3 and T4
- Derived from tyrosine but lipid-soluble enough to act like steroid hormones
- Intracellular receptors
- Change transcription

## Tropic versus direct hormones

### Tropic hormone
Acts on another endocrine gland and causes release of another hormone.

Required examples:
- GnRH → anterior pituitary
- FSH/LH → gonads
- TSH → thyroid
- ACTH → adrenal cortex

### Direct/non-tropic hormone
Acts mainly on a non-endocrine target tissue.

Required examples:
- ADH → collecting duct
- Oxytocin → uterus/mammary myoepithelial cells
- Prolactin → mammary gland

### Classification warning
Chemical class and functional class are independent:
- FSH = peptide + tropic
- LH = peptide + tropic
- ACTH = peptide + tropic
- Estrogen = steroid + mainly downstream/end hormone

## Integrated reproductive axis
Show the domino sequence:
- Hypothalamus releases GnRH
- Anterior pituitary releases FSH and LH
- FSH/LH rapidly signal through membrane receptors on gonadal cells
- Gonads produce steroid hormones
- Estrogen, progesterone, or testosterone enter target cells
- Intracellular receptor complexes change transcription
- Downstream effects are slower and longer-lasting

Required principle:
A fast peptide tropic signal can initiate production of a slower steroid hormone downstream.

## Common traps
- Peptide hormones are generally fast, not slow.
- Requiring a receptor does not make a hormone slow; all hormones require receptors.
- ADH is not ADHD.
- Epinephrine is amino acid-derived but acts through membrane receptors.
- Thyroid hormone is amino acid-derived but acts through intracellular receptors.
- Acute ADH and insulin effects primarily relocate existing membrane proteins rather than waiting to synthesize new ones.
- “Effector” is not “vector.”

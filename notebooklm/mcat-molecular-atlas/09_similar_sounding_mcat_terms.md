# NotebookLM Prompt — Similar-Sounding MCAT Terms

```text
You are creating an MCAT tutor-style audio overview for Neissa.

Topic family:
Similar-sounding or similar-looking MCAT terms across C/P, B/B, and Behavioral Science. Do not include CARS.

Goal:
Prevent word-recognition errors. Neissa needs to distinguish terms that sound alike, look alike, or appear in the same topic area but mean different things.

Example confusing sets:
- lysosome vs lysozyme
- pepsin vs peptidase vs peptide vs protease
- anomia vs anomie
- phagocyte vs phagosome vs phagocytosis
- endosome vs episome vs exosome
- ribosome vs ribozyme
- glucagon vs glycogen vs glucose
- kinase vs phosphatase
- oxidation vs reduction
- aldose vs aldehyde vs aldosterone if relevant
- amine vs amide vs amino acid
- ester vs ether
- acetyl vs acyl vs acetate
- antigen vs antibody
- epitope vs epithelia if relevant
- operon vs operator vs promoter if relevant

Use Kaplan and UWorld materials as the factual foundation. If a term is not supported by the uploaded sources, say so briefly and focus on supported high-yield terms.

Teaching style:
Speak like two calm MCAT tutors. Be slow, clear, and concrete. Pronounce each term clearly. Make the differences obvious. Do not give vague definitions.

For every confusing pair or group, use this exact structure:

1. Terms
Say the terms slowly and separately.

2. One-sentence definition of each
Define each term in plain English.

3. Why they are easy to confuse
Explain whether they sound similar, share a prefix, appear in the same topic, or have overlapping biology/chemistry context.

4. The clean distinction
Give the shortest useful difference. Example: “A lysosome is an organelle; lysozyme is an enzyme.”

5. MCAT context
Explain where each term appears: passage, answer choice, experimental method, pathway, graph, psych/soc question, or biology concept.

6. Trap sentence
Give one MCAT-style trap sentence that could confuse Neissa.

7. Fix
Give one memory hook or recognition rule.

8. Do not confuse warning
End with: “Do not confuse X with Y because…”

Required final sections:
1. Top 20 confusing term pairs or groups from this audio
2. Fastest way to tell each pair apart
3. Terms that are molecules vs organelles vs enzymes vs processes vs social science concepts
4. A 10-line cheat sheet Neissa can memorize

Important:
This is a term-discrimination podcast, not a full content lecture. Keep each pair sharp, practical, and test-relevant.
```
